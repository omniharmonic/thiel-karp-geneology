# Allan Bloom

**(1930–1992)**

American political philosopher. Student of [Leo Strauss](leo-strauss.md) at Chicago; one of the two most influential American Straussians (with Harvey Mansfield). Translator of Plato's *Republic* and Rousseau's *Emile*. Author of *The Closing of the American Mind* (1987), a Straussian diagnosis of the American university that became an unlikely bestseller and shaped the undergraduate intellectual culture of [Thiel](peter-thiel.md)'s Stanford years.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Indianapolis. Entered the University of Chicago at 15 as part of the Hutchins College program. Studied under Strauss and David Grene. Taught at Chicago, Yale, and Cornell before returning to Chicago for the rest of his career, where he was a founding figure of the Committee on Social Thought.

Bloom's intellectual persona: a classical Straussian who read deeply in Plato and Rousseau and Shakespeare, who loved music and elite culture, who was both intellectually aristocratic and warmly devoted to his students. Saul Bellow's novel *Ravelstein* (2000) is a portrait of Bloom.

---

## Core contributions

### *The Closing of the American Mind* (1987)

The book that put Straussianism on American bestseller lists. Bloom's argument: American higher education has abandoned the Great Books tradition, opened itself to cultural relativism, and thereby left its students without the intellectual resources to ask serious questions about how to live. The book is a diagnosis of the American university's "crisis" — read through Nietzsche, Rousseau, and the Straussian three-waves framework.

Bloom's Nietzsche is especially important for the American Right's reception: Nietzsche as the diagnostician of egalitarian democratic shallowness, the thinker who forces Americans to confront what they have in fact abandoned by their commitment to value-neutrality. This is the Nietzsche Thiel encountered as an undergraduate.

### Translations

Bloom's *Republic* (1968) became the standard translation for Straussian-influenced teaching. His *Emile* (1979) and his interpretive essay treated Rousseau as a central figure of the second wave of modernity — the attempt to recover freedom within the modern frame.

### Teaching

Bloom was a legendary teacher — the kind whose undergraduate classes produced students who went on to carry the Straussian project forward. His students include Francis Fukuyama, Paul Wolfowitz, Alan Keyes, and many others who shaped American conservative intellectual life.

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel

Bloom is the transmission vector by which Strauss reached the American undergraduate mainstream, and the figure who most shaped the intellectual milieu of Thiel's Stanford. *The Closing of the American Mind* was a foundational text for the cohort that founded *The Stanford Review* in 1987 — they were, in a sense, the students Bloom was writing for. Thiel's critique of the contemporary academy still has a distinctly Bloom-shaped mouth.

### To Karp

More distant, but structurally important: Karp's undergraduate education was at Haverford, not Chicago, and his German years were spent inside the Habermasian tradition Bloom would have regarded with suspicion. Bloom's Nietzsche and Karp's Nietzsche are very different Nietzsches; their readings of the American university are diametrically opposed.
