# Milton Friedman

**(1912–2006)**

American economist. Nobel laureate (1976). The dominant figure of the Chicago School of economics and the most effective 20th-century popularizer of free-market policy. His work on monetarism, on the permanent income hypothesis, and on the natural rate of unemployment shaped the technical center of 20th-century macroeconomics; his popular work (*Capitalism and Freedom*, 1962; *Free to Choose*, 1980 TV series) shaped American and British policy.

## Core claims

- **Milton's test:** "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." Monetary policy, not fiscal intervention, is the decisive macroeconomic lever.
- **Positive economics:** The job of economics is predictive success; "realism" of assumptions doesn't matter if the model predicts.
- **Shareholder theory:** Corporations exist to maximize shareholder value; broader "social responsibility" is a category error.
- **Policy program:** floating exchange rates, school vouchers, volunteer military, negative income tax, drug legalization, monetary rule-based policy.

## Influences

## Influenced

- The Chicago School generally

- [Thiel](peter-thiel.md) — the Stanford undergraduate libertarian substrate

## Relevance to Thiel

Friedman is part of the free-market substrate of young Thiel's politics. *The Stanford Review* in the late 1980s was operating within the intellectual world Friedman had built. Thiel has since moved in more conservative-theological and nationalist-industrial-policy directions, but the Friedmanite commitment to markets-as-coordination remains in his thinking about startups and monopolies.
