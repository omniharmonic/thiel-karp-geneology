# Leo Strauss

**(1899–1973)**

German-born American political philosopher. Founded a distinctive and influential school of political thought — [Straussianism](../schools/straussianism.md) — centered on the claim that modernity represents a decisive break with classical and medieval political philosophy, that this break is catastrophic, and that philosophical writing prior to the Enlightenment concealed its most important teachings behind exoteric surfaces intended for popular consumption ([esoteric writing](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md)).

Strauss's political-philosophical diagnosis — that modern liberalism is unstable because it cannot sustain its own foundations — is the frame inside which [Thiel's](peter-thiel.md) *The Straussian Moment* essay is written.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Kirchhain, Hesse, into an Orthodox Jewish family. PhD under Ernst Cassirer at Hamburg (1921). Worked at the Akademie für die Wissenschaft des Judentums in Berlin and studied with [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md) and Edmund Husserl. Left Germany in 1932 — first to France, then England, then the United States in 1937. Held positions at the New School, then the University of Chicago (1949–1968), where he shaped two generations of American political theorists. Later years at Claremont and St. John's College.

A formative early exchange: Strauss's correspondence and intellectual dueling with [Carl Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md). Strauss's 1932 review of Schmitt's *Concept of the Political* was judged by Schmitt himself to be the most penetrating reading his book received. The two men disagreed profoundly, but Strauss took from Schmitt the conviction that the theological-political question could not be avoided, that liberal modernity had tried to neutralize it, and that this attempt was failing.

## Core contributions

### The critique of modernity as three waves

Strauss periodized modern political philosophy into three waves:

1. **Machiavelli and Hobbes** — the "lowering of the horizon" from virtue to security/comfort; the redefinition of the good as what is effective
2. **Rousseau and Kant** — the attempt to recover freedom and dignity within the modern frame, now as autonomy rather than virtue
3. **Nietzsche and Heidegger** — the radicalization of the project into a confrontation with nihilism; the discovery that the modern project has no ground

Each wave, for Strauss, dug the West deeper into a crisis it then could not climb out of. His recommended response: return to the classical political philosophers (Plato, Aristotle, Xenophon, Thucydides) who posed the right questions.

### The theological-political problem

Strauss's lifelong preoccupation: the tension between *Jerusalem* (revelation, obedience, the authority of the sacred) and *Athens* (philosophy, free inquiry, reason). Both are genuine and neither can be reduced to the other. Modern liberalism's attempt to dissolve the tension by privatizing religion and rationalizing politics, per Strauss, is an evasion, not a solution.

### Esoteric writing

Strauss's scandalous hermeneutical claim: the great philosophers wrote in a double register. The exoteric text is for the general reader; the esoteric text, decipherable through close reading, contains teachings too dangerous, too heterodox, or too destabilizing for public consumption. *Persecution and the Art of Writing* (1952) lays out the method.

See [vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/concepts/esoteric-writing](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md).

This claim is the most contested of Strauss's contributions. His defenders see it as a recovery of how serious thinkers have always written. His critics see it as an apology for elitist manipulation and a license for intellectual cliquism.

### The recovery of classical political philosophy

Strauss argued that Plato, Aristotle, and the medieval Islamic and Jewish political philosophers (Alfarabi, Averroes, [Maimonides](maimonides.md)) understood political things in ways moderns had forgotten — in particular, the complex relationship between philosophy and the city, and the necessity of a rhetorical mask for philosophical truth in any political order.

## Key works

- *Spinoza's Critique of Religion* (1930)
- *Philosophy and Law* (1935)
- *On Tyranny* (1948, with the later Kojève exchange)
- *Persecution and the Art of Writing* (1952)
- *Natural Right and History* (1953) — his single most influential book
- *Thoughts on Machiavelli* (1958)
- *What Is Political Philosophy?* (1959)
- *The City and Man* (1964)
- *Liberalism Ancient and Modern* (1968)

## Influenced by

- Plato, Xenophon, Aristotle, Thucydides — treated as living interlocutors, not historical artifacts
- [vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/people/maimonides](maimonides.md) — medieval Jewish political philosopher; Strauss's lifelong touchstone
- Alfarabi, Averroes — medieval Islamic political philosophers who preserved the classical tradition
- [Machiavelli](niccolo-machiavelli.md) — Strauss's modernity-bad-guy-in-chief; *Thoughts on Machiavelli* is a hundred pages of close reading
- [Nietzsche](friedrich-nietzsche.md) — Strauss's early influence and lifelong provocation; the Straussian Nietzsche is the diagnostician of nihilism
- [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md) — Strauss's most formidable modern antagonist; the figure against whom Strauss defined his recovery of classical philosophy
- [Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md) — interlocutor on the theological-political problem
- [Kojève](alexandre-kojeve.md) — lifelong friend and philosophical correspondent; the Kojève–Strauss debate in *On Tyranny* is one of the great 20th-century philosophical exchanges

## Influenced

- [Allan Bloom](allan-bloom.md) — student; transmission vector to American higher education and to [Thiel](peter-thiel.md)
- Harry Jaffa, Joseph Cropsey, Harvey Mansfield, Stanley Rosen, Seth Benardete — core Straussian scholars
- The generation of American "neoconservative" intellectuals — Irving Kristol, William Kristol, Paul Wolfowitz, Francis Fukuyama (via Kojève as well) — with contested fidelity to actual Strauss
- Claremont Graduate School, the Hoover Institution — institutional Straussianism
- [Thiel](peter-thiel.md) — via Bloom and the Stanford/Hoover circle

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel

- Thiel's 2004 essay *The Straussian Moment* is its author's most explicit engagement with Strauss. The essay argues that Strauss and Schmitt were right about the theological-political problem and that Girard provides the anthropological ground both lacked.
- The Straussian posture of the philosopher as quiet, hidden, and in tension with the city informs Thiel's self-presentation.
- *Zero to One*'s mode — deliberately cryptic, saying things plainly to readers who can hear them — can be read as a Straussian popular text.

### To Karp

Strauss is essentially a figure Karp's tradition defined itself against. Habermas's defense of modern Enlightenment rationality is structurally the opposite of Strauss's critique of it. Karp reading Habermas is reading the most rigorous 20th-century defense of exactly what Strauss said could not be defended.
