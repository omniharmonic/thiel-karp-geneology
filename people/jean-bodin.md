# Jean Bodin

**(1530–1596)**

French jurist and political theorist. Author of *Les Six livres de la République* (*Six Books of the Commonwealth*, 1576) — the founding early-modern treatise on sovereignty. Relevant to this vault as the upstream source of the [political-theology](../concepts/political-theology.md) vocabulary [Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md) radicalized in the 20th century.

---

## The sovereignty claim

Writing in the aftermath of the French Wars of Religion (1562–1598), Bodin confronted a civil order that had lost confessional and political coherence. His answer: a unitary, absolute, indivisible sovereignty (*souveraineté*) residing in one person or body, whose authority cannot be shared or delegated without dissolution. The sovereign is the maker of law (*legislator*), not subject to it.

Key features of Bodin's sovereignty:

- **Indivisible.** Sovereignty cannot be mixed; the attempts of medieval political theory to balance competing authorities (king, estates, church) were, per Bodin, unstable.
- **Perpetual.** Not contingent on office-holder; not term-limited.
- **Constrained only by divine law, natural law, and the fundamental laws of the realm.** Otherwise unlimited.
- **The mark of sovereignty:** the power to make law unilaterally.

Bodin's framing is the foundation on which Hobbes ([see profile](thomas-hobbes.md)) will build his social-contract theory of the Leviathan, and on which modern state sovereignty as an institutional form will be constructed.

---

## Why Bodin matters for the Schmitt lineage

Schmitt's famous definition — "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception" — is a radicalization of Bodin's thesis. Where Bodin locates sovereignty in the power to make general law, Schmitt locates it in the power to suspend general law in a crisis. The move is philosophically decisive: Schmitt transfers the definition of sovereignty from the *routine* to the *exceptional*, which is what makes the concept [politically-theological](../concepts/political-theology.md).

But the structural frame is Bodin's. Without the Bodinian inheritance, Schmitt's move is not legible as a move on sovereignty at all.
