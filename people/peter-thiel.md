# Peter Thiel

**(b. 1967, Frankfurt, West Germany — ⋯)**

American entrepreneur and self-styled political philosopher. Co-founder of PayPal (1998), Palantir (2003, with [alex-karp](alex-karp.md)), and Founders Fund. One of the most philosophically self-conscious figures in American tech, and among the most consequential political donors in the 2016 and 2024 Republican cycles.

This profile treats Thiel as an intellectual figure — his philosophical influences, not his business history or political activities except where those illuminate the philosophy.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Frankfurt, raised partly in Namibia and South Africa, immigrated to the U.S. as a child. Undergraduate at Stanford (BA philosophy, 1989), where he took courses with [rene-girard](rene-girard.md) and founded *The Stanford Review* in 1987 as a conservative student paper. Stanford Law (JD, 1992), where he met [alex-karp](alex-karp.md). Brief career in securities law and derivatives trading before co-founding Confinity (→ PayPal) in 1998.

The two intellectual projects that run parallel to his business career:

1. **A Girardian diagnosis of late modernity** — most fully articulated in his 2004 essay *The Straussian Moment*, written in the aftermath of 9/11 for the Hoover Institution.
2. **A Schmittian/Straussian political theology** — developed in lectures, interviews, and through his funding of institutions like the Thiel Fellowship, the Edmund Burke Foundation, and various neoreactionary figures.

His 2014 book *Zero to One* (co-written with Blake Masters from his Stanford CS183 lectures) translates these ideas into a popular register.

## Core intellectual contributions

Thiel is not an academic philosopher. He is a synthesizer and vectorizer — someone who takes ideas from marginal corners of 20th-century political philosophy and translates them into the operating assumptions of one of the world's most powerful business and political networks. His contributions are:

### 1. The Girardian diagnosis of tech and late capitalism

Thiel extends Girard's [mimetic-theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md) into a theory of competition, innovation, and social media. His claim: most of what Silicon Valley calls "competition" is mimetic rivalry — desire copied from others, not rooted in reality — and therefore destructive. True innovation is *monopoly*: the creation of something so new that there is no one yet to imitate. *Zero to One*'s titular thesis (build something that doesn't exist, not something incrementally better than what does) is Girard translated into founder-speak.

He also reads social media in Girardian terms: platforms as mimetic amplifiers, content as mimetic contagion, and the resulting culture as a perpetual low-grade [scapegoat crisis](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md).

### 2. "The Straussian Moment" (2004)

Thiel's essay — available in the volume *Politics and Apocalypse* — argues that Western political thought has no resources to understand the violence of the post-9/11 world because it has forgotten the theological questions that [Strauss](leo-strauss.md) and [Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md) insisted were primary. The essay fuses:

- Strauss's critique of modern rationalism as a forgetting of the theological-political question
- Schmitt's [friend/enemy distinction](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md) and political theology
- Girard's [mimetic-theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md) as the true anthropological ground of both

The essay's central claim: modern liberalism's attempt to bracket the theological dimension of politics (via Hobbes, Locke, the Enlightenment) has produced a civilization that cannot comprehend its own enemies. Only Girard, per Thiel, offers a way to think the problem without collapsing back into Schmittian warfare.

### 3. Monopoly as Nietzschean will-to-form

Thiel's business philosophy — that "competition is for losers" — is explicitly Nietzschean in structure. Creating a monopoly is an act of [will-to-power](../concepts/will-to-power.md) against the democratic/mimetic leveling of the market. This is also where his [Nietzsche](friedrich-nietzsche.md) and his [Rand](ayn-rand.md) come together, though he has since distanced himself from Rand.

### 4. Techno-accelerationist eschatology

A recurring Thiel theme (especially post-2020): technological progress has slowed, the West is in stagnation, and without renewed acceleration the window for a human future closes. This draws on [Land](nick-land.md)'s accelerationism and [Yarvin](curtis-yarvin.md)'s neoreactionary diagnosis, refracted through Thiel's own Christian eschatology.

## Direct intellectual influences

### Girard — the primary

[rene-girard](rene-girard.md) was Thiel's Stanford professor. Thiel has called Girard the single most important thinker for understanding modernity. The influence runs through every layer of Thiel's thought: business strategy, political diagnosis, theological commitment. Thiel contributed a foreword to *Mimesis and Science* and has funded Girardian institutions (Imitatio).

See also: [mimetic-theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md), [scapegoat-mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md), [girardian-school](../schools/girardian-school.md).

### Strauss — the political framing

[leo-strauss](leo-strauss.md) gives Thiel the posture of [esoteric reading](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md) — the assumption that serious philosophical texts conceal more than they reveal, and that to read them rightly is to participate in a kind of elite interpretive community. "The Straussian Moment" is itself written in this mode: it says things plainly to those who can hear them and evasively to everyone else.

See: [straussianism](../schools/straussianism.md).

### Schmitt — the diagnostic register

[carl-schmitt](carl-schmitt.md) provides Thiel with a vocabulary for political conflict that modernity has forgotten how to speak: sovereignty, exception, decision, [friend/enemy](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md). Schmitt's critique of liberal parliamentarism is a recurring Thiel theme.

### Bloom and the Straussian undergraduate canon

[allan-bloom](allan-bloom.md)'s *The Closing of the American Mind* (1987) was assigned and argued over in Thiel's Stanford years. Bloom is the American transmission vector for the Straussian reading of Nietzsche, Plato, and the crisis of the university.

### Nietzsche — through Bloom and Girard

[friedrich-nietzsche](friedrich-nietzsche.md) reaches Thiel through two distinct paths: Bloom's Straussian Nietzsche (as diagnostician of modern nihilism) and Girard's anti-Nietzschean Nietzsche (as the great prophet of the post-Christian violent sacred). Thiel holds both.

### The libertarian axis: Hayek, Friedman, Rand

[friedrich-hayek](friedrich-hayek.md) and [milton-friedman](milton-friedman.md) form the economic substrate of Thiel's undergraduate politics — the [Austrian](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md) and [Chicago school](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md) traditions. [ayn-rand](ayn-rand.md) is the more popular-register entry point, though Thiel has explicitly moved away from Rand in his later writing as he became more Christian and more Girardian.

### Neoreaction and accelerationism

[curtis-yarvin](curtis-yarvin.md) (Mencius Moldbug) and [nick-land](nick-land.md) are contemporary interlocutors Thiel has funded, hosted, or explicitly endorsed. Both extend earlier Thielian themes — the critique of democratic modernity, the rejection of progressive inevitability, the call for civilizational reset.

### Christianity

Thiel has described himself as a Christian and has read that commitment explicitly through Girard: the Gospels as the revelation of the scapegoat mechanism, Christianity as the only tradition that has seen through sacred violence. This is distinct from American evangelical politics and closer to the Augustinian strain Girard himself inhabited.

### Tolkien

[jrr-tolkien](jrr-tolkien.md) — not a philosophical influence, but worth noting as imaginative substrate. Palantir (Elvish for "seeing stone"), Valar Ventures, Mithril Capital, Rivendell — the naming system is deliberate. Thiel has described his companies as attempts to build what Middle-earth's wise characters would build. The Tolkien frame positions the modern enterprise as a defense of civilization against orcish/Sauronic forces — a mythopoetic version of Schmittian political theology.

## Relationship to Karp

See [alex-karp](alex-karp.md) for the reciprocal view and [the-central-tension](../synthesis/the-central-tension.md) for analysis.

The short version: Karp was Thiel's philosophical sparring partner at Stanford Law. They disagreed about everything. Karp was (and is) broadly on the left; Thiel on the right. Karp's philosophical formation was about to become Frankfurt School critical theory via [jurgen-habermas](jurgen-habermas.md); Thiel's was Strauss–Girard–Schmitt. When Thiel co-founded Palantir in 2003 he called Karp — not despite the disagreement, but because of it. The Palantir founding team has described the friendship as argumentative, continuous, and mutually sharpening.

## Critical angles

Thiel's philosophical synthesis has been critiqued from both the left (for its authoritarian tendencies, its Schmittian politics of exception) and from more rigorous traditional corners of the schools he draws on (Girardians who think his business-strategy extension of mimetic theory is too instrumental; Straussians who think his public mode is not sufficiently esoteric). These critiques are noted across the [Girardian school](../schools/girardian-school.md) and [Straussian](../schools/straussianism.md) school entries.
