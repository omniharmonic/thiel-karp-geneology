# Martin Heidegger

**(1889–1976)**

German philosopher. The most influential continental philosopher of the 20th century and one of the most politically compromised. Nazi Party member from 1933 until the end of the war; never repudiated the commitment in a way his critics found adequate. Also the thinker whose engagement with the question of being — and with the nature of technology — shaped virtually every major figure in this vault on either side of the Thiel–Karp divide.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Messkirch, Baden, to a Catholic sexton. Trained in scholastic theology; then philosophy under Husserl at Freiburg. *Sein und Zeit* (*Being and Time*) in 1927 made him internationally famous. Succeeded Husserl as chair at Freiburg in 1928. Rector of Freiburg 1933–1934, delivered the notorious Nazi rectoral address. After the war barred from teaching until 1951, then restored. Continued to write and lecture from his hut in Tödtnauberg until his death.

## Core contributions

### The question of being (*Seinsfrage*)

Heidegger's lifelong project: to retrieve the question of being, which he argued had been forgotten by the Western philosophical tradition since Plato. Being is not a being — not an entity among entities — but the horizon within which entities show up as entities. Philosophy, since Plato, has been obsessed with *beings* and has lost the question of *Being as such*.

### *Being and Time* (1927)

An analysis of *Dasein* — the kind of being we ourselves are — as the entity for whom being is a question. Being-in-the-world, thrownness, the they-self, authenticity, being-toward-death, temporality, historicity. These concepts shaped continental philosophy and existentialism for a century.

### The Kehre (turn) and later Heidegger

From around 1930, Heidegger's work turned from the analytic of Dasein toward *the history of being* — the claim that the Western philosophical tradition is itself an epochal unfolding of being's self-concealment. Each epoch (Platonism, Christianity, Enlightenment, nihilism) reveals entities in a characteristic way and forgets what is forgotten.

### The question concerning technology

Heidegger's most consequential 20th-century essay. Technology is not merely a collection of tools. It is a mode of revealing — a way of letting things show up. Modern technology reveals everything as *standing-reserve* (*Bestand*): resources to be extracted, optimized, and deployed. This revealing is not neutral; it covers over other ways things could show up. The danger is not technology's misuse but its successful use: we become unable to see anything except through the lens of optimization.

This essay is ground zero for the philosophical critique of technology that both Karp's Frankfurt School lineage and various Thiel-adjacent thinkers have continued. Adorno's *Dialectic of Enlightenment* and Karp's worry about engineering talent being privatized are both audible here, at distance.

### Language, poetry, and the clearing (*Lichtung*)

Late Heidegger turns toward poetry (Hölderlin, Trakl, Rilke) as the site where being still speaks. Language is "the house of being." The clearing is the opening in which beings can appear. These moves are often read as quasi-mystical.

## Influenced by

- Edmund Husserl — phenomenology; his teacher
- Aristotle — especially on phronesis and the categories
- Pre-Socratics (Heraclitus, Parmenides) — as the origin before the fall into metaphysics
- Søren Kierkegaard — existence, anxiety, the individual
- Wilhelm Dilthey — historicity, life-philosophy
- [Nietzsche](friedrich-nietzsche.md) — the last metaphysician in Heidegger's reading
- Hölderlin, Rilke, Trakl — poetic sources for late Heidegger

## Influenced

- [Strauss](leo-strauss.md) — attended Heidegger's seminars; eventually defined his own project against Heidegger's
- Hans-Georg Gadamer — hermeneutics
- Hannah Arendt — student and lover; Arendt's political philosophy is partly a response
- Karl Jaspers — existentialist interlocutor
- Jean-Paul Sartre — French existentialism; Heidegger distanced himself from Sartre's reading
- Maurice Merleau-Ponty — phenomenology of perception
- Emmanuel Levinas — the ethics Heidegger never wrote
- Jacques Derrida, Michel Foucault — French post-structuralism
- [Marcuse](herbert-marcuse.md) — studied with Heidegger before leaving Germany
- [Apel](karl-otto-apel.md) — started inside Heideggerian phenomenology before breaking toward pragmatism
- [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) — Heidegger's 1953 republication of *Introduction to Metaphysics* was Habermas's formative political awakening; spent his career trying to exit Heideggerian thought-shapes while preserving their phenomenological insights
- Giorgio Agamben — directly Heideggerian

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel

Indirect but real. Heidegger's critique of modern technology as *Gestell* (enframing) and his diagnosis of the "forgetting of being" feed into the broader Straussian, Schmittian, and neoreactionary critiques of modernity that Thiel has absorbed. Thiel's concerns about civilizational stagnation and the slowing of technological progress have a Heideggerian structure (though Thiel would resist the comparison: Heidegger thinks technology is too much; Thiel thinks there isn't enough of it).

Yarvin is explicitly Heideggerian in places. Nick Land is post-Heideggerian at every step.

### To Karp

Through Habermas: the figure you cannot avoid but must never accept. Habermas's entire life's work is a sustained effort to preserve what is true in Heidegger's diagnosis of modernity while refusing the political collapse into authoritarianism that Heidegger's own biography dramatized. Karp inherits this posture.

Through Marcuse and Adorno: the technology-critique vocabulary that Karp deploys in *The Technological Republic* is Heideggerian at the root, even where it has been worked through critical theory.
