# Max Horkheimer

**(1895–1973)**

German philosopher. Director of the Institute for Social Research (the Frankfurt School's institutional body) from 1930, through its American exile, and during its return to Frankfurt in 1951. With [Adorno](theodor-adorno.md), co-author of *Dialectic of Enlightenment*. The figure who more than any other made the Frankfurt School what it became.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born to a Jewish industrialist family in Stuttgart. Expected to inherit the family textile business; broke with his father to pursue philosophy. Habilitation at Frankfurt (1925). Took over the Institute for Social Research in 1930 and immediately reoriented it from orthodox Marxist economics toward a multi-disciplinary integration of philosophy, sociology, psychoanalysis, and aesthetics.

Led the Institute into exile in 1933 (Geneva, Paris, then New York's Columbia University via sponsorship). During the American years, the Institute's research included *The Authoritarian Personality* study and *Dialectic of Enlightenment*. Returned to Frankfurt in 1949; director of the Institute and Rector of the University. Retirement (1958), after which Adorno assumed direction.

Horkheimer's own later philosophical orientation turned increasingly toward Schopenhauer and a melancholic theological register (the "longing for the wholly other") — a move his more politically engaged students (Marcuse, Habermas) did not follow.

## Core contributions

### "Traditional and Critical Theory" (1937)

Horkheimer's programmatic essay. "Traditional theory" describes the theorist as detached observer describing a world that stands over against them. "Critical theory" refuses this posture: the theorist is always located in society, has interests, and theorizes in service of emancipatory practice. Theory and emancipation are internal to each other.

This essay defines what "critical theory" would come to mean as a research program. See [critical-theory](../schools/critical-theory.md).

### The integration of social research

Under Horkheimer's direction, the Institute pioneered an integrated research program that combined:
- Philosophical reflection (Adorno, Marcuse, Horkheimer himself)
- Empirical sociology (Pollock on political economy; later *The Authoritarian Personality*)
- Psychoanalysis (Erich Fromm, then the appropriation of Freud by Adorno and Marcuse)
- Cultural analysis (Benjamin, Lowenthal, Kracauer)
- Economic analysis (Pollock, Neumann)

No single thinker within the Institute would have produced critical theory as such. Horkheimer's contribution was the framework within which their collective work could accumulate.

### *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (with Adorno)

See [Adorno](theodor-adorno.md) profile and [dialectic-of-enlightenment](../concepts/dialectic-of-enlightenment.md) for the detailed account. Horkheimer's contributions foregrounded the critique of instrumental reason (*Zweckrationalität*) and the historical genealogy of the Enlightenment subject.

### *Eclipse of Reason* (1947)

A more accessible version of the *Dialectic of Enlightenment* themes, written in English during the American years. Traces the degeneration of "objective reason" (reason as capable of identifying the good) into "subjective reason" (reason as mere calculation in service of pre-given ends). Anticipates much of Karp's later diagnosis about purely instrumental engineering talent.

## Key works

- "Materialism and Metaphysics" (1933)
- "Traditional and Critical Theory" (1937)
- *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1944/1947, with Adorno)
- *Eclipse of Reason* (1947)
- *Critique of Instrumental Reason* (late essays, collected 1967)
- *The Longing for the Wholly Other* (late essays and interviews)

## Influenced by

- [Marx](karl-marx.md) — the Institute was founded as a Marxist research institute; Horkheimer preserved the orientation while radically expanding its terrain
- [Hegel](gwf-hegel.md) — dialectical structure
- [Freud](sigmund-freud.md) — psychoanalysis integrated into social theory
- Schopenhauer — especially in later years
- Kant and Hegel — the German idealist backdrop

## Influenced

- [Adorno](theodor-adorno.md) — collaborator; successor at the Institute
- [Marcuse](herbert-marcuse.md) — Institute colleague
- [Benjamin](walter-benjamin.md) — supported with Institute funding in exile
- [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) — complicated relationship; Horkheimer opposed Habermas's Habilitation on political grounds
- Erich Fromm, Leo Lowenthal, Friedrich Pollock, Franz Neumann, Otto Kirchheimer — other Institute members
- The entire Frankfurt School as institutional program
- [Karp](alex-karp.md) — indirect, via Habermas and the Frankfurt School lineage

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

Indirect but structurally important. Karp's formal academic formation was inside the institution Horkheimer built. The Habermasian system Karp works within was made possible by the Institute Horkheimer directed. The concept of critical theory itself — the idea that a theorist's work should emancipate rather than merely describe — is Horkheimer's coinage and Karp's inherited posture.
