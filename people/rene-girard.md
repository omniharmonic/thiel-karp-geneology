# René Girard

**(1923–2015)**

French literary critic, philosophical anthropologist, and Catholic Christian apologist. Long-time professor at Stanford University (1981–1995 and emeritus thereafter). Author of a unified theoretical project — [mimetic theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md) — that claims to explain the origins of human desire, culture, sacred violence, and the singular world-historical role of the Judeo-Christian Gospels.

Girard is the single most important intellectual influence on [Peter Thiel](peter-thiel.md), who took Girard's courses as a Stanford undergraduate.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Avignon. Trained as a medievalist at the École des Chartes, then emigrated to the United States in 1947, teaching French literature at Indiana, Bryn Mawr, Johns Hopkins, SUNY Buffalo, and finally Stanford, where he remained the rest of his life. Elected to the Académie Française in 2005.

His career unfolds as a long arc of three phases, each building on the previous:

1. **Literary mimesis (1961)** — *Deceit, Desire, and the Novel* argues that the great European novelists (Cervantes, Stendhal, Flaubert, Proust, Dostoevsky) all discovered the same truth: human desire is not autonomous but triangular — we desire what others desire because they desire it. The novel is the genre in which this truth becomes visible.

2. **Anthropological mimesis (1972)** — *Violence and the Sacred* extends the thesis into a theory of culture: mimetic desire produces rivalry, rivalry escalates into crisis, crisis is resolved through the spontaneous unanimous violence of the community against an arbitrary victim (the [scapegoat](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md)), and the residue of that resolution is religion — ritual to repeat it, myth to conceal it, prohibition to prevent its recurrence.

3. **Christian revelation (1978)** — *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World* argues that the Hebrew Bible and especially the Gospels are the unique texts that reveal the scapegoat mechanism from the side of the victim, thereby rendering it progressively inoperative. Christianity, in this reading, is not one more myth but the anti-myth: the revelation of the lie that all mythology depends on.

## Core contributions

### Mimetic desire

Humans do not desire objects for themselves. We desire them because someone else — a *model* — desires them. Desire is mediated, triangular, copied. This is [vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/concepts/mimetic-theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md).

The everyday consequence: identity is not prior to desire but constituted by the models we imitate. "Tell me what you want and I'll tell me who you are" is a Girardian maxim.

The catastrophic consequence: when two people desire the same object via mutual mediation, they converge on the object and then on each other — rivalry. Rivalry makes the model into an obstacle, and the obstacle into a double. Mimetic escalation is the engine of conflict.

### The scapegoat mechanism

When mimetic rivalry becomes generalized across a community — a "mimetic crisis" — violence converges spontaneously on an arbitrary victim. The victim is blamed for the crisis; the victim is killed or expelled; the community is unified in its violence; peace returns.

The victim retroactively appears to have caused the crisis *and* to have brought the peace — hence sacred. Religion (ritual, prohibition, myth) grows up to re-enact, remember, and repeat this founding murder. Culture, for Girard, is founded on a lie — the lie that the victim was guilty.

See [vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/concepts/scapegoat-mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md).

### The Judeo-Christian revelation

Girard's most controversial claim: the Hebrew Bible progressively inverts the mythic point of view (the victim is innocent — Abel, Job, the Psalmists, the Suffering Servant), and the Gospels complete the inversion (Jesus is the victim whose innocence is definitively proclaimed). Once this revelation is in the cultural bloodstream, scapegoating still works — but it works less well, and with more shame, than in purely mythic cultures. The modern concern for victims, even when it is itself turned into a scapegoating mechanism, is a Gospel artifact.

This is why Girard insists he is a *Christian* thinker, not merely a theorist who happens to notice that the Gospels have an interesting structure.

### The apocalyptic turn

In his later work (*I See Satan Fall Like Lightning*, 1999; *Battling to the End*, 2007), Girard argues that the Gospel's de-mystification of sacred violence does not make the world more peaceful — it makes mimetic rivalry more dangerous by stripping away the containment mechanism. Modernity's "escalation to extremes" (read through his late engagement with [[vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/people/carl-von-clausewitz|Clausewitz]]) is the logical endpoint.

Thiel's apocalyptic register — his recurring concern that modernity has broken a containment and is heading for civilizational crisis — is directly Girardian.

## Influenced by

- [Sigmund Freud](sigmund-freud.md) — Girard defines his work partly against Freud, but borrows deeply from the Oedipal structure, which he re-reads as mimetic rivalry
- [Nietzsche](friedrich-nietzsche.md) — Girard calls Nietzsche "the most profound of the anti-Christians" and grants him the status of the one modern thinker who saw what was at stake in the Gospel's inversion of mythic perspective, even while getting the valence backwards
- [Hegel](gwf-hegel.md) (via [Kojève](alexandre-kojeve.md)) — the master/slave dialectic informs Girard's account of mimetic doubling
- Marcel Proust, Fyodor Dostoevsky, Miguel de Cervantes — the novelists who for Girard had already discovered mimetic theory
- Émile Durkheim — especially *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* on collective effervescence
- Claude Lévi-Strauss — whose structural anthropology Girard argues is incomplete without the scapegoat mechanism
- The Hebrew Bible and the Gospels — treated as primary texts, not as allegory

## Influenced

- [Peter Thiel](peter-thiel.md) — direct student at Stanford; primary American transmission vector
- Jean-Michel Oughourlian — Girard's collaborator and interlocutor in *Things Hidden*
- Eric Gans — Girard's student; founder of Generative Anthropology
- James Alison — Catholic theologian, primary English-language interpreter
- Gil Bailie, Robert Hamerton-Kelly, Raymund Schwager — theological interpreters
- The entire [Girardian school](../schools/girardian-school.md) and the Colloquium on Violence and Religion
- Thiel-funded Imitatio foundation (2007) — continues Girard's research program

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel

Thiel has cited Girard more than any other figure as his primary intellectual influence. Specific threads:

- **Mimetic theory as business strategy** — *Zero to One*'s central argument (competition is destructive mimesis; monopoly is the only form of value creation) is Girard operationalized.
- **Scapegoat mechanism as political diagnosis** — Thiel's readings of contemporary politics (cancel culture, social media pile-ons, the "Woke Religion") are Girardian.
- **Christianity as non-sacrificial truth** — Thiel's theological commitment runs through Girard, not through ordinary American evangelicalism.
- **Apocalyptic sensibility** — Thiel's diagnosis of civilizational crisis picks up Girard's late work directly.

### To Karp

No direct formal relationship. But Girard's theory of violence is the obvious Thielian counterweight to Karp's Habermasian theory of communicative rationality. Their friendship is, in one reading, a live debate between these two theories of how humans manage (or fail to manage) violence.
