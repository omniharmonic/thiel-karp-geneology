# Alexandre Kojève

**(Alexandre Vladimirovitch Kojevnikov, 1902–1968)**

Russian-born French philosopher. His 1933–1939 seminar on [Hegel](gwf-hegel.md)'s *Phenomenology of Spirit* at the École Pratique des Hautes Études in Paris — attended by Jacques Lacan, Raymond Aron, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Georges Bataille, Raymond Queneau, and (via transmission) virtually the entire postwar French philosophical generation — is the single most consequential piece of 20th-century Hegel reception. Kojève is the reason the master–slave dialectic became a central reference for postwar continental thought.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Moscow to a bourgeois family; left Russia after the Revolution. PhD under Karl Jaspers at Heidelberg (1926) on Vladimir Solovyov. Paris from 1927. Took over Alexandre Koyré's seminar on Hegel in 1933.

After the seminar's end and WWII, Kojève became a senior French economic bureaucrat — a principal architect of what became the European Economic Community and GATT. He continued to think and write philosophy but produced no major book. He died on the job at a trade conference.

## Core contributions

### The Kojèvian Hegel

Kojève's reading — philosophically idiosyncratic, philologically arguable — treats the *Phenomenology* as organized around the master–slave dialectic. Human self-consciousness emerges only through the struggle for recognition: two proto-humans confront each other, each demanding to be recognized as human by the other. The one who fears death surrenders and becomes the slave; the one who risks death becomes the master. But the master, recognized only by a slave, receives degraded recognition; the slave, by working on the world, develops consciousness. History is the working-out of this asymmetry toward universal mutual recognition.

### The end of history

History, for Kojève, has a telos: the condition in which every human is recognized as such by every other. The French Revolution, completing what Napoleon enacted at Jena (where Hegel watched him ride past while finishing the *Phenomenology*), is the inauguration of this condition. The remainder of history is the global diffusion of the terms of universal recognition.

Kojève's late thinking worried that the end-state is post-historical: humans become either American consumers (happy animals) or Japanese snobs (playing stylized games with content they no longer believe). This image structures Francis Fukuyama's *The End of History and the Last Man* and, via Fukuyama, the broader post-1989 debate.

### The Kojève–Strauss exchange

One of the great 20th-century philosophical dialogues, around Xenophon's *Hiero*. Kojève defends the tyrant-philosopher alliance as necessary for historical action; Strauss defends philosophy as withdrawal from tyranny. Both men treated the exchange as fundamental.

## Key works

- *Introduction to the Reading of Hegel* (*Introduction à la lecture de Hegel*, 1947, edited by Raymond Queneau from seminar notes)
- *On Tyranny* (1948, the exchange with [Strauss](leo-strauss.md))
- *Outline of a Phenomenology of Right* (posthumous)
- Various essays on Kant, Descartes, and the philosophy of history

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

Kojève is the transmission node that connects [Hegel](gwf-hegel.md)'s master-slave dialectic to both Thiel's and Karp's worlds:

- **To Thiel**: via [Girard](rene-girard.md) (who absorbed Kojève's reading into mimetic theory) and via Fukuyama (whose end-of-history thesis Thiel engaged extensively).
- **To Karp**: via [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md)'s engagement with Hegel and via the Frankfurt School's inheritance of the French Hegelian moment.

The recognition-struggle structure is the deep pattern both traditions share — Thiel reading it as doomed without Christianity, Karp reading it as rationally completable in democratic institutions.
