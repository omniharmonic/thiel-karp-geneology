# Ludwig Wittgenstein

**(1889–1951)**

Austrian-British philosopher. One of the two or three most influential philosophers of the 20th century; the central figure of 20th-century analytic philosophy of language. Relevant to this vault as the deep background to the Anglophone speech-act theory that [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) and [Apel](karl-otto-apel.md) imported into German critical theory in the 1970s, and as the thinker whose later work on language-games is in live (if indirect) dialogue with the hermeneutic tradition Karp's branch inherited.

---

## Two philosophies

Wittgenstein's work divides into two distinct phases separated by a decade of non-academic life.

### Early Wittgenstein: *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921)

A short, highly compressed book structured in numbered propositions. Core claims:

- Language mirrors the world by logical *picturing*
- Elementary propositions depict possible states of affairs
- The limits of language are the limits of my world
- What cannot be said must be passed over in silence

The *Tractatus* was canonical for the Vienna Circle logical positivists (Carnap, Neurath, Schlick) — though Wittgenstein considered the Circle's logical empiricism a misreading.

### Later Wittgenstein: *Philosophical Investigations* (1953, posthumous)

Wittgenstein's repudiation and reconstruction of his own early work. Core moves:

- **Meaning is use.** The meaning of a word is its role in a pattern of human activity, not a picture of an object.
- **Language-games.** Language is a plurality of embedded practices, each with its own internal logic. There is no single thing called "language" with a single function.
- **Forms of life** (*Lebensformen*). Language-games are embedded in shared patterns of human activity. To understand a language is to participate in the form of life that makes it possible.
- **Private language is impossible.** A purely private meaning, accessible only to the speaker, would not count as meaning; meaning requires public criteria.

The later Wittgenstein is a foundational text for ordinary-language philosophy, for much of contemporary philosophy of mind, and for the Anglophone pragmatic turn.

## Relevance to this vault

### The speech-act tradition

J.L. Austin, John Searle, and Paul Grice — whose speech-act theory [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) explicitly imported to ground his theory of [communicative action](../concepts/communicative-action.md) — operate downstream of later Wittgenstein. The claim that speech acts have force, that validity claims are raised implicitly in ordinary speech, that the meaning of an utterance is its use-in-context — these are Wittgenstein-adjacent ideas that Habermas welded to the Frankfurt-School project.

### The Apel synthesis

[Apel](karl-otto-apel.md)'s transcendental pragmatics is explicitly an attempt to combine Wittgensteinian ordinary-language analysis with the continental tradition. Apel's claim that every argument presupposes certain pragmatic conditions (a community of interlocutors, validity claims raisable in principle by any competent speaker) is Wittgenstein-informed.

### The *private language* argument and publicity

Habermas's insistence on the publicity of reasons — that validity claims must be defensible in principle to any participant in the discourse — is a generalization of Wittgenstein's private-language argument. Language is irreducibly public because meaning requires public criteria. This is the Anglophone-analytic ground of Habermas's anti-[esoteric-writing](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md) commitment.

### The Strauss-Wittgenstein opposition

[Strauss](leo-strauss.md) and Wittgenstein are rarely read together, but they represent opposite 20th-century hermeneutic commitments. Strauss: philosophical texts have esoteric depths recoverable by careful authorial-intention reading. Late Wittgenstein: meaning is use; there is no author's-intention depth beneath the practices. The graph includes no direct Strauss-Wittgenstein edge, but the two traditions face each other as alternative 20th-century answers to the question of how to read.

## Influenced by

- Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell — logical ancestors (early period)
- Arthur Schopenhauer — early influence
- [Freud](sigmund-freud.md) — acknowledged ambivalently
- No obvious later-period canonical influences; the *Investigations* emerges from Wittgenstein's own rethinking

## Influenced

- The Vienna Circle (early)
- Ordinary language philosophy — Austin, Ryle, Strawson
- Philosophy of mind generally
- [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) and [Apel](karl-otto-apel.md) via Austin/Searle
- Saul Kripke (*Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language*)
- Richard Rorty, John McDowell — pragmatist inheritors
