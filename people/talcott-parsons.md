# Talcott Parsons

**(1902–1979)**

American sociologist. The dominant figure of post-war American sociology, author of a vast systematic sociology of action and social systems. Karp's dissertation (*Aggression in der Lebenswelt*) extends Parsons's concept of aggression — Karp's dissertation is in part an argument about how Parsons's systems theory should be articulated with Habermas's life-world theory.

## Core contributions

- **The AGIL schema:** every social system must solve four functional problems — Adaptation (economy), Goal-attainment (polity), Integration (societal community / law), and Latent pattern-maintenance (fiduciary system / culture).
- **Action theory:** *The Structure of Social Action* (1937) synthesizes Weber, Durkheim, Pareto, and Marshall into a voluntarist theory of action.
- **Systems theory:** *The Social System* (1951) extends action theory into a systems-theoretic account of how societies maintain equilibrium under internal differentiation.

## Relevance to Karp

Karp's dissertation works at the intersection of Parsons (for the systems-theoretic frame of aggression as a social-structural problem) and Habermas (for the communicative-action frame of how aggression is metabolized in the life-world through linguistic accommodation). Parsons is the American sociological vocabulary Karp brought to Germany.

## Relevance to Thiel

Indirect. Parsons is not part of Thiel's reading. But Parsons's vocabulary of differentiation, integration, and the cybernetic ordering of action systems is part of the mid-20th-century American social-science frame that Silicon Valley management theory eventually absorbed.
