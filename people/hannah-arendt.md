# Hannah Arendt

**(1906–1975)**

German-American political philosopher. Student and lover of [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md); student of Karl Jaspers; friend of [Benjamin](walter-benjamin.md) (she edited the first English selection of his work, *Illuminations*, 1968); acute observer of totalitarianism, revolution, and the human condition. A figure neither Thiel nor Karp has named as an explicit influence, but whose work sits at several of the graph's real crossing-points.

---

## Core contributions

### *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951)

A three-volume analysis of the conditions that produced Nazi and Soviet totalitarianism: 19th-century antisemitism; European imperialism and its racialization of power; the atomization of mass society. The book treats totalitarianism as a genuinely novel political form — not a variation on older tyrannies but something that depends on specifically modern conditions (mass society, instrumental rationality, the ideologization of politics).

The book is a live companion to the [Frankfurt-School dialectic of Enlightenment](../concepts/dialectic-of-enlightenment.md), though Arendt arrived at related conclusions from very different premises.

### *The Human Condition* (1958)

A phenomenological reconstruction of three modes of human activity:

- **Labor** — biological necessity; cyclical; disappears with consumption
- **Work** — fabrication; creation of the durable world of objects
- **Action** — the disclosure of *who* a person is through speech and deed, among equals, in public

Arendt's argument: modernity has progressively elevated labor over work and action, dissolving the public realm where genuine politics occurs. This is a diagnostic parallel to what Habermas will later theorize as the colonization of the life-world, and to the Frankfurt-School critique of instrumental reason.

### *Eichmann in Jerusalem* (1963)

Arendt's report on the Jerusalem trial of Adolf Eichmann, which introduced the phrase *the banality of evil*. The claim: Eichmann was not a demonic figure but a thoughtless bureaucrat; the specifically modern form of evil operates through non-reflection and careerist accommodation. The book was deeply controversial in Jewish intellectual life for its treatment of Jewish Council collaboration but has become canonical.

### On revolution, freedom, and public action

*On Revolution* (1963) contrasts the American and French revolutions as models of founding. *Between Past and Future* (1961) collects essays on authority, freedom, and tradition. Throughout, Arendt maintains a commitment to politics as the distinctive sphere of human freedom — neither the private household nor the administered society.

## Relevance to this vault

Arendt is a structural bridge for several reasons the graph-topology report surfaced:

### Heidegger student, political opponent

Arendt preserves the phenomenological depth of [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md)'s analytic of *Dasein* while repudiating his political collapse into Nazism. She is, in a sense, the non-Nazi Heideggerian — a model of how the philosophical apparatus can be separated from the political ruin. Both Karp's and Thiel's traditions have had to perform the same separation.

### Benjamin editor

Arendt's 1968 *Illuminations* introduced [Benjamin](walter-benjamin.md) to the English-reading world. The Frankfurt School's American reception ran substantially through her editorial work.

### Political theology, in dialogue with Schmitt

Arendt's theorization of political action, power, and founding is in sustained implicit dialogue with [Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md). Her *On Violence* (1970) is partly a reply to Schmitt (and to her former student Sheldon Wolin's engagement with him). Where Schmitt identifies sovereignty with the decision, Arendt identifies power with joint action.

### The banality of evil as Frankfurt-adjacent

*Eichmann in Jerusalem*'s diagnosis of bureaucratic evil-through-thoughtlessness converges with the Frankfurt-school analysis of the administered society, though Arendt arrived there phenomenologically rather than through Marxian ideology-critique.

## Influenced by

- [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md) — teacher, lover; the formative philosophical influence
- Karl Jaspers — thesis advisor; lifelong friend and correspondent
- [Walter Benjamin](walter-benjamin.md) — friend and intellectual partner
- Aristotle — on the polis and political action
- Augustine — Arendt's dissertation was on Augustine's concept of love
- Tocqueville and Montesquieu — on political forms

## Influenced

- Sheldon Wolin, Judith Shklar — American political theorists who built on her
- Seyla Benhabib (who bridges Arendt and Habermas) — contemporary political theory
- Giorgio Agamben — political theology
- Much of the post-1989 debate about civic life and democratic renewal
