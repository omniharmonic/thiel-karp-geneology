# J.R.R. Tolkien

**(John Ronald Reuel Tolkien, 1892–1973)**

British philologist, Oxford professor, Catholic, and author of *The Hobbit* (1937) and *The Lord of the Rings* (1954–1955). Not a philosopher in the technical sense, but the imaginative substrate of Thiel's corporate naming and of his larger mythopoetic self-understanding.

## Relevance to Thiel

Thiel's business-naming system is unashamedly Tolkien-derived:

- **Palantir** — Elvish *palantír*, "seeing stone." In Tolkien, a dangerous artifact: the one who looks in sees truly but is drawn into the gaze of Sauron.
- **Valar Ventures** — the Valar, Tolkien's angelic powers who guide Middle-earth.
- **Mithril Capital** — mithril, the indestructible silver-like metal, symbol of rare value.
- **Rivendell** — a cluster of related entities over time.

The naming pattern is more than decorative. Thiel has spoken about Tolkien in interviews as a formative influence on his moral imagination, especially the Tolkienian stance that civilization is fragile, that most people would prefer to ignore the threat, and that the work of the wise is to see clearly and act even when others cannot.

This Tolkienian frame *mythologizes* Schmittian political theology: Palantir's adversaries become orcs and Sauronic forces; the founder becomes a steward of the West; surveillance technology becomes a seeing stone whose use is fraught but necessary. Critics have argued that this mythology is more self-flattering than self-diagnostic. But it is a real through-line.

## Other reception

Tolkien himself was a devout Catholic and deeply conservative in temperament, though uninterested in party politics. His work was shaped by WWI trauma (Battle of the Somme), medieval Catholic sensibility, and love of Northern European languages. His friend and Inkling C.S. Lewis represents a parallel (though more polemical) cultural conservatism.
