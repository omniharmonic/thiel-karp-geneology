# Friedrich Hayek

**(Friedrich August von Hayek, 1899–1992)**

Austrian-British economist and political philosopher. Nobel laureate (1974). Alongside Mises, the central figure of the [Austrian school](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md) and the most serious 20th-century theorist of spontaneous order and the limits of economic planning.

## Core claims

- **Knowledge problem:** Economic planning cannot work because the knowledge relevant to coordination ("knowledge of time and place") is dispersed across millions of actors and cannot be aggregated by any central authority. Prices are the mechanism that aggregates this dispersed knowledge.
- **Spontaneous order:** Complex social institutions (language, law, markets) are results of human action but not human design. Attempts to rationalize them wholesale destroy information they encoded.
- **Road to serfdom:** Economic planning requires political authoritarianism to work; a socialist economy cannot be democratic.
- **Rule-of-law liberalism:** The proper role of the state is to enforce general rules, not to direct outcomes.

## Influences

- Carl Menger, Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk — Austrian school founders
- Ludwig von Mises — Hayek's teacher; the socialist-calculation-debate framework
- Karl Popper — epistemology of open society
- British classical liberalism (Hume, Smith, Acton)

## Influenced

- Friedman — Mont Pelerin Society
- [Thiel](peter-thiel.md) — libertarian substrate
- Post-1979 neoliberal policy
- Internet libertarianism and crypto culture

## Relevance to Thiel

Hayek's knowledge problem and his spontaneous-order framing are part of Thiel's operating vocabulary. The Palantir defense of decentralized battlefield intelligence, and the startup ecosystem generally, are often described in Hayekian terms. Thiel's distrust of central planning derives here.
