# Carl Schmitt

**(1888–1985)**

German jurist, political theorist, and self-described "Catholic with political-theological interests." The most influential and most contaminated political thinker of the 20th century. Briefly the "crown jurist" of the Nazi regime after 1933 — a fact that any account of Schmitt must hold in view — and the originator of the vocabulary that most subsequent theorists of sovereignty, the state of exception, and the political have had to either adopt or refute.

Schmitt is read by [Thiel](peter-thiel.md) (approvingly, with reservations) and by [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) (as the antagonist of liberal democracy his own work is designed to defeat). The two primary subjects of this vault face each other across a Schmittian line.

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Plettenberg, Westphalia, into a Catholic family in a Protestant region. Studied law at Berlin, Munich, and Strasbourg. Academic career at Bonn, then Berlin, then Cologne. A formidable legal-political theorist of the Weimar Republic, writing against parliamentary liberalism from a Catholic-authoritarian perspective.

Joined the Nazi Party in May 1933. Wrote *State, Movement, People* (1933) attempting a legal theory of the Nazi state. Fell out of favor with the SS in 1936 but retained his chair. After 1945 he was detained but never tried; barred from university teaching for the rest of his life. Continued to publish and to cultivate a private circle of correspondents from Plettenberg until his death in 1985 at 96.

The post-war influence runs in two directions:

1. **Directly on the right** — to authoritarian-conservative and religious-right thinkers who took Schmitt's critique of liberalism as diagnostic
2. **On the left via critical engagement** — Walter Benjamin, the early Frankfurt School, Giorgio Agamben, Chantal Mouffe, and Habermas (as antagonist) all read Schmitt to extract what is diagnostically useful while repudiating the politics

Schmitt is thus one of the rare figures with a major reception on both right and left — which is why he functions as a bridge between Thiel and Karp's traditions.

## Core contributions

### The concept of the political (1927/1932)

Schmitt's most famous claim: the specific distinction of the political is [friend and enemy](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md). Politics is the domain in which groups identify who is their enemy — that is, who they are prepared, if necessary, to kill. This is not a moral judgment but an existential one. The enemy is not the criminal (who remains within the political community) or the economic competitor (who is in a different domain). The enemy is the other collectivity who may at the limit require violent confrontation.

Liberalism, per Schmitt, tries to dissolve the political by reducing everything to economic competition or moral judgment. Schmitt argues this is impossible — the political persists regardless; liberalism simply fails to see it.

### Political theology (1922)

> "All significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are secularized theological concepts."

Schmitt's thesis: modern political concepts (sovereignty, the general will, the state of exception) are transposed theological concepts. The sovereign — he who decides the exception — occupies the structural position of the omnipotent God. Liberalism is an attempt to run politics without this theological substrate. Schmitt thinks the attempt must fail.

See [political-theology](../concepts/political-theology.md).

### Sovereignty and the state of exception

> "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception."

The sovereign is defined not by routine governance but by the capacity to suspend the law when the situation requires — to decide that *this* is the emergency, *this* is the moment of crisis. This power is by definition extra-legal; no legal rule can specify in advance when to suspend the rules. Schmitt's point is not that emergencies should be invented but that the legal order always rests on a prior political decision that cannot itself be legally grounded.

### Critique of parliamentarism and liberal democracy

*The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923) argues that the justifications of 19th-century parliamentarism (open discussion, public reason) no longer describe 20th-century mass-party politics, which is manipulative and organized. Liberal democracy is thus in a legitimacy crisis that it disguises with procedural rituals.

This critique — unnervingly — has been adopted by both the post-war far right (who use it to discredit democracy) and by critical-theory left (who use it to critique actually-existing democracy while defending a more substantive version).

### The *Nomos* of the Earth

Schmitt's later geopolitical work (*The Nomos of the Earth*, 1950) argues that every legal order rests on a *nomos* — a fundamental spatial ordering of land and sea. The modern European *jus publicum Europaeum* rested on a distinctive such ordering (the sovereign territorial state in Europe, colonial space outside); its collapse in the 20th century is, for Schmitt, the crisis we are still inside.

## Influenced by

- [Thomas Hobbes](thomas-hobbes.md) — the central reference; sovereignty as answer to civil war
- Joseph de Maistre, Donoso Cortés — Catholic counter-revolutionary tradition
- Georges Sorel — myth as political force
- Max Weber — charisma, rationalization; Schmitt attended Weber's seminars
- Catholic political theology going back to Augustine
- [Hegel](gwf-hegel.md) — on the state; ambivalent debt
- Jean Bodin — early modern theorist of sovereignty

## Influenced

- [Leo Strauss](leo-strauss.md) — friendly critic; their 1932 exchange on *The Concept of the Political* is canonical
- [Walter Benjamin](walter-benjamin.md) — the Benjamin–Schmitt exchange runs through both thinkers' work; "state of exception" becomes central to Benjamin as well
- [Herbert Marcuse](herbert-marcuse.md), early Frankfurt School — took Schmitt seriously as the sharpest critic of liberalism from the right
- Giorgio Agamben — *Homo Sacer*, *State of Exception* — the most influential contemporary extension
- Chantal Mouffe — agonistic democracy as left Schmittianism
- [Thiel](peter-thiel.md) — via Strauss and directly
- [Yarvin](curtis-yarvin.md) — neoreactionary absorption of Schmittian sovereignty
- [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) — antagonistic influence; much of Habermas's mature work is designed to disprove Schmitt

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel

- Friend/enemy, the state of exception, and political theology are Thielian vocabulary, used in *The Straussian Moment* and in many talks.
- Schmitt's critique of liberalism dovetails with Thiel's own — though Thiel reads it through Girard and the Gospels, whereas Schmitt had no such Girardian redemption.
- Schmitt's geopolitics of land/sea, developed in *The Nomos of the Earth*, resonates with Thiel's recent framing of U.S.–China competition.

### To Karp

Karp's entire tradition — Habermas, Apel, the Frankfurt School heritage — is built against Schmitt. Habermas's *The Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (1962) is a genealogy of the bourgeois public sphere against Schmittian decisionism. Karp's commitment to the "Western project" is a commitment to exactly the liberal parliamentarism Schmitt diagnosed as impossible.

This is where the Thiel–Karp disagreement gets its sharpness: the two men are on opposite sides of a 20th-century argument that Schmitt both initiated and named.

---

## A caution

Schmitt's Nazism cannot be bracketed. Serious engagement with Schmitt requires holding both: the analytical acuity that even his antagonists acknowledge, and the political commitments he was willing to make (including the defense of the 1933 Enabling Act and, shamefully, antisemitic writings from the mid-1930s). The existence of left-Schmittians, right-Schmittians, and everything in between is possible because this acuity can be separated from the politics — but readers must always be alert to the joint.
