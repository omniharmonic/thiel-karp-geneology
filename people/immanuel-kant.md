# Immanuel Kant

**(1724–1804)**

Prussian philosopher. The central figure of the European Enlightenment and the upstream of virtually all modern critical and normative philosophy. A *shared* ancestor for both sides of this genealogy — but much more explicitly so for Karp's side.

## Core contributions

- **The Critical project:** *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781) argues that our cognition is structured by a priori forms of intuition (space, time) and categories of the understanding; we can know phenomena but not things-in-themselves.
- **Practical reason and autonomy:** *Critique of Practical Reason* (1788) grounds morality in the rational will's self-legislation. The categorical imperative: act only on maxims you can will as universal law; treat persons as ends, not merely as means.
- **Republican peace and cosmopolitanism:** *Perpetual Peace* (1795) argues that the federation of republican states under international law is the rational trajectory of political modernity.
- **Enlightenment as self-emancipation:** *What is Enlightenment?* (1784) — *Sapere aude*: dare to know; emergence from self-incurred minority.

## Influence on this vault

- **To Karp:** Kant is the foundation of the Habermasian discourse-ethics program. Karp's commitment to the "Western project" as a normative rather than merely geographical category is Kantian.
- **To Thiel:** Kant is less directly important; in the Straussian reading, Kant is the second-wave modern whose attempt to ground freedom in autonomous reason had to fail. But Kant's republican-peace frame is part of the broader liberal-democratic frame that Thiel's tradition both inherits and critiques.
