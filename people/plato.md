# Plato

**(c. 428–348 BCE)**

Athenian philosopher. Founder of the Academy. Author of the dialogues that are the foundation document of Western political philosophy. Deeply central to this vault via [Strauss](leo-strauss.md)'s recovery of classical political philosophy and [Bloom](allan-bloom.md)'s mid-century American transmission.

The [graph topology report](../synthesis/graph-topology-report.md) flagged Plato as a genuine orphan — under-connected given how foundational he is to the Thiel branch. This entry has been rewritten to address that gap.

## Core contributions (for the purposes of this vault)

### The tension between philosophy and the city

Socrates was tried and executed by Athens. Plato's dialogues return to this trauma repeatedly. The philosopher asks questions the political community cannot tolerate; the political community requires opinions the philosopher cannot share. This is not an incidental problem but, for Plato, the constitutive condition of philosophical life.

Strauss's entire program turns on this Platonic framing. See [esoteric writing](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md): the philosopher who wants to remain in the city must learn how to write in a way that transmits the teaching without provoking the city. The model is Plato himself, whose dialogues are (per Strauss) the great example of philosophical writing in a political-rhetorical register.

### The tripartite soul

*Republic* Book IV divides the soul into:

- **Reason** (*logistikon*) — the rational faculty that pursues truth
- **Appetite** (*epithymetikon*) — desires for food, drink, sex, wealth
- **Spirit** (*thymos*) — the seat of honor, indignation, the longing for recognition

The tripartite soul became, for [Fukuyama](francis-fukuyama.md) specifically, the psychological substrate of the Hegelian-Kojèvian recognition-struggle. The "thymos" chapter of *The End of History and the Last Man* is an extended Platonic reading of Hegel via Nietzsche. The Platonic soul is thus a direct ancestor of the Thiel-branch engagement with Fukuyama's thesis.

### The Socratic question

What is justice? What is the good? What is the best regime? Plato's dialogues refuse to close these questions — the *Republic* ends with Socrates's admission that the just city-in-speech may not exist anywhere on earth. This methodological openness is the Straussian model of philosophical inquiry, as against the modern model (from Descartes onward) of systematic construction.

### The doctrine of forms

The metaphysical claim that sensible particulars participate in immutable intelligible forms. This is the doctrine that most distinguishes Plato from modern philosophy and that Strauss insists must be read carefully, not dismissed.

## Key dialogues for this vault

- **[*Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*](../texts/strauss-persecution-and-art-of-writing.md)** — the trial and death of Socrates; the philosopher-vs-city question made concrete
- **The *Republic*** — the political-philosophical core; also Bloom's famous translation
- **The *Symposium* and *Phaedrus*** — on love and rhetoric; crucial for the Straussian reading
- **The *Laws*** — Plato's late, more pessimistic political work
- **The *Statesman* and *Theaetetus*** — on politics and knowledge

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

### To Thiel (via Strauss and Bloom)

- [Strauss](leo-strauss.md) treats Plato as a living interlocutor whose works must be read slowly, carefully, and esoterically
- [Bloom](allan-bloom.md) translated the *Republic* into the English that shaped American political-philosophy teaching; Bloom's extensive interpretive essay frames Plato for a generation
- [Straussianism](../schools/straussianism.md) as a school is, in important respects, a commitment to continuing Platonic philosophy under modern conditions
- [Fukuyama](francis-fukuyama.md)'s Plato (the tripartite soul, thymos) is the specific Plato Thiel engages via his reading of Fukuyama
- The Straussian Plato is inseparable from [the Strauss–Kojève debate](../texts/strauss-kojeve-debate.md), where Plato's presentation of Socratic philosophy stands against Kojève's Hegelian sage

### To Karp (mostly antagonistic)

- The Habermasian tradition reads Plato as the origin of a philosophy-of-consciousness paradigm that modernity finally transcends with the linguistic turn
- For [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md), Plato is the figure whose "doctrine of truth" (truth as correctness of representation) inaugurates the metaphysical tradition that forgets Being
- Both Heidegger's and Habermas's post-Platonic programs would be unintelligible without Plato as the figure they are working past

## Influenced by

- Socrates — his teacher; the dramatic protagonist of most dialogues
- The Pythagoreans — on number, form, and the soul
- Parmenides and Heraclitus — pre-Socratic predecessors
- The tradition of Athenian tragedy — Plato wrote dialogues partly as philosophical counter-performance to tragedy

## Influenced

- Virtually the entire subsequent Western philosophical tradition
- Aristotle, Plotinus, Augustine — most explicitly
- Medieval Islamic and Jewish political philosophy ([Maimonides](maimonides.md), Alfarabi) — whose readings of Plato Strauss later recovers
- [Strauss](leo-strauss.md) and the [Straussian tradition](../schools/straussianism.md) — direct philosophical heirs
- [Bloom](allan-bloom.md) and American Straussian teaching
- [Fukuyama](francis-fukuyama.md) — via Bloom
- [Girard](rene-girard.md), via his engagement with Plato on rivalry and mimesis (less central to Girard than Freud or the Gospels, but present)
- The modern opposition: [Heidegger](martin-heidegger.md), Nietzsche, Derrida — all defined themselves partly against Plato
