# Max Weber

**(1864–1920)**

German sociologist, jurist, political economist. One of the founders of sociology as a discipline. Weber's vocabulary — rationalization, the Protestant ethic, charisma, the iron cage, instrumental (*zweckrational*) vs. value-rational (*wertrational*) action, the monopoly of legitimate violence — has permeated 20th-century social theory, including both the Frankfurt School and the Schmittian political theology tradition.

## Core contributions

- **Rationalization and disenchantment:** modernity is defined by the progressive rationalization of all spheres of life, with a corresponding disenchantment (*Entzauberung*) of the world. Religion becomes private; meaning becomes a personal choice rather than a public given.
- ***The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*** (1905): ascetic Protestantism's inner-worldly discipline gave capitalism its characteristic spirit.
- **Authority types:** traditional, charismatic, legal-rational.
- **Instrumental vs. value rationality:** modernity privileges the former; the latter retreats into private conscience.
- **The state:** the political community that successfully claims the monopoly of legitimate physical violence in a given territory.

## Influence on this vault

- **To Karp via Habermas:** Weber's rationalization thesis is the backdrop of the *Theory of Communicative Action*. Habermas argues modernity's pathology is not rationalization as such but the *colonization of the life-world by systems rationality*.
- **To [Schmitt](carl-schmitt.md):** attended Weber's seminars; Schmitt's charismatic-sovereign theory is in dialogue with Weber's charisma.
- **To Adorno and Horkheimer:** *Dialectic of Enlightenment* generalizes Weber's rationalization into a civilizational diagnosis.
- **To Thiel via Girard:** Weber is part of the background of modernity-as-disenchantment that Girard reads through the Gospel's unmasking of the sacred.
