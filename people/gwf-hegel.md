# Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel

**(1770–1831)**

German philosopher. Along with Kant, the central figure of German idealism and arguably the most influential philosopher of the 19th century. The third of the three [shared upstream ancestors](../synthesis/shared-upstream-ancestors.md) Thiel and Karp have in common — both via [Kojève](alexandre-kojeve.md)'s reading (to Girard and thus Thiel) and via Marx/Frankfurt School (to Habermas and thus Karp).

---

## Biographical sketch

Born in Stuttgart. Friend and roommate of Hölderlin and Schelling at the Tübingen Stift. Private tutor in Switzerland and Frankfurt. *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) in Jena, supposedly finished as Napoleon's troops approached. Newspaper editor in Bamberg. Headmaster of a secondary school in Nuremberg. Professor at Heidelberg, then Berlin (1818), where he dominated German philosophy until his death in a cholera epidemic.

## Core contributions

### The dialectic

Hegel's signature philosophical method, often summarized as *thesis – antithesis – synthesis* (though Hegel himself rarely used these words). Reality, thought, and history proceed through the self-overcoming of contradictions: every stable position generates its negation, and the tension between them is resolved in a higher stage that preserves and cancels both. *Aufhebung* — sublation — is the key word.

### The *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807)

A philosophical odyssey that traces the development of consciousness from naive sense-certainty through increasingly sophisticated shapes (perception, self-consciousness, reason, spirit, religion, absolute knowing). The [master–slave dialectic](../concepts/master-slave-dialectic.md) in Chapter IV is probably the most famous passage in 19th-century philosophy — and the key via Kojève to a dozen 20th-century thinkers.

### Philosophy of history

History is the self-realization of spirit (*Geist*) as freedom. From Oriental despotism (one is free) to Greco-Roman citizenship (some are free) to Germanic Christianity (all are free in principle), history unfolds the progressive recognition of human freedom in institutions. The modern state — properly understood — is the concrete achievement of this freedom in ethical life (*Sittlichkeit*).

### *Elements of the Philosophy of Right* (1820)

Hegel's political philosophy. The rational actualization of freedom proceeds from abstract right (persons, property) through morality (the internal standpoint) to ethical life, which has three moments: family, civil society, and the state. Civil society — the sphere of market, labor, voluntary association — is recognized as modernity's achievement, but incapable on its own of resolving the contradictions it generates. Only the state, as concrete universal, can do that.

### The end of history

Hegel's claim, controversial and often mis-stated: history as the struggle for recognition of freedom has, in principle, reached its conclusion with the recognition that all humans are free as such. What remains is the unfolding and actualization of this recognition in concrete institutions. This claim will be picked up and radicalized by Kojève, and via Kojève shape both Fukuyama and the broader post-1989 "end of history" debate.

- *Phenomenology of Spirit* (*Phänomenologie des Geistes*, 1807)

## Influenced by

- Kant — the transcendental frame Hegel radicalizes and historicizes
- Fichte, Schelling — contemporaries and interlocutors in German idealism
- Aristotle — on teleology and on ethical life
- The French Revolution — as the event that proved abstract freedom insufficient without institutional form
- Protestant Christianity — secularized into the philosophy of spirit

## Influenced

- [Marx](karl-marx.md) — the "left Hegelians" who inverted Hegel's idealism into historical materialism
- [Kojève](alexandre-kojeve.md) — the 20th-century's most influential reader of the master–slave dialectic
- [Girard](rene-girard.md) — via Kojève; the structure of mimetic rivalry has Hegelian bones
- [Adorno](theodor-adorno.md) — *Negative Dialectics* is an immanent critique of Hegel
- [Habermas](jurgen-habermas.md) — deep inheritance of dialectical thinking about modernity
- Francis Fukuyama — *The End of History* as Kojèvian Hegelianism
- [Strauss](leo-strauss.md) — polemically: Hegel as the zenith of modernity's mistake

## Relevance to Thiel / Karp

The master–slave dialectic is where Hegel enters both men's genealogies most powerfully. Girard's mimetic rivalry is, in part, Hegel's recognition-struggle read through the lens of desire. Habermas's communicative action is, in part, Hegel's ethical life re-grounded in language. Kojève's reading of Hegel's end of history shapes the Fukuyama-style post-Cold-War triumphalism that Thiel partly endorsed and partly rejected.

See [master-slave-dialectic](../concepts/master-slave-dialectic.md) for the concept in detail.
