# Stanford as Meeting Point

*Essay 4 of 5.*

---

## What was in the water, 1988–1992

Both [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) and [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) moved through Stanford in a window — roughly 1985–1992, with Stanford Law focused in 1989–1992 — when several things were true of the place that would not be true a decade later.

### The Girardian presence

[René Girard](../people/rene-girard.md) joined Stanford in 1981 and was at the peak of his late American reception. His seminars drew serious undergraduates across disciplines, including Thiel, who took Girard's courses during his 1985–1989 undergraduate years. Robert Hamerton-Kelly, the Stanford Methodist minister, was running Girardian seminars in which Thiel would later deliver *The Straussian Moment* (2004). The infrastructure for Girardian thought — later formalized by Thiel's 2007 funding of Imitatio — was already gathering at Stanford.

### The Hoover conservative scene

The [Hoover Institution](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md), as Stanford's right-of-center think tank, was in a relatively muscular period. [Milton Friedman](../people/milton-friedman.md) was a Senior Research Fellow. The institutional network connecting Stanford conservatives to the American Right's broader infrastructure was active.

Thiel founded *The Stanford Review* in 1987 inside this milieu. The publication was conceived explicitly as a conservative counter-weight to what the founders saw as a progressive orthodoxy in the mainstream campus culture — a stance directly legible within [Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md)'s *Closing of the American Mind* (1987), published in Thiel's first Stanford year.

### The Western Civ controversy

Stanford was in the middle of a famous curricular dispute: whether to replace the mandatory Western Civilization course ("Western Culture") with a broader multicultural survey ("Cultures, Ideas, and Values"). The fight was resolved in favor of the broader survey in 1988. The *Stanford Review* was on the losing side; this radicalized the conservatives.

This controversy is the specific political-intellectual event that shaped Thiel's undergraduate sensibility. The "West" as a contested category, not a taken-for-granted one, is a Stanford-shaped frame that Thiel has carried forward — and that appears, oddly rephrased, in Karp's much later *The Technological Republic*.

### Law school's law-and-economics moment

Stanford Law in the late 1980s and early 1990s was absorbing the law-and-economics movement centered at Chicago and Yale — a Posner-Calabresi-Epstein-shaped intellectual reshaping of American legal education. This was the broader neoliberal-adjacent intellectual climate in which Thiel and Karp met.

### The PayPal cohort in seed

The *Stanford Review* alumni network that Thiel founded produced, over the subsequent decade, an extraordinary list of Silicon Valley figures (David Sacks, Keith Rabois, Reid Hoffman via Thiel adjacency though not the Review directly, Max Levchin as PayPal co-founder). The intellectual-political coherence of what became the "PayPal Mafia" was seeded in this Stanford conservative-libertarian milieu.

## Why it matters for the Karp relationship

Karp arrived at Stanford Law from Haverford, philosophically different from Thiel but intellectually serious in a way that made Thiel recognizable to him and vice versa. The law school's Socratic teaching style privileges argument. Two students equally committed to arguing well are likely to find each other; they are especially likely to find each other if they disagree substantively.

Karp's German years (doctoral studies at Frankfurt, 1990s) came after Stanford. At Stanford itself, Karp was already intellectually engaged with American political philosophy but had not yet been formally disciplined in Habermasian critical theory. The deep Karp–Habermas relationship was still in front of him.

This means Stanford was the meeting of *pre-crystallized* versions of each man's philosophy. Each was still in formation. Each could see where the other was heading. Each could find the other interesting precisely because the trajectories were about to diverge.

## What Stanford didn't do

Stanford Law did not produce Palantir. Thiel and Karp went separate ways after graduating in 1992 — Thiel to securities law, derivatives trading, and ultimately PayPal; Karp to Germany and his PhD, then to London as an investor. The Palantir founding was in 2003, eleven years later.

What Stanford did was create the friendship. The friendship, in turn, created the possibility that when Thiel needed a CEO for a company whose philosophical ambitions would be unusual, he could call someone he actually trusted to argue with him.

## Afterlives

Stanford's post-2015 relationship to this lineage has been complicated. The university is now routinely cited by Thiel and Thiel-adjacent voices as a paradigmatic case of the "Cathedral" (Yarvin) or the administered-progressive campus (Bloomian Straussian critique). Thiel's own institutional drift has been away from Stanford toward his own ecosystem (Thiel Fellowship, Founders Fund, the Hoover Review) and toward Catholic-intellectual institutions (First Things, the Edmund Burke Foundation). Karp, by contrast, remains a Stanford Law alumnus with no comparably visible alienation from the institution.

This is one more index of where the two men's trajectories have gone since their shared early-1990s moment.
