# Political Theology (the concept)

The concept, distinct from the broader discipline traced in [vault/research/thiel-karp-genealogy/schools/political-theology](../schools/political-theology.md).

## The Schmitt formulation

From [Carl Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md), *Politische Theologie* (1922):

> "All significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are secularized theological concepts."

The thesis has three nested levels:

1. **Historical.** Modern political concepts (sovereignty, the general will, popular legitimacy) are historical continuations of earlier theological concepts (divine omnipotence, providence, divine right). This is an empirical-genealogical claim.
2. **Structural.** These modern concepts retain the structural form of their theological origins even when the content has been secularized. The sovereign (he who decides on the [exception](friend-enemy-distinction.md)) functions structurally as the omnipotent God of nominalist theology.
3. **Normative.** Modernity's attempt to run politics without acknowledging its theological substrate fails. The attempt is crypto-theological rather than post-theological, and its repressed premises return in pathological forms.

## Why the concept matters

Political theology names the problem modern liberalism refuses. Liberalism's constitutive move is to privatize religion and rationalize politics — to make it possible for a plurality of faiths to live under shared institutions by removing the theological premises. Schmitt argues this is not only historically inadequate (those premises remain embedded in the institutions' structure) but politically dangerous (unacknowledged theology is unaccountable theology).

## The concept at the left / right fork

- **On the right** (Schmitt, neoreaction, integralists): political theology is diagnostic *and* prescriptive. The theological substrate should be reacknowledged; politics should be re-theologized.
- **On the left** (Agamben, Taubes, Hardt & Negri): political theology is diagnostic but not prescriptive. The goal is to break the spell, not to return to a pre-modern political form. Understanding how theological structures operate in secular politics helps us identify the hidden authoritarian tendencies of liberalism without endorsing their theological grounding.
- **On the Habermasian middle**: the challenge of political theology is the challenge of *post-metaphysical normativity*. Can we ground political legitimacy without theological reference? Habermas's *Between Facts and Norms* tries to say yes — popular sovereignty and human rights are co-original in the structure of communicative action, and need no further metaphysical support.

## Relevance to this vault

The concept is the specific axis on which Thiel's and Karp's traditions most directly confront each other:

- **Thiel** thinks political theology is inescapable; the liberal attempt to bracket it has failed; we need to reacknowledge theological premises (though in his Girardian reading, not in Schmitt's Catholic one). This is the explicit thesis of *The Straussian Moment*.
- **Karp** thinks Habermasian post-metaphysical legitimation is the path forward; Palantir's civic-defense work is the defense of exactly this Enlightenment project. Schmittian political theology is the opposite of what Karp believes.

Palantir, unusually, contains both answers in its senior leadership.
