# Will to Power

[Nietzsche](../people/friedrich-nietzsche.md)'s central metaphysical hypothesis. Scattered across his mature works (from *Zarathustra* through *Beyond Good and Evil* and the *Genealogy*), articulated in unfinished form in the notebooks posthumously assembled (unreliably, by his sister) as *The Will to Power*.

## The claim

The fundamental drive of all living things is not self-preservation (Darwin), pleasure (utilitarians), or Schopenhauerian suffering-reduction, but the striving for greater power, expansion, and self-overcoming. Every instinct, value, interpretation, institution, and morality is a crystallization of will to power.

Nietzsche offers this as a *hypothesis*, not a dogma — but he takes it seriously as a candidate unified explanation.

## What "power" means here

Critical to Nietzsche's use: *Macht* (power) is not primarily political dominion or military force. It is the capacity to affirm, to interpret, to create, to organize oneself against resistance. A scholar's will to power is expressed in the capacity to hold contradictions and organize thought; an artist's in composition; a religious founder's in the creation of a new evaluation. The conquering general is one case among many, not the paradigmatic case.

Slave morality is itself a will to power — the resentful will to power of those who cannot win directly and so turn the terms of evaluation against their masters.

## Three common misreadings

1. **Will to power = desire for political dominance.** This is the fascist reading, encouraged by Nietzsche's sister's editorial work and by Nazi appropriations. Careful readers since Walter Kaufmann have shown this is indefensible as a reading of Nietzsche.
2. **Will to power = Schopenhauerian will to live.** Nietzsche's whole point is to break from Schopenhauer here.
3. **Will to power = motive of all action.** Nietzsche is not offering a psychological egoism; he is offering an ontological hypothesis about what kinds of forces constitute reality.

## The Übermensch and eternal recurrence

The will to power's highest expression is the Übermensch (overman) — the type capable of self-overcoming, capable of *creating* values (not merely discovering or obeying them), capable of affirming existence so thoroughly that one could will its eternal recurrence.

## Relevance to this vault

- **[Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)** on monopoly reads as a popular-register will-to-power. *Zero to One*'s thesis (create something that did not exist; refuse to participate in mere competition) is Nietzsche-shaped: creation over imitation, value-setting over value-obeying. Thiel has, however, been careful to distance himself from the vulgar Nietzschean posture; his Girardian Christianity wants to redirect the will to power into non-rivalrous creation rather than endorse the aristocratic politics.
- **[Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s** tradition regards the will-to-power hypothesis as a diagnostic tool (the [dialectic of Enlightenment](dialectic-of-enlightenment.md) uses it, as does Adorno's *Minima Moralia*) rather than as a prescriptive anthropology. Habermas's move: replace the Nietzschean will-to-power with communicative rationality as the deep structure of human sociality.
