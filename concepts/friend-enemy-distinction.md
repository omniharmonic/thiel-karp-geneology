# Friend / Enemy Distinction

[Carl Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md)'s definition of the political, from *The Concept of the Political* (1927/1932).

> "The specific political distinction to which political actions and motives can be reduced is that between friend and enemy."

## What the distinction claims

- **The political is its own domain.** It is not reducible to morality, economics, or aesthetics. Each sphere has its own fundamental distinction: good/evil (morality), beautiful/ugly (aesthetics), profitable/unprofitable (economics), friend/enemy (politics).
- **The enemy is not the criminal, the competitor, or the disagreeable.** The criminal is internal to the political community; the competitor is in the economic sphere; the disagreeable is an aesthetic judgment. The enemy is the *other collectivity* against whom one may, at the limit, need to fight.
- **The enemy is existential, not moral.** The enemy is not necessarily evil or ugly; they are simply *other*, and at the limit, incompatible. Schmitt's enemy is the public enemy (*hostis*), not the private enemy (*inimicus*); the Christian commandment to love one's *inimicus* does not, for Schmitt, abolish the political *hostis*.
- **The political persists.** Any attempt to dissolve the political — reducing everything to market competition (liberalism) or moral judgment (humanitarianism) — fails. The political distinction returns. A community that refuses to name its enemies has been named by them.

## Why it is dangerous

Schmitt's distinction has been used to authorize actual violence — Schmitt's own support for the Nazi state being the historical test case. The distinction can slide easily from "a real political community may at the limit confront an enemy" to "we must identify enemies to be authentically political," which in turn slides to "these are our enemies, and everything follows."

Habermas, Arendt, and others have spent careers showing why this slide is not accidental but structural: the friend/enemy distinction does not have internal resources for self-limitation.

## Left appropriation

Notwithstanding its origin, the distinction has been taken up on the left as diagnostic. Chantal Mouffe's *agonistic democracy* accepts the inescapability of political conflict but tries to translate *antagonism* (friend/enemy, war-logic) into *agonism* (political rivals who accept each other's legitimacy).

## Relevance to this vault

- **[Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s *Straussian Moment*** uses the friend/enemy distinction as diagnostic: post-9/11 America had forgotten how to name its enemy, and therefore could not effectively confront what had happened. Thiel's Tolkien-inflected corporate mythology (defending civilization against Sauronic forces) is friend/enemy in mythopoetic register.
- **[Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s tradition** is built to refuse the friend/enemy framing without denying political conflict. Habermas's discourse ethics: conflicts are real, but they must be settled by reasons rather than by the existential decision. Karp's recent hawkishness about China and Russia is complicated on Habermasian grounds — a difficulty he seems to navigate by invoking the normative Western project rather than Schmittian enmity.
