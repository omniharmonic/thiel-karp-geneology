# Mimetic Theory

[René Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)'s unified anthropological hypothesis. The name marks a tradition of three nested claims, each radicalizing the last.

## 1. Mimetic desire

Humans do not desire objects directly. Beyond basic needs, human desire is *triangular*: subject – model – object. We learn what to want by watching whom we admire (the model) and copying their desire. This is true even when we mythologize desire as spontaneous and autonomous — in fact especially then.

Consequences:

- **Identity is borrowed.** Your taste in music, politics, career, partners — all are first crystallized through models, even as they come to feel like expressions of an authentic self.
- **Rivalry is inevitable.** If you and I share a model, we converge on the same object and, mediated by each other, become rivals. The rival is not a contingent obstacle but a structural consequence of shared desire.
- **Reciprocity generalizes.** Two mutually-fixated rivals become each other's models; desire becomes self-reinforcing; the object itself may become secondary to the rivalry.

## 2. Mimetic crisis

When mimetic rivalry spreads through a community, social differentiation breaks down. The crisis state is one of undifferentiation — everyone wants the same things, feels the same resentments, becomes each other's double. The community is dangerously close to war of all against all.

## 3. The [scapegoat mechanism](scapegoat-mechanism.md)

The mimetic crisis is resolved — not through rational decision, but through a spontaneous convergence of violence on an arbitrary victim. See that note for the full account.

## Relevance to this vault

Via [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md):

- **Business strategy.** *Zero to One*'s central claim — that competition is destructive mimesis and that monopoly (creation of something genuinely new) is the only source of value — is mimetic theory operationalized. Two founders racing each other are mimetic rivals wasting the energy that could produce something that didn't exist.
- **Social media.** Thiel reads platforms as mimetic amplifiers: each user is constantly exposed to others' desires, and the machine turns the amplification into profit. Contagion in the literal Girardian sense.
- **Politics.** Thiel reads contemporary partisan mobilization as a mimetic crisis — undifferentiated rivalry whose only resolution is scapegoating.

## Critiques

- **Universalist overreach.** Mimetic theory claims very large explanatory scope. Critics ask whether all desire is really mimetic, or whether some desires (hunger, pain-avoidance, curiosity) are better explained on other grounds.
- **The Christian reading is strong.** Many secular readers admire mimetic theory while declining to follow Girard into the thesis that the Gospels uniquely unmask sacred violence.
- **Application is risky.** Mimetic theory is an anthropology, not a handbook. Using it to generate strategic advice (as Thiel does) tends to flatten the analysis toward instrumentality.
