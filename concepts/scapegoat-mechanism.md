# The Scapegoat Mechanism

The structural resolution of a [mimetic crisis](mimetic-theory.md) in [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)'s anthropology. The mechanism has six elements:

1. **Undifferentiation.** Mimetic rivalry reaches a point where distinctions collapse; all are each other's doubles; violence is imminent and unmanageable.
2. **Polarization onto a victim.** Spontaneously — not by conscious choice — collective attention converges on one figure, often arbitrary or marginal (a stranger, a disabled person, a disgraced leader, a foreigner, a witch).
3. **Unanimity.** The whole community turns against the victim. The signal that the mechanism is working is that opposition disappears.
4. **Violence and expulsion.** The victim is killed, exiled, or symbolically destroyed.
5. **Peace returns.** The mimetic energy spent on the victim produces a miraculous calm. The community, having united in violence, now discovers it has a future.
6. **Retroactive sacralization.** The victim is misread as the *cause* of the crisis and as the source of the peace — hence demonic and divine at once. Religion grows up to repeat, control, and conceal the mechanism: ritual (controlled re-enactment), myth (official version that hides the arbitrariness), prohibition (rules to prevent recurrence).

## The Girardian claim: culture is built on this lie

Per Girard, every archaic culture begins with a founding murder and builds itself on the concealment of the victim's innocence. This is what the mechanism *does*: it generates community through unanimous violence and then disguises what it did.

## The Judeo-Christian revelation

Girard's distinctive theological claim: the Hebrew Bible and the Gospels are the texts in which the scapegoat mechanism is progressively shown from the victim's side, and thereby dismantled. Abel, Job, the Psalms of the persecuted, the Suffering Servant, and finally the Passion narrative all expose the innocence of the victim and the arbitrariness of the violence. Once this revelation is in the cultural bloodstream, scapegoating still works — but it works less well, and with more shame.

The modern concern for victims, even when itself turned scapegoating, is for Girard a Gospel artifact. A civilization that has been through the Gospel cannot entirely unsee what it has seen.

## Relevance to this vault

Via [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md):

- **Contemporary politics.** Thiel reads cancellation waves, social media pile-ons, and partisan mobilization as scapegoat mechanisms. The pattern is the same: undifferentiated rivalry → convergence on victim → unanimity → expulsion → community temporarily re-coheres.
- **The "Woke" as religion.** Thiel's recurring claim that progressive movements operate as a religion specifically derives from the Girardian frame: any belief system whose function is to identify and expel victims is structurally religious.
- **Christianity.** Thiel's own faith is explicitly Girardian — he treats the Cross as the *exposure* of scapegoating, not its continuation.

## Tensions

A recurring question in the Girardian school: does the exposure of scapegoating in the Gospels make the mechanism obsolete, or merely make it more perverse? Girard's late work (*Battling to the End*) argues that exposure without healing makes violence more dangerous, not less — a claim Thiel echoes when he describes modernity as apocalyptic.
