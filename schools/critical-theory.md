# Critical Theory

The tradition of social-philosophical analysis that aims at emancipatory diagnosis of society's self-imposed constraints. As a specific research program, the term derives from [Max Horkheimer](../people/max-horkheimer.md)'s 1937 essay *Traditional and Critical Theory*, which distinguished it from the detached-observer posture of "traditional" theoretical science.

Narrowly: critical theory = the [Frankfurt School](frankfurt-school.md) research program.

Broadly: any theoretical work that (a) takes its own social embedding seriously, (b) analyzes domination and distortion, and (c) orients itself toward emancipation.

In this vault, both senses matter. [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) was trained in the narrow-sense tradition. Contemporary "critical theory" as used in American political debate (usually as an object of critique) is closer to the broad sense.

## Horkheimer's distinction (1937)

- **Traditional theory:** positions the theorist as disembodied observer describing a world. Models itself on the natural sciences. Aims at prediction and control.
- **Critical theory:** acknowledges that the theorist is socially located, interested, historical. The theorist does not merely describe but diagnoses, and the diagnosis is itself a moment of the practice that could transform the situation.

The critical-theoretical move is thus always double: it describes the target (institution, ideology, form of life) *and* it describes the theoretical work itself as positioned, as taking a side, as part of the practice of emancipation.

## Core concerns (narrow sense, Frankfurt lineage)

- **Ideology critique** — the ways that dominant forms of thought naturalize unjust arrangements
- **Reification** — the process (from Lukács via Marx) by which social relations appear as relations between things
- **The [dialectic of Enlightenment](../concepts/dialectic-of-enlightenment.md)** — the way critical reason can produce new forms of domination
- **The [culture industry](../concepts/culture-industry.md)** — how mass entertainment pacifies and integrates
- **The [colonization of the life-world](../concepts/communicative-action.md)** (Habermas) — how systems rationality invades communicative spheres
- **Recognition** (Honneth) — how misrecognition structures injustice

## Core concerns (broad sense, extended tradition)

The broader reception of critical theory in American universities (usually mediated through French post-structuralism rather than German Frankfurt School) includes:

- **Post-colonial theory** — Said, Spivak, Bhabha
- **Feminist theory** — especially poststructuralist-inflected: Butler, Haraway
- **Critical race theory** — Kimberlé Crenshaw, Derrick Bell
- **Queer theory** — Sedgwick, Berlant, Edelman

These traditions have shared commitments with Frankfurt critical theory (social-locatedness of knowledge; diagnosis of domination; emancipatory orientation) but also significant differences in philosophical substrate (Foucault, Derrida, Deleuze rather than Hegel-Marx-Freud).

The conflation of all of the above into a single "critical theory" in contemporary American political polemic — and particularly the polemical attribution of specific campus phenomena to "critical race theory" regardless of whether the targets actually use the CRT framework — obscures this history.

## Relevance to this vault

- [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) is formally a critical theorist in the strict Frankfurt sense. His dissertation is a critical-theoretical work.
- [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) has made critique of "wokeness" and related phenomena a recurring public theme. To the extent that these phenomena have any connection to the critical-theoretical tradition, it runs mostly through the broad-sense poststructuralist inheritance rather than through the Frankfurt School directly.

The ironic result: Thiel critiques contemporary American cultural politics while being in a business partnership with a man who is, by formal training, one of the few American CEOs qualified to give a Frankfurt School critique of that same American cultural politics. The structures of the critiques would be related but not identical.
