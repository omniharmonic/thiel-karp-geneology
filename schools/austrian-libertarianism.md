# Austrian Libertarianism

The intellectual tradition of classical-liberal political economy running from the Austrian school of economics (Menger, Böhm-Bawerk, Mises, [Hayek](../people/friedrich-hayek.md)) and, in a related but distinct lineage, the Chicago school ([Friedman](../people/milton-friedman.md), Stigler, Becker). This is the economic substrate of the libertarian politics [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) was formed inside as an undergraduate.

## The Austrian school

Founded by Carl Menger at the University of Vienna (1871) with the marginal-utility theory of value — a breakthrough that unseated the labor theory of value and reorganized economic theory around subjective valuation.

Key figures:

- **Carl Menger** (1840–1921) — founder
- **Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk** (1851–1914) — theorist of capital and interest
- **Ludwig von Mises** (1881–1973) — *Human Action*; the socialist calculation debate
- **[F.A. Hayek](../people/friedrich-hayek.md)** (1899–1992) — the most influential; knowledge problem; spontaneous order
- **Murray Rothbard** (1926–1995) — anarcho-capitalism
- **Israel Kirzner** — entrepreneurship theory
- **Hans-Hermann Hoppe** — more recent; deeply anti-democratic

### Core Austrian commitments

- **Methodological individualism** — economic facts are the aggregate of individual choices
- **Subjective theory of value** — value is not intrinsic but arises from preferences
- **Knowledge problem** — economic planning can't work because relevant knowledge is dispersed
- **Spontaneous order** — complex institutions evolve without design
- **Hostility to econometrics** — Austrian economics is verbal-logical, not mathematical
- **Business cycle theory** — cycles are driven by malinvestment induced by credit expansion

## The Chicago school

Parallel but distinct. More mathematically formal, less Viennese in sensibility, more engaged with American policy.

Key figures:

- **Frank Knight** — early Chicago
- **[Milton Friedman](../people/milton-friedman.md)** — monetarism, positive economics
- **George Stigler** — regulatory capture, information economics
- **Gary Becker** — economics of human behavior
- **Robert Lucas** — rational expectations
- **Eugene Fama** — efficient markets

### Chicago commitments

- **Price theory** — markets clear via prices
- **Empirical economics** — positive economics via predictive success
- **Skepticism of regulation** — most regulation helps incumbents
- **Monetary policy primacy** — inflation is a monetary phenomenon

## Libertarianism as political program

The Mont Pelerin Society (1947, founded by Hayek) is the institutional nexus that linked Austrian and Chicago economists to classical-liberal political philosophy (Popper, Karl Raimund, Robert Nozick, James Buchanan).

American libertarian institutions:

- **Cato Institute** (1977) — policy
- **Reason magazine** (1968) — journalism
- **Mises Institute** (1982) — more hard-line, Rothbardian
- **Hoover Institution** at Stanford — more classical-liberal than libertarian proper, but an important West-Coast hub
- **[Ayn Rand](../people/ayn-rand.md)** and the Ayn Rand Institute — adjacent but not quite the same tradition; Objectivism claims Aristotelian rather than Austrian grounding

## Relevance to Thiel

This is the intellectual substrate of Thiel's undergraduate libertarianism. *The Stanford Review* (founded 1987) was a product of this milieu. Thiel's early political essays were libertarian in the Cato/Reason register.

Thiel has since moved in more conservative-nationalist directions — his 2009 Cato essay ("I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible") marks the beginning of this drift — but Austrian-Chicago commitments persist in his thinking about startups, monopolies, regulation, and monetary policy.

The PayPal origin story has a genuinely libertarian element: the founders' thesis was to create a global internet-based currency outside state control, a proto-crypto vision.

## Relevance to Karp

Minimal. Karp's Frankfurt School formation would read Austrian libertarianism as a sophisticated ideological cover for the extension of market rationality into spheres of life where it does not belong — the very phenomenon Habermas's *Theory of Communicative Action* diagnoses as the colonization of the life-world.
