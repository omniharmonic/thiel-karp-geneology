# Straussianism

The tradition of political philosophy and classical scholarship growing out of [Leo Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md)'s teaching at the University of Chicago (1949–1968). One of the most distinctive and influential traditions in 20th-century American political thought, and the intellectual substrate of the milieu in which [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) was formed.

## Core commitments

- **Return to classical political philosophy** — Plato, Aristotle, Thucydides, Xenophon — as living interlocutors.
- **Critique of modernity as three waves** ([Machiavelli](../people/niccolo-machiavelli.md)/[Hobbes](../people/thomas-hobbes.md) → Rousseau/Kant → [Nietzsche](../people/friedrich-nietzsche.md)/[Heidegger](../people/martin-heidegger.md)).
- **The theological-political problem** — the irreducible tension between Jerusalem (revelation) and Athens (philosophy).
- **[Esoteric reading](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md)** — the hermeneutical claim that great philosophers wrote in a double register.
- **The virtue of the gentleman and the philosopher** — recovery of pre-modern ethical categories.
- **Anti-historicism** — rejection of the view that philosophy can be fully explained by its historical context.

## Internal divisions

### East Coast Straussians

The older, more academic, more textually austere wing. Centered on the East Coast universities and the New School. Focus: careful classical scholarship, philosophical reading of the tradition, relative quietism about contemporary politics.

Key figures: Seth Benardete, Stanley Rosen, Harry Neumann, Joseph Cropsey.

### West Coast Straussians

Politically engaged, more explicitly attached to the American founding as philosophical achievement, more willing to participate in public and political life. Centered at Claremont Graduate School.

Key figures: Harry V. Jaffa, Charles Kesler. The *Claremont Review of Books* is their flagship.

### Chicago Straussians

[Allan Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md), Harvey Mansfield (at Harvard, but Chicago-trained), Leon Kass. Mid-way between East Coast scholarship and West Coast engagement.

### Neoconservatives

A contested lineage: some neoconservatives (Irving Kristol, William Kristol, Paul Wolfowitz, Francis Fukuyama) were Straussian students; others are neoconservatives with only loose Straussian affiliation (Norman Podhoretz). The conflation of Straussianism with American neoconservatism in popular writing is partial at best: many Straussians have repudiated the Iraq War, and the Straussian theory of politics is only loosely connected to the neocon policy program.

## Distinctive practices

- **Close textual reading** — long, careful commentaries on classical texts
- **Lecture-and-seminar teaching** — the Straussian classroom, often remembered vividly by former students
- **Translation projects** — Bloom's *Republic*, Mansfield's *Machiavelli*, Strauss's and Cropsey's *History of Political Philosophy*
- **Skepticism toward social science** — political philosophy as distinct from and prior to empirical political science

## Relevance to this vault

- **To Thiel:** via Allan Bloom and the Hoover Institution scene at Stanford, Thiel encountered Strauss as an undergraduate. *The Straussian Moment* (2004) is Thiel's most explicit Straussian essay. Thiel's mode of public writing — cryptic, compressed, assuming sophisticated readers — is Straussian in structure.
- **To Karp:** via negation. Habermas's defense of Enlightenment modernity is the precise opposite of the Straussian critique of it. Karp's philosophical formation equipped him to recognize and respond to Straussian arguments — which may be one of the reasons his friendship with Thiel works: each man has a formed opinion on what the other's tradition actually claims.
