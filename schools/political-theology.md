# Political Theology

The tradition — both intellectual discipline and polemical concept — concerned with the ways that modern political concepts are secularized theological concepts, and with the question of what a politics informed by theological rather than purely secular reasons would look like. See also the concept note [political-theology](../concepts/political-theology.md).

## The Schmitt coinage

The phrase as a modern analytical category comes from [Carl Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md)'s *Politische Theologie* (1922), which opened with the famous thesis: "All significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are secularized theological concepts." The sovereign (he who decides on the exception) occupies the structural position of the omnipotent God; general will inherits from providence; popular sovereignty inherits from divine sovereignty.

Schmitt's claim was genealogical and polemical at once: modern liberalism is not *post-theological* but *crypto-theological*, and its attempt to run politics without acknowledging theological premises is both intellectually dishonest and politically unstable.

## Pre-Schmitt currents

The concept has antecedents. In the medieval period, the canonical reference is **Ernst Kantorowicz**'s *The King's Two Bodies* (1957), which traced the medieval theological-legal doctrine of the king's sacred body into modern constitutional theory. Earlier still, the 19th-century Catholic counter-revolutionary tradition — Joseph de Maistre, Donoso Cortés — worked the theological-political seam from the right.

## Post-Schmitt: the left appropriation

Schmitt's political theology has been appropriated by theorists on the left as a diagnostic of liberal democracy's blind spots:

- [Walter Benjamin](../people/walter-benjamin.md), *Critique of Violence* and related essays
- **Giorgio Agamben**, *Homo Sacer* series and *The Kingdom and the Glory* (2007) — the most systematic contemporary extension
- **Jacob Taubes**, *The Political Theology of Paul* (1993)
- **Eric Santner**, *The Royal Remains* (2011)
- **Chantal Mouffe**, agonistic democracy
- **Michael Hardt and Antonio Negri**, *Empire* (2000)

## Post-Schmitt: the right appropriation

The traditional-conservative and religious-right reception of Schmitt is more straightforward: his diagnosis of liberalism is accepted as correct, and his call for sovereign decision as the ground of political legitimacy is affirmed.

- Contemporary "common good constitutionalism" (Adrian Vermeule, Harvard Law) has political-theological tendencies
- The traditionalist-Catholic right (First Things, Patrick Deneen) operates in this register
- [Neoreaction](neoreaction.md) ([Yarvin](../people/curtis-yarvin.md), [Land](../people/nick-land.md)) is political theology minus the Catholicism
- [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s *The Straussian Moment* reads Schmitt through a Girardian lens

## Christian political theology more broadly

Adjacent to the Schmitt-inflected analytical tradition is the broader Christian political theology of 20th-century theologians:

- **Karl Barth** (Reformed; anti-Nazi, wrote the Barmen Declaration)
- **Dietrich Bonhoeffer** (Lutheran martyr)
- **Henri de Lubac**, **Hans Urs von Balthasar** (Catholic *nouvelle théologie*)
- **Jürgen Moltmann** (theology of hope)
- **Stanley Hauerwas** (American virtue-ethics, Anabaptist-adjacent)
- **John Milbank** and Radical Orthodoxy (contemporary Anglican Catholic)

## Relevance to this vault

- [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s entire public political idiom is political-theological. The Palantir corporate mythology (Tolkien, guarded civilization, the dangerous seeing-stone) is political theology in pop register. The *Straussian Moment* essay is explicitly a political-theological argument.
- [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s tradition — [Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md)'s *Between Facts and Norms*, the Frankfurt School generally — was built explicitly to answer Schmittian political theology with a post-metaphysical defense of democratic publicity. Karp's Habermasian commitments make him the single most Schmitt-unfriendly major American tech executive.

Palantir is thus the unlikely site where political theology (Thiel) and its principled Habermasian refutation (Karp) are co-directors.
