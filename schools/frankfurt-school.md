# The Frankfurt School

The intellectual tradition growing out of the Institute for Social Research (*Institut für Sozialforschung*) founded in Frankfurt in 1923 and revived after the war in 1951. The institutional and philosophical home of [critical theory](critical-theory.md) — and the direct academic lineage in which [Alex Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) received his doctoral formation.

## Three generations

### First generation (1923–1950s)

Founded by Felix Weil and Carl Grünberg. Reorganized under [Max Horkheimer](../people/max-horkheimer.md)'s directorship (1930–), who pivoted the Institute from orthodox Marxist economics to an integrated, interdisciplinary program combining philosophy, sociology, psychoanalysis, and cultural analysis.

Key figures:

- [Horkheimer](../people/max-horkheimer.md) — director, programmatic theorist
- [Adorno](../people/theodor-adorno.md) — philosophy, music, aesthetics, sociology
- [Marcuse](../people/herbert-marcuse.md) — political theory, Marx and Freud integration
- [Walter Benjamin](../people/walter-benjamin.md) — close associate, not formally employed
- Erich Fromm — psychoanalysis, character structure
- Leo Lowenthal — sociology of literature
- Friedrich Pollock — political economy
- Franz Neumann, Otto Kirchheimer — jurisprudence, political analysis of fascism

The first generation fled Nazi Germany (1933) for Geneva and then New York, where the Institute continued research under Columbia's umbrella (1934–1949). Major works of this period: *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, *The Authoritarian Personality*, *Eclipse of Reason*.

Return to Frankfurt (1949–1951) with Horkheimer and Adorno. Marcuse remained in America.

### Second generation (1960s–1990s)

- [Jürgen Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md) — the dominant figure
- [Karl-Otto Apel](../people/karl-otto-apel.md) — discourse ethics co-founder
- Albrecht Wellmer, Oskar Negt, Alfred Schmidt — contributing figures

Habermas's re-grounding of critical theory on the basis of [communicative action](../concepts/communicative-action.md) rather than consciousness-philosophy was the major second-generation achievement. This re-orientation made it possible to defend the Enlightenment project against both the Frankfurt-style pessimism of *Dialectic of Enlightenment* and the postmodern declarations of its bankruptcy.

### Third generation (1990s–present)

- Axel Honneth — Habermas's successor at Frankfurt; theorist of recognition
- Rainer Forst — justification, power, toleration
- Hauke Brunkhorst — solidarity, global law
- Nancy Fraser, Seyla Benhabib — American inheritors; recognition/redistribution debate
- Jürgen Habermas — still writing into the 2020s

## Distinctive features

- **Interdisciplinary:** philosophy, sociology, psychoanalysis, aesthetics, political economy.
- **Normative commitment:** diagnosis in service of emancipation; theory as reflective moment of practice.
- **Dialectical:** not linear causation but immanent critique; the target is shown to be in contradiction with itself.
- **Historical:** every concept is placed in its historical genesis and trajectory.
- **Defense of modernity, ambivalent and differentiated:** the Enlightenment must be saved from itself.

## Relevance to this vault

The Frankfurt School is [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s direct intellectual home. His doctoral dissertation was written inside this tradition under Habermas with Apel on the committee. Karp's public writing (*The Technological Republic*, shareholder letters) uses Frankfurt School vocabulary — the life-world, instrumental reason, the systematic distortions of communication — in ways that are transparent to other Frankfurt-formed readers and largely opaque to others.

For the [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) side: the Frankfurt School is the 20th-century antagonist. Thiel's favored thinkers (Strauss, Schmitt, Girard) either explicitly fought the Frankfurt School or worked on incompatible ground. But see also the curious convergences: Adorno on the culture industry and Thiel on social media, for instance, diagnose the same phenomenon from opposite sides.
