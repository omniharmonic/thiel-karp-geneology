## Persecution and the Art of Writing

### A deep dive on Strauss's 1952 hermeneutic manifesto

**Author:** [Leo Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md)
**Published:** 1952 (Free Press)
**Composition:** A collection of four previously published essays, unified by the introductory essay "Persecution and the Art of Writing," which became the book's title piece

This is the single most influential (and most controversial) methodological work Strauss ever wrote. It argues — against the dominant mid-20th-century hermeneutic orthodoxy — that philosophers through history have often written *esoterically*, and that learning to read them rightly requires recovering the art of esoteric reading.

The book's afterlife has been enormous. It has shaped American political theory, legal hermeneutics, classical scholarship, and (via Allan Bloom and the Hoover circle) the intellectual posture of [Peter Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s public writing. See also [esoteric-writing](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md).

---

## The essays

The book collects four essays:

1. **"Persecution and the Art of Writing"** (1941, revised) — the programmatic introduction.
2. **"The Literary Character of the *Guide for the Perplexed*"** — Strauss on [Maimonides](../people/maimonides.md). Maimonides explicitly tells his reader he is going to write esoterically; Strauss reads the *Guide* accordingly.
3. **"How Fārābī Read Plato's *Laws*"** — Strauss on the medieval Islamic political philosopher Alfārābī. Alfārābī's reading of Plato becomes, for Strauss, an exemplary case of one esoteric writer reading another.
4. **"How to Study Spinoza's *Theologico-Political Treatise*"** — Strauss on Spinoza. Spinoza, working in a Christian Amsterdam hostile to his philosophical views, wrote to preserve his teaching under censorship.

The introductory essay generalizes the method; the three case studies apply it.

## The central argument

Strauss's claim unfolds in careful steps:

### Step 1: Philosophy and the city are in tension

Philosophy, per Strauss (following Plato), is the free questioning of everything — including the opinions on which political community is founded. The political community, per its own needs, cannot tolerate unrestricted questioning of its founding opinions without risking its own stability. There is a structural tension between philosophy and the city.

This tension is not contingent; it is constitutive. A philosopher in a political community is always, at some level, in a dangerous position.

### Step 2: Writing exposes the philosopher more than speaking

Socrates did not write. This is not accidental. Oral speech can be tailored to the hearer; writing goes out into the world and cannot defend itself against misreading. A writing philosopher who wants to communicate to philosophical readers without inviting political persecution must develop techniques.

### Step 3: Esoteric writing is the technique

The esoteric technique: the text is organized in two layers.

- **Exoteric layer:** the surface that the majority reader (the politically-unreflective, the orthodox, the censor) will engage. This surface is designed to be harmless, edifying, or at most mildly heterodox.
- **Esoteric layer:** the depth that the careful philosophical reader will recover by attending to:
 - Contradictions the author leaves in the text
 - What the author says once and then contradicts
 - What is attributed to a character versus said in the author's voice
 - What is carefully placed at openings, centers, and closings of texts
 - What is omitted from a standard list
 - Where the author protests too much

### Step 4: The method is recoverable

Strauss argues that esoteric writing is not a secret code requiring initiation. It is an art whose techniques can be learned by careful reading. The reader who learns the art joins, in effect, a community of philosophical readers across centuries — a trans-historical interpretive community.

### Step 5: Moderns have forgotten this

Enlightenment liberalism's programmatic commitment to publicity (all speech should be open, all reasons transparent, censorship is the exception) has led modern readers to assume pre-modern texts were written in this mode. They were not. Modern reading of Plato, Maimonides, Machiavelli, Spinoza, etc., has systematically flattened the texts by missing the esoteric dimension.

## The scandal

*Persecution and the Art of Writing* caused an uproar that has not fully subsided. The specific contested claims:

### It is anti-democratic

If serious philosophical truth is by nature accessible only to careful readers, and if those readers constitute a kind of trans-historical elite, then philosophy and democracy are in tension. Strauss did not deny this; he argued that it had always been so.

Critics (Shadia Drury, Stephen Holmes, Myles Burnyeat) have read this as authorizing a politics of elite manipulation — the philosopher-rulers tell noble lies to the masses while preserving the truth among themselves. Strauss's defenders (Catherine and Michael Zuckert, Steven Smith, Nathan Tarcov) read him more charitably: the philosopher is not a ruler but a preserver of inquiry, and esoteric writing serves a pedagogical rather than a manipulative function.

### It is unfalsifiable

The esoteric reader can always claim a deeper teaching where the surface reading shows one. There is no neutral test between the esoteric and the non-esoteric reading. Critics charge that this makes the method self-confirming: anyone skeptical of the deeper reading is by definition not a good-enough reader.

Strauss's defense: the method produces *specific* readings that can be assessed for coherence, plausibility, and textual adequacy. An esoteric reading is not a license to project; it is a disciplined interpretation that must track textual signals.

### It is politically dangerous

From the left, Strauss has been read as providing intellectual cover for authoritarianism (manipulators need obfuscating texts to manipulate). From some parts of the right, Strauss has been read as providing intellectual cover for American neoconservative foreign policy (noble lies to drive populations into wars). The latter reading, especially prominent after the Iraq War, has done as much as anything to sour American academic reception of Strauss in the last two decades.

Strauss's more careful defenders point out that most actual Straussians reject both readings, and that the identification of Straussianism with neoconservative policy is partial at best.

## Why this matters for Thiel

Three through-lines:

### The method

Thiel writes in modes many readers recognize as Straussian. *The Straussian Moment* (2004) is explicitly so. Many of his interviews and lectures have a compressed, layered quality in which the apparent surface is not quite the teaching. Whether Thiel intends to write esoterically or not, the stylistic register is closer to *Persecution and the Art of Writing* than to standard contemporary essay form.

### The posture

The Straussian philosophical posture — at once philosophically serious, in tension with the city, communicating to a community of readers across contexts — is Thielian. The Thiel Fellowship, the various private intellectual gatherings Thiel has hosted, the cultivated air of speaking to those who can hear — all are Straussian in structure, whatever Thiel's specific doctrinal commitments.

### The content

The specific content of Strauss's critique of modern publicity — that the Enlightenment program of universal reason-publicity is incoherent about its own theological-political substrate — is directly absorbed into Thiel's diagnosis of contemporary American politics.

## Why this matters for Karp

The Habermasian tradition is, method-for-method, the opposite of *Persecution and the Art of Writing*. [Communicative action](../concepts/communicative-action.md) requires *publicity*: all claims must be defensible with reasons accessible to all competent participants in the discourse.

Karp writes exoterically. His shareholder letters, his interview responses, his book — none are written to conceal a deeper teaching from the uninitiated. They are written to persuade, to submit claims to scrutiny, to perform Habermasian discourse-ethics in public.

This is a non-trivial stylistic-ethical difference between Thiel and Karp. It is one of the clearest places to see their philosophical traditions at work in their own communicative conduct.
