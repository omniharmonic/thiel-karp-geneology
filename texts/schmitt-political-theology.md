## Political Theology

### A deep dive on Schmitt's 1922 manifesto of sovereignty

**Full title:** *Politische Theologie. Vier Kapitel zur Lehre von der Souveränität*
**Author:** [Carl Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md)
**Published:** 1922 (Duncker & Humblot, Munich and Leipzig); expanded 1934 second edition with a new preface
**English translation:** George Schwab, *Political Theology: Four Chapters on the Concept of Sovereignty* (MIT Press 1985; reissued University of Chicago Press 2005, with foreword by Tracy B. Strong)

*Politische Theologie* is roughly seventy pages in every standard edition. It is almost certainly the most consequential short book of 20th-century political thought. The book names a concept ([political theology](../concepts/political-theology.md)) that has since given its title to a sub-discipline; offers a four-word definition of sovereignty that every subsequent theorist of the sovereign has had to either adopt or refute; and, through its last chapter, revives a 19th-century Catholic counter-revolutionary tradition that had seemed safely buried. Schmitt wrote it in the crisis of the early Weimar Republic, at thirty-four, before his catastrophic political choice. He reissued it in 1934, a year into his tenure as Nazi Germany's would-be crown jurist, with a preface that quietly tried to re-anchor the argument for its new political moment. It is, in other words, a book that has to be read with the full history held in view — which is precisely why it has become the bridging text between Thiel's and Karp's traditions in this vault.

---

## The book's structure

The book consists of four chapters of roughly equal length. Each chapter is self-contained but the sequence is deliberate: from the definition of sovereignty, to the polemic against legal positivism, to the historical-structural thesis on secularization, to the counter-revolutionary philosophy of the state. The movement is from concept to enemy to genealogy to tradition.

### Chapter 1: "Definition of Sovereignty"

The book opens with the sentence that has defined the Schmittian vocabulary: *Souverän ist, wer über den Ausnahmezustand entscheidet.* "Sovereign is he who decides on the exception." Schwab translates *Ausnahmezustand* as "state of exception" in Schmitt's usage here, though the German term covers a wider semantic field that includes "state of emergency" and "exceptional case."

The chapter does two things. First, it defines the exception negatively: the exception is not any of the ordinary legally-specified emergencies (martial law, emergency decree, state of siege) that a constitutional order might itself anticipate and regulate. The genuine exception is precisely the case that the legal order cannot anticipate, and that therefore cannot itself be regulated from within the legal order. When the situation becomes genuinely extreme, a decision has to be made about whether *this* is such a case and what the appropriate response is. Neither the determination nor the response can be specified in advance by a rule.

Second, it draws the philosophical consequence. The capacity to decide the exception cannot itself be legally grounded, because any legal grounding presupposes exactly the settled legal order that the exception has called into question. Sovereignty, in Schmitt's formulation, is therefore a *borderline concept* (*Grenzbegriff*) — not a concept of the central case of governance but a concept of its limit. The sovereign is whoever, when the legal order is in jeopardy, decides what is to be done.

This is the move that makes Schmitt so hard to refute from within the standard vocabulary of legal theory. He has located sovereignty not in the ordinary power to govern but in the extraordinary power to stand outside the norm — and he has claimed that this power is structurally prior to the norm itself. The political decision precedes the legal rule.

### Chapter 2: "The Problem of Sovereignty as the Problem of the Legal Form and of the Decision"

The second chapter is the book's polemical core. Schmitt's target is explicit: Hans Kelsen's pure theory of law (*Reine Rechtslehre*), the dominant legal-theoretical framework of interwar Central European jurisprudence. Kelsen had argued for a rigorous separation of legal from political questions — law is a system of norms whose validity derives from higher norms, ultimately from a presupposed *Grundnorm* (basic norm). Sovereignty, for Kelsen, was either a redundant concept (reducible to the validity of the legal order) or a metaphysical residue to be eliminated.

Schmitt's counter-argument turns on the claim that the decision is not derivable from the norm. A legal norm is general; its application to a particular case always requires a moment of concretization that cannot itself be specified in advance. Even within ordinary legal adjudication, the judge decides. At the limit — in the genuine exception — the entire legal order requires a decision that no legal rule has programmed. Kelsen's pure theory, Schmitt argues, has eliminated the decision by assumption rather than by argument; it has described a ghost-world of norms without the juridical flesh of the decision that makes those norms operative.

The polemic is usually labeled Schmitt's "decisionism" (*Dezisionismus*). The name is slightly misleading — Schmitt's own self-description evolved toward a later *concrete-order thinking* (*konkretes Ordnungsdenken*) that moderated the pure decisionism. But in the 1922 book, the decisionist thesis is stark: every legal order rests on a prior political decision, and that decision cannot be legally justified without circularity. The pretense that it can — that liberal legality is self-grounding — is the specific illusion of early-twentieth-century jurisprudence.

### Chapter 3: "Political Theology"

The third chapter is the one that gives the book its title and its concept. Schmitt's thesis unfolds from a single famous sentence: *"Alle prägnanten Begriffe der modernen Staatslehre sind säkularisierte theologische Begriffe."* "All significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are secularized theological concepts."

The claim has, as the concept note distinguishes, three levels. At the *historical* level, it is a genealogical claim: modern political concepts developed out of earlier theological concepts through processes of secularization. Sovereignty inherits from divine omnipotence; the general will inherits from providence; the miracle (God's suspension of natural law) is the theological original of the political exception (the sovereign's suspension of positive law). The history of modern political thought can be rewritten as the history of a secularization — that is, of a certain kind of transposition.

At the *structural* level, the claim is stronger. It is not only that the concepts once had theological sources; it is that they have retained the *structural form* of their theological origins even after the content has been secularized. The sovereign who decides the exception occupies the same structural position in modern political theory that the omnipotent God of late-medieval nominalist theology occupies in late-medieval metaphysics. The structural analogies are not decorative; they are constitutive.

At the *normative* level, the claim is strongest and most contested. Schmitt argues that modernity's attempt to run politics without acknowledging this theological substrate must fail. Modern liberalism has tried to produce a politics whose premises are purely rational, purely procedural, purely public. In doing so it has repressed but not eliminated the theological substrate that its concepts still carry. The repressed returns — in the form of crises that liberal theory cannot describe, and in the form of political decisions that liberal theory cannot recognize as political. What looks from the liberal vantage like politics without theology is, for Schmitt, politics whose theology has gone underground and thereby become unaccountable.

This is where Schmitt's book is both diagnostic and provocation. It diagnoses a structural truth that many readers, even hostile readers, have found hard to dismiss. And it provokes the normative question that the left Schmittian and the right Schmittian answer in opposite directions: having diagnosed the theology, what do we do about it?

### Chapter 4: "On the Counterrevolutionary Philosophy of the State"

The fourth chapter turns from analytical argument to intellectual history. Schmitt reads three 19th-century Catholic counter-revolutionaries — Joseph de Maistre (1753–1821), Louis de Bonald (1754–1840), and above all the Spanish conservative Donoso Cortés (1809–1853). They are, for Schmitt, the genuine critics of liberal parliamentarism in the 19th century, and the authors who saw earliest and clearest that the French Revolution had ruptured the theological-political order on which European legitimacy had rested.

De Maistre's *Considerations on France* (1797) and *Du Pape* (1819) had argued that all genuine sovereignty is absolute and that the liberal attempt to distribute it among elected assemblies was an incoherent denial of what sovereignty is. De Bonald had argued for a theocratic traditionalism grounded in the inseparability of language, religion, and authority. Donoso Cortés — the most influential for Schmitt — had diagnosed the 19th century as a battle between Catholicism and an atheist socialism, and had insisted that liberal parliamentarism was too indecisive, too discussion-oriented, too committed to procedural compromise to meet the crisis. For Donoso, the discussion-class (*clase discutidora*) was by definition incapable of decision, and therefore incapable of defending order against revolutionary negation.

Schmitt's use of this tradition is not simple endorsement. He picks up the counter-revolutionary diagnosis — that liberal parliamentarism has replaced decision with discussion and thereby hollowed out political legitimacy — without fully embracing the Catholic-monarchical programmatic. What he takes from the counter-revolutionaries is their sharper-than-liberal perception of what liberalism has repressed. The 1922 book ends by placing this counter-revolutionary tradition as the preserver of an intellectual vocabulary that modern politics has lost and will need to recover.

## The intellectual targets

Three targets sit in the background of the book. The first is Kelsen, the direct polemical object of Chapter 2. The second is [Max Weber](../people/max-weber.md), whose sociology of legitimacy Schmitt had studied closely — Schmitt attended Weber's seminars in Munich in 1919–20 — and whose *value-free* analysis of charisma, rationality, and tradition Schmitt regarded as insufficient. Weber had described modern legitimacy in terms of legal-rational domination; Schmitt argued that this description elided the sovereign decision that founds the legal-rational order in the first place. The third target, more diffuse, is the liberal parliamentarism of the Weimar constitution — the system of deliberative representation, coalition government, and constitutional review that Schmitt regarded as fundamentally unable to defend itself against existential challenge.

The targets share a common form: each, in Schmitt's diagnosis, refuses to name the decision. Kelsen refuses it at the level of legal theory; Weber refuses it at the level of sociological description; Weimar parliamentarism refuses it at the level of political practice. *Politische Theologie* is, from this angle, one long argument that the decision cannot be refused — that the attempt to refuse it only relocates it to places where it becomes less accountable.

## The 1934 second edition

The second edition appeared in November 1934. Schmitt had joined the Nazi Party in May 1933, been appointed Prussian state councilor, and assumed editorship of the Nazi lawyers' journal. He had also just survived the Night of the Long Knives (June–July 1934), which removed several of his erstwhile patrons and suggested the fragility of even the most elevated academic-political positions.

The second edition's changes are limited but pointed. The body of the text is substantially the original; the intervention comes in the preface and in selective edits. The new preface positions the book against what Schmitt now frames as the twin errors of interwar jurisprudence: Kelsen's pure normativism (still the target of Chapter 2) and a pure decisionism that Schmitt now wants to distinguish from his own view. He introduces the framework of *concrete-order thinking* — the idea that political legality should be grounded in the concrete order of a historical people — and positions *Politische Theologie* as a stage on the way to that view, rather than as the final word.

Readers have long debated how far the 1934 revisions should be read as strategic accommodation to the Nazi moment and how far they reflect genuine intellectual development. The honest answer is probably both. The *concrete-order* framework is compatible with the Nazi ideology of the *Volk* as the concrete political subject; it is also compatible with a more general Catholic-institutionalist critique of pure decisionism. Schmitt is doing several things at once, and their motivations are not fully separable.

One edit deserves particular notice: the 1934 edition expands the engagement with [Thomas Hobbes](../people/thomas-hobbes.md). Schmitt had been deepening his Hobbes reading through the early 1930s, and would publish *The Leviathan in the State Theory of Thomas Hobbes* in 1938. The Hobbes of the 1934 *Politische Theologie* is the prototype of Schmitt's decisionist sovereign — the figure who decides the foundational political question ("Who is the enemy?", "Who is to be obeyed?") in the moment that the decision cannot be deferred any longer.

## The upstream: Bodin and Hobbes

Schmitt's sovereignty concept is a radicalization of earlier early-modern theories, and the book is legible only when that lineage is held in view. [Jean Bodin](../people/jean-bodin.md)'s *Six Books of the Commonwealth* (1576) had defined sovereignty as the absolute, indivisible, perpetual power to make law. Hobbes's *Leviathan* (1651) had grounded sovereignty in the social contract and the answer to civil war. Both had located sovereignty in the power to make the law. Schmitt relocates it: from the power to make law to the power to *suspend* law in the exception. The move is small on the page and decisive in practice. It transfers the definition of sovereignty from the routine case (the sovereign as legislator) to the limit case (the sovereign as exception-decider), and in doing so transforms sovereignty from a concept of ordered governance into a concept of ungrounded decision.

Without Bodin and Hobbes, Schmitt's move is illegible as a move on *sovereignty* at all. He is not inventing the concept; he is relocating it. This is part of why the book has functioned as a bridge: readers who approach it through Bodin and Hobbes find it legible as early-modern political theory made more dangerous; readers who approach it through Kelsen and Weber find it legible as a polemic internal to 20th-century jurisprudence.

## Reception — the left appropriation

The 20th-century left has read Schmitt against Schmitt, extracting the diagnostic apparatus while rejecting the political commitments. The line runs:

**[Walter Benjamin](../people/walter-benjamin.md)**, *Critique of Violence* (1921). Benjamin's essay predates *Politische Theologie* by a year but was circulating in the same Weimar intellectual milieu; the two texts stage a dialogue that runs through both thinkers' subsequent work. Benjamin's distinction between *law-preserving violence* and *law-founding violence* tracks Schmitt's between routine legality and the exceptional decision, but Benjamin deploys the distinction to a revolutionary end — a *divine violence* that would break the law-violence nexus altogether. Benjamin wrote the 8th thesis in *On the Concept of History* (1940) — "the tradition of the oppressed teaches us that the 'state of emergency' in which we live is not the exception but the rule" — as a direct reply to the Schmittian sovereign. His 1930 letter to Schmitt acknowledging the methodological debt (while repudiating the politics) is one of the most-cited documents in 20th-century political theory.

**Karl Löwith**, *Meaning in History* (1949). Löwith extended the secularization thesis — that modern categories are transposed theological categories — to the philosophy of history as a whole. Modern progress-narratives, for Löwith, are secularized Christian eschatology. The argument owes much to Schmitt and was read for decades as a companion to *Politische Theologie*.

**Jacob Taubes**, *The Political Theology of Paul* (1987 lectures, published 1993). Taubes, a Jewish philosopher of religion and one of Schmitt's few post-war interlocutors, read the apostle Paul as a political theologian in the Schmittian sense — a thinker of sovereignty, exception, and the eschatological cut in historical time. Taubes's reading has been crucial for the recent surge of interest in Paul among continental political philosophers (Agamben, Badiou, Žižek).

**Giorgio Agamben**, the *Homo Sacer* project (1995–2014). Agamben's extension of Schmitt is the most systematic contemporary appropriation. *Homo Sacer* (1995) rereads Roman law through the Schmittian sovereign-exception relation and argues that bare life — the life exposed to sovereign violence — is the hidden foundation of Western politics. *State of Exception* (2003) reads the emergency powers of 20th- and 21st-century liberal democracies as the normalization of what Schmitt described as an exceptional moment. *The Kingdom and the Glory* (2007) traces the political-theological origins of modern governmental rationality into its Trinitarian theological origins. Agamben is Schmittian in vocabulary and concept but anti-Schmittian in politics: his aim is to deactivate the sovereign-exception relation, not to preserve it.

**[Slavoj Žižek](../people/slavoj-zizek.md)** and the Ljubljana school have read Schmitt through Lacanian psychoanalysis, treating the decisionist sovereign as the political analogue of the Lacanian master-signifier that anchors a symbolic order against the Real. Žižek's reading is characteristically paradoxical — he takes the Schmittian diagnosis seriously enough to worry about it, and uses it to critique liberal multiculturalism from the left.

**Chantal Mouffe**, starting in the 1990s, built an "agonistic" political theory explicitly grounded in a Schmittian recognition of the [friend/enemy](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md) structure of the political — but recast as *adversaries* rather than *enemies*, to preserve the conflictual core of democratic politics without the lethal register.

The left Schmittians share a common structure: accept the diagnosis that liberal democracy has repressed the political-theological substrate of its own concepts; refuse the prescription that the repression should be reversed by a more authoritarian sovereignty.

## Reception — the right appropriation

The right appropriation is more straightforward because it takes Schmitt's normative as well as diagnostic claims. Contemporary nodes include:

**Adrian Vermeule** (Harvard Law), whose "common good constitutionalism" is a Catholic-integralist attempt to retheorize American constitutional law on explicitly moral-theological premises, with Schmittian resonances throughout. Vermeule has written approvingly of Schmitt on specific points while maintaining the (necessary) distance from Schmitt's 1933–36 political choices.

**Patrick Deneen**, whose *Why Liberalism Failed* (2018) is an essentially Schmittian diagnosis of the late-modern liberal order as a regime that has repressed its own theological-political substrate and is, in consequence, hollowing out. Deneen is more cautious than Vermeule on programmatics but the diagnostic structure is recognizably Schmittian.

**[Curtis Yarvin](../people/curtis-yarvin.md)** (Mencius Moldbug) and the neoreactionary tradition. Yarvin's claim that modern liberal democracy is a theology that denies being a theology — and that sovereignty should be restored to an explicit, accountable unitary source — is Schmittian sovereignty transposed into 21st-century American political debate. Yarvin is not a systematic Schmitt reader in the academic sense but the conceptual vocabulary is unmistakable.

**The traditionalist-Catholic right** more broadly (First Things circle, Sohrab Ahmari, Ross Douthat in selected moods, the integralist milieu around Edmund Waldstein and Josiah Joncas) works in the political-theological register Schmitt opened, even where it disavows Schmitt directly.

The right Schmittians share the diagnosis of liberal crisis with the left Schmittians. What they differ on is prescription: the right wants to reacknowledge and restore the political-theological substrate, not to deactivate it.

## Relevance to Thiel

The *Politische Theologie* vocabulary — sovereignty as decision, the state of exception, the secularization thesis — is Thielian vocabulary. It is most explicit in [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s *The Straussian Moment* (2004), the essay that announced his political-theoretical orientation and remains the most systematic public statement of his worldview. The essay reads Schmitt directly (alongside [Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md) and Girard), and its central diagnosis — that American post-9/11 politics has entered a moment in which the liberal-procedural vocabulary is inadequate to the existential stakes — is a Schmittian move made in the Schmittian register.

Thiel's reading is not a pure Schmitt reading. He filters Schmitt through Girard, so the political-theological substrate that Schmitt wants to preserve Thiel wants to *see through* — to recognize the scapegoat mechanism operating beneath the political-theological claims that Schmitt takes at face value. This is a non-trivial modification. In the pure Schmittian reading, political theology is the truth liberalism represses; in the Thielian-Girardian reading, political theology is the surface beneath which sacrificial mechanics operate, and the Gospel is the unmasking. Thiel's political-theological vocabulary is thus closer to Schmitt's phrasing than to Schmitt's underlying metaphysics.

The Tolkienian mythopoetic register in Thiel's corporate and public rhetoric — Palantir as the seeing-stone, the orcs at the gates, the Numenorean civilization to defend — is political theology in pop-cultural form. Schmitt's insistence that modern politics operates on repressed theological substrates is not only compatible with but actively demands such mythopoetic supplementation when the high-register theological language has become culturally unavailable. Tolkien does the political-theological work that explicit Catholic monarchism can no longer do.

## Relevance to Karp

[Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s intellectual formation is in the Frankfurt School, under [Jürgen Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md) and Karl-Otto Apel. His doctoral work on aggressive behavior in U.S. jurisprudence was conducted in Frankfurt in the mid-1990s. The Habermasian project is the most systematic post-war attempt to defeat Schmitt on philosophical grounds — to provide a post-metaphysical grounding of democratic legitimacy that does not require the theological substrate Schmitt argues is ineliminable.

Habermas's *Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (1962) is a genealogy of the bourgeois public sphere as an institution of rational-critical discourse; it is, implicitly throughout, the counter-narrative to Schmitt's diagnosis of parliamentarism as a degenerated discussion-class. Where Schmitt (via Donoso Cortés) sees discussion as the evasion of decision, Habermas sees discussion as the medium through which democratic legitimacy is continuously produced.

Habermas's *Between Facts and Norms* (*Faktizität und Geltung*, 1992) is the mature statement of the position and the most direct answer to Schmitt in the post-war canon. Habermas argues that popular sovereignty and human rights are co-originally grounded in the structure of communicative action; neither requires a theological foundation, and the Schmittian decisionist sovereign is therefore not the only option for a philosophically serious political theory. Legitimacy is discursively produced and normatively assessable without the Schmittian decision.

For Karp, *Politische Theologie* is thus the book his tradition was built to refute. When he defends what he calls "the Western project" — the institutional commitment to liberal-democratic norms of public reason, procedural legitimacy, and rights-based governance — he is defending exactly what Schmitt diagnosed as incoherent. Karp's Habermasian training makes him the single major American tech executive whose philosophical formation is structurally anti-Schmittian.

That Thiel and Karp co-direct the same firm while standing on opposite sides of the Schmitt–Habermas axis is not a coincidence but a condition of Palantir's cultural legibility: the firm contains both the Schmittian political-theological inflection (the security mission, the seeing-stone mythos, the existential framing of U.S.–China) and its principled Habermasian refutation (the insistence on legal accountability, the public defense of democratic institutions, the rhetoric of the Western Enlightenment project).

## The ethical-political stakes

Any serious reading of *Politische Theologie* must hold the Nazi question open. Schmitt was not yet a Party member in 1922; he would not join until 1933. The first edition is not a Nazi book. But the vocabulary it developed — sovereign decision above the law, exception as the political moment of truth, decisionism against proceduralism — became operationally useful to Nazi legal theory in ways Schmitt himself would enact after 1933. The 1934 preface is written from inside that operational use. Any reading that ignores this sequence is inadequate.

The question the tradition has had to face is whether the analytical apparatus of *Politische Theologie* can be separated from the political commitments that Schmitt chose. The left Schmittians (Benjamin, Agamben, Mouffe, Taubes, Žižek) have argued yes — the diagnostic can be extracted and redeployed against authoritarianism, not for it. The right Schmittians have been less careful, and the Schmitt revival in some traditionalist-Catholic and neoreactionary circles has sometimes been more continuous with the 1934 Schmitt than its defenders like to acknowledge. The honest reader stays alert to the joint: the analytical acuity is real; the political commitments are real; their relation is not fully stable.

One further point: Schmitt's famous antisemitic writings from the mid-1930s — the 1936 conference on Jewish influence in German jurisprudence — are not in *Politische Theologie* and are in many ways atypical of Schmitt's work before and after. But they happened, and they preclude any reading that presents Schmitt simply as a brilliant analyst caught in bad historical weather. He made choices. The book has to be read with those choices in view.

## Key concepts the book names

*Politische Theologie* introduces or stabilizes the following conceptual vocabulary:

- **Sovereignty as decision** — sovereignty defined by the capacity to decide the exception, not by the capacity to enact routine law.
- **The state of exception** (*Ausnahmezustand*) — the limit case in which the legal order is suspended and a new decision is required.
- **Decisionism** (*Dezisionismus*) — the thesis that every legal order ultimately rests on a prior political decision that cannot itself be legally grounded.
- **Secularization** — the thesis that modern political concepts are transposed theological concepts, retaining structural form after content has been secularized.
- **Political theology** — both the concept (specific to this book) and, downstream, the name of an entire 20th-century sub-discipline. See [the concept note](../concepts/political-theology.md) and [the school note](../schools/political-theology.md).

These concepts are no longer Schmitt's private vocabulary. They have become general property of 20th- and 21st-century political theory. A reader who does not know where they came from is reading political theory as if its vocabulary grew by spontaneous generation.
