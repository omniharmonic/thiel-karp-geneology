## Violence and the Sacred

### A deep dive on Girard's 1972 anthropological breakthrough

**Full title:** *La violence et le sacré*
**Author:** [René Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)
**Published:** 1972 (Paris, Grasset); English translation 1977 (Johns Hopkins University Press, tr. Patrick Gregory)

*Violence and the Sacred* is the book in which Girard moved from literary criticism ([mimetic desire](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md) as analyzed in great novelists) to general anthropology (mimetic rivalry as the engine of cultural formation). It is the book that most directly accounts for the [scapegoat mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md) and for religion as the historical residue of sacred violence. Every subsequent Girardian text — including Girard's own work on Christianity — builds on the framework laid down here.

---

## What the book is doing

The 1961 *Deceit, Desire, and the Novel* had made a literary-critical claim: great European novelists discovered that desire is not autonomous but triangular, mediated through models. *Violence and the Sacred* generalizes this claim into a theory of culture as such.

The argument structure:

1. **Mimetic desire is universal** — not a literary phenomenon but the fundamental structure of human desire.
2. **Mimetic desire produces rivalry** — when two humans share a model, they converge on the same object and, via each other, become doubles.
3. **Rivalry escalates toward violence** — mimetic doubling is unstable; when it generalizes across a community (a *mimetic crisis*), violence becomes imminent.
4. **The scapegoat mechanism resolves the crisis** — spontaneously, collective violence polarizes on an arbitrary victim; the victim's killing or expulsion restores unity.
5. **Religion grows up to repeat, remember, and conceal the founding violence** — ritual, prohibition, myth. Sacred = that which is marked by sacrificial violence.

The book's originality: it claims to account for the very existence of religion, prohibition, and ritual using a single anthropological mechanism that had been hiding in plain sight.

## The specific arguments

### The critique of ethnology

Girard opens with a long, careful reading of the French ethnographic tradition — Émile Durkheim, Claude Lévi-Strauss, Marcel Mauss. He argues that these traditions had seen everything except the founding violence. Lévi-Strauss's structural anthropology, for instance, brilliantly maps the exchange-patterns of primitive cultures but fails to explain *why* these patterns exist — what problem they are solving. Girard's answer: they are solving the problem of escalating mimetic violence, and the exchange-patterns are how culture distributes what would otherwise converge catastrophically.

### The critique of [Freud](../people/sigmund-freud.md)

Girard's reading of Freud is extensive and polemical. The Oedipus complex, per Girard, is a mis-description of mimetic rivalry. Freud got the structure right (the father as rival to the son) but the phenomenology wrong (the desire is not primarily sexual; the structure is primarily mimetic). The *father*, in Girard's reading, is not the obstacle to the son's Oedipal desire for the mother; the father is the *model* through whom the mother becomes desirable in the first place.

This makes *Violence and the Sacred* one of the great 20th-century quarrels with psychoanalysis. Girard is not dismissing Freud; he is claiming that a deeper theory (his own) subsumes what Freud correctly noticed while avoiding Freud's specific mistakes.

### The reading of Greek tragedy

Girard's case studies include Sophocles' *Oedipus Rex* and *Antigone*, Euripides' *Bacchae*, and the broader tragic corpus. His thesis: tragedy is the literary form that *almost* unmasks the scapegoat mechanism but backs away at the last moment. The tragic genre's tension — hero doubles, oracles that look like prophecies but turn out to be self-fulfilling, endings in which the community re-unites against the hero — is, for Girard, the scapegoat mechanism becoming conscious of itself without yet being able to see clearly what it is.

This reading of tragedy is one of the most influential features of the book, even among non-Girardian literary critics.

### The reading of ritual

Girard's treatment of ritual (sacrifice, animal substitutes, scapegoat rites, festivals of violent unanimity like Dionysian festivals) is an attempt to show that all ritual is derivative of the founding scapegoat event. Rituals are *controlled re-enactments*, *symbolic repetitions*, or *calendrical repetitions* of what was originally a spontaneous crisis-resolution.

Sacrifice is not, per Girard, a gift to the gods. Sacrifice is a controlled repetition of the founding murder, performed to prevent a new uncontrolled crisis. The victim is the scapegoat; the god is the sacralized scapegoat.

## What the book does not yet do

*Violence and the Sacred* does not make Girard's Christian claim. That move — that the Hebrew Bible and the Gospels are the unique texts that unmask the scapegoat mechanism from the victim's side — waits for *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World* (1978).

So *Violence and the Sacred* is the book you hand to a reader who wants to engage Girard anthropologically without the theological commitments. It is the Girard that secular anthropologists, literary critics, and Silicon Valley strategy theorists can work with. It is also the Girard that non-religious readers sometimes prefer, though Girard himself would insist the anthropology cannot be fully understood without the theology.

## Why this matters for Thiel

*Violence and the Sacred* is the book that gives [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) the apparatus for his diagnostic reading of modern society. Specifically:

- **The critique of undifferentiation.** Girard's account of mimetic crisis as the collapse of social differentiation is directly operational in Thiel's reading of contemporary cultural politics. "Competition is for losers" is Girard's undifferentiation thesis reframed as business strategy.
- **Tragedy as model for reading modernity.** Thiel's repeated recourse to classical-tragic and mythopoetic frames (the Tolkienian, but also Greek-tragic references in his interviews) is Girard-compatible. Modernity, read through *Violence and the Sacred*, is a tragedy approaching catastrophe.
- **The critique of Lévi-Strauss and ethnography.** Thiel's skepticism of academic social science is structurally continuous with Girard's critique — though Thiel rarely cites the specific polemic.

One caveat: Thiel reads *Violence and the Sacred* alongside *Things Hidden*, not as a standalone. For Thiel, Girard's anthropology is inseparable from the Christian unmasking. Some of his Silicon Valley popularizers (Luke Burgis, others) read Girard more as a business strategist and strip out the theology — a move Thiel himself would resist.

## Critical questions about the book

Serious Girardian scholarship has identified several tensions in *Violence and the Sacred* that subsequent work (including Girard's own) has tried to address:

- **Empirical falsifiability.** Girard's anthropological hypothesis is extraordinarily broad. Critics have asked whether any conceivable evidence could falsify it — and whether a theory that explains everything explains anything.
- **The scope of mimetic desire.** Is *all* desire (beyond basic biological needs) mimetic? Or only some? Girard's strong claim is often read as overreaching.
- **Proxies for founding violence.** The founding murder is, by hypothesis, pre-historical and pre-literate. The evidence for it must be reconstructed from myth, ritual, and textual traces. Critics have asked whether this reconstruction is genuinely evidentiary.
- **The status of women.** The book's scapegoat mechanism is articulated in largely male-subject terms. Later Girardian scholarship (and feminist critique) has asked what happens to the theory when gender is taken seriously as a structural dimension of mimetic rivalry.
