# Thiel's Writings — Corpus Overview

### An annotated bibliography with links to the vault's text deep-dives

[Peter Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) is an unusually prolific business figure when it comes to philosophical writing. His corpus spans two proper books, a canonical essay in a Girardian festschrift volume, several high-circulation short essays, countless interviews and lectures, and the undergraduate paper trail of *The Stanford Review*. This note catalogs the corpus and points to the vault's individual deep-dives for each major text.

This is the Thiel-side parallel to how [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s corpus is organized around two primary texts ([the dissertation](karp-dissertation-aggression-in-lifeworld.md) and [*The Technological Republic*](technological-republic.md)). Thiel has a larger and more varied corpus; this overview is a reading order and a navigation map.

## The major works — with vault deep-dives

### Books

#### *The Diversity Myth: 'Multiculturalism' and the Politics of Intolerance at Stanford* (1995)

Co-authored with David O. Sacks. Published by Independent Institute. The Stanford-years book documenting the 1986–1988 Western Civ controversy and the formation of early Thiel's political sensibility. Pre-Girardian, Bloom-inflected, legalistic in register. The clearest primary-source document of what Thiel was doing at Stanford before his business career.

→ Deep-dive: [The Diversity Myth (1995) Deep Dive](thiel-diversity-myth.md)

#### *Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future* (2014)

With Blake Masters. Published by Crown Business. Thiel's mass-market book, adapted from his Stanford CS183 course. A #1 NYT bestseller translated into ~30 languages; the most-cited book in Silicon Valley founder discourse. Popular-register Girardianism (though Girard is rarely named); the book whose "competition is for losers" and "zero to one" framings entered business-strategy vocabulary.

→ Deep-dive: [Zero to One (2014) Deep Dive](thiel-zero-to-one.md)

### The canonical essay

#### "The Straussian Moment" (2004)

Published as chapter 6 in *Politics and Apocalypse*, edited by Robert Hamerton-Kelly (Michigan State University Press, Studies in Violence, Mimesis, and Culture series). Thiel's most philosophically serious piece of writing. A ~30-page synthesis of [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md), [Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md), and [Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md) as a diagnosis of post-9/11 modernity. Written for a Girardian scholarly audience; not widely read outside that circle, but the interpretive key to the rest of his corpus. **Start here for serious philosophical engagement with Thiel.**

→ Deep-dive: [The Straussian Moment (2004) Deep Dive](thiel-straussian-moment.md)

### Short-form essays (combined deep-dive)

Three short essays from 2009–2011 that together articulate Thiel's political, civilizational, and philosophical commitments in public-facing register:

- **"The Education of a Libertarian"** (*Cato Unbound*, April 2009) — the famous "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible" essay. Thiel's most cited political statement.
- **"The End of the Future"** (*National Review*, October 2011) — the technological stagnation thesis in mature form, predating *Zero to One*.
- **Foreword to *Mimesis and Science*** (Michigan State University Press, 2011) — the public commitment to the [Girardian research program](../schools/girardian-school.md) as a disciple, aligned with his 2007 funding of Imitatio.

→ Deep-dive: [Thiel's Essays — Education of a Libertarian, End of the Future, Mimesis Foreword (Deep Dive)](thiel-essays.md)

## The rest of the corpus (not yet individually deep-dived)

These are shorter pieces, public speeches, interviews, and curated materials. Several would merit their own deep-dives if the vault expands further; none has been given one yet.

### Op-eds and shorter essays (chronological)

- **"The Education of a Libertarian"** (2009) — *covered above*
- **"Thinking Too Highly of Higher Education"** (2010) — Forbes essay; prefigures the Thiel Fellowship (launched 2010/2011) and the critique of credential-based higher education
- **"The End of the Future"** (2011) — *covered above*
- **Foreword to *Mimesis and Science*** (2011) — *covered above*
- **"Good for Google, Bad for America"** (2019) — *New York Times* op-ed arguing that Google's AI work with China was compromising American interests
- **"The Straussian Moment" reprintings** — the essay has been reprinted and excerpted in subsequent Girardian volumes

### Interviews and lectures (as documentary sources)

The interview/lecture record is extensive and philosophically substantive. Key venues include:

- **Stanford CS183 (Spring 2012)** — "Computer Science 183: Startup." The course from which *Zero to One* was adapted. Blake Masters's class notes circulated widely before becoming the book.
- **Hoover Institution / Uncommon Knowledge** — recurring long-form interviews with Peter Robinson covering philosophy, business strategy, and politics. Especially valuable for Thiel's public engagement with contemporary political questions.
- **Commonwealth Club of California** — multiple appearances; often the venue for Thiel's bigger public statements
- **Manhattan Institute, Federalist Society, various university lectures** — conservative-intellectual institutional circuit
- **Tyler Cowen conversations** — the "Conversations with Tyler" episodes are dense and philosophical
- **Jordan Peterson, Joe Rogan, Lex Fridman podcasts** — longer-form; mixed philosophical content
- **The Oxford Union debate (2013)** — a formative public statement of the stagnation thesis
- **The 2016 Republican National Convention speech** — one of the most politically consequential speeches Thiel has ever given; the first major Silicon Valley endorsement of Trump
- **The 2025 Commonwealth Club "Antichrist" lectures** — recent cycle of lectures explicitly unpacking the late-Girardian apocalyptic register in Thiel's thought

A future deep-dive synthesis note on the interview/lecture corpus would be worthwhile; it's not yet written.

### *The Stanford Review* (1987–1989 and ongoing)

The conservative student newspaper Thiel founded in 1987. Thiel wrote and edited actively during his undergraduate and law-school years (1987–1992). The archive is the primary source for understanding Thiel's undergraduate formation.

Key themes of Thiel's early *Review* writing:
- Defense of Western Civilization curriculum
- Critique of campus political correctness
- Free-speech and due-process arguments
- Early libertarian political economy

The *Stanford Review* as an institution continues to publish; the alumni network has produced many figures now central to Silicon Valley and Washington (David Sacks, Keith Rabois, Joe Lonsdale, and others adjacent).

### Forewords and introductions (miscellaneous)

- **Foreword to Blake Masters's edited *Zero to One* notes** — technically integrated into the book itself
- **Forewords to various friends' and collaborators' books** — scattered across the publishing record; not individually significant

### Palantir S-1 filing (2020)

When Palantir filed to go public via direct listing in September 2020, the S-1 included a strikingly philosophical founders' letter (co-signed by Thiel and Karp). The letter is sometimes treated as a Thiel text but is substantially Karp's register; see the [*Technological Republic*](technological-republic.md) deep-dive for analysis of Karp's Palantir-letter voice. A future note could trace how Thiel's contributions to Palantir corporate documents (where distinguishable) track his philosophical positions.

## What Thiel's corpus tells us

### The Girardian register is everywhere

Every major Thiel text is intelligible as a Girardian document, once you know to look. *The Straussian Moment* names the framework; *Zero to One* deploys it in business register; the *Mimesis and Science* foreword confirms it publicly; the Cato essay on libertarianism takes mimetic-theory assumptions about democracy for granted. Without Girard, Thiel is hard to read coherently; with Girard, the whole corpus organizes itself.

### The Straussian register is almost everywhere

Thiel writes in layers. The mass-market books look accessible but have esoteric depths; the scholarly essay is explicitly esoteric. This is a distinctive posture — the mature Thiel's rhetorical mode is [Straussian](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md) even when the subject matter isn't.

### The Schmittian register appears under pressure

Thiel's writing on democracy, enmity, and crisis politics consistently uses Schmittian vocabulary (the state of exception, friend/enemy, political theology), but he rarely endorses Schmitt's own politics. The Schmittian frame functions as diagnostic — this is how modern politics actually works — but not as prescription.

### The Christian register becomes more explicit over time

The 1995 *Diversity Myth* is essentially secular. *The Straussian Moment* (2004) is explicitly Christian-theological. *Zero to One* (2014) is secular in mode but Girardian-Christian in substrate. The 2025 "Antichrist" lecture cycle is the most openly theological Thiel has been in public. There is a clear through-line from a Hoover-libertarian young Thiel to a more theologically-committed mature Thiel.

### The libertarian register softens

Thiel's undergraduate and early-career libertarianism — Cato-Institute, Rand-reading, Stanford-Review libertarianism — persists but is steadily qualified over time. The 2009 Cato essay is the pivot point ("I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible"). Post-2009, Thiel writes less as a libertarian and more as a critic of modernity from a theological-political perspective. The libertarian institutional affiliations remain, but the philosophical register has moved.
