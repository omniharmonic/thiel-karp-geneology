# The Straussian Moment

## A deep dive on Thiel's 2004 synthesis of Girard, Strauss, and Schmitt

**Full title:** "The Straussian Moment"
**Author:** [Peter Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)
**Published:** 2004, as chapter 6 in *Politics and Apocalypse*, edited by Robert Hamerton-Kelly (Michigan State University Press, Studies in Violence, Mimesis, and Culture series)
**Length:** approximately 30 pages in published form
**Origin:** delivered at the Stanford Girardian colloquium circle run by Hamerton-Kelly (Colloquium on Violence and Religion / Stanford Bass seminar series), then published in the edited volume

"The Straussian Moment" is the single most philosophically serious piece of writing [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) has published. Every other document in his corpus — *Zero to One*, the Cato "Education of a Libertarian" essay, the Hoover interviews, the 2020s political speeches — flows downstream of the framework articulated here. The essay is Thiel's attempt to synthesize his three central influences ([Girard](../people/rene-girard.md), [Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md), [Schmitt](../people/carl-schmitt.md)) into a single diagnosis of post-9/11 modernity. Without a careful reading of this essay, Thiel's subsequent public speech sounds like assorted reactionary gestures. With it, those gestures become the legible surface of a worked-through position.

---

## Bibliographic context and institutional setting

The essay sits inside a specific institutional lineage that the standard biographical treatments of Thiel tend to flatten. *Politics and Apocalypse* (MSU Press 2007; the 2004 date refers to the conference and the essay's composition) is volume ten in the Studies in Violence, Mimesis, and Culture series — the flagship book series of the Colloquium on Violence and Religion (COV&R), the international Girardian scholarly society founded in 1990. The series is edited under the sign of mimetic theory and gathers contributions from the Girardian scholarly community worldwide.

Robert Hamerton-Kelly (1938–2013), the volume's editor, was a Methodist theologian and dean of the Stanford Memorial Church who ran a long-standing Girardian faculty seminar at Stanford (sometimes called the Stanford Bass seminar after its Bass family funding). Hamerton-Kelly had been a close colleague of [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)'s since the 1980s and had published his own work (notably *Sacred Violence*, 1992, a Girardian reading of Paul) in the same series. The seminar drew Stanford faculty, graduate students, and selected interlocutors; Thiel had been a participant since his undergraduate years in the late 1980s when Girard was still actively teaching.

The 2004 conference that produced the volume took the theme of *apocalypse* seriously in both the Girardian and the geopolitical senses. The Girardian sense comes from [late apocalyptic Girard](girard-apocalyptic-turn.md) — the Girard of the post-1999 work who had increasingly framed modernity itself as approaching an eschatological limit at which the scapegoat mechanism's failure would leave humanity without its traditional containment for mimetic violence. The geopolitical sense comes from the post-9/11 moment: the conference took place during the early years of the Iraq War, when the possibility of large-scale civilizational confrontation had become a live intellectual object rather than a Cold War historical memory.

Thiel is an unusual contributor to this volume. The other essayists are professional theologians, philosophers, and religious-studies scholars — the kind of readers who read *Les Origines de la culture* in its original language and argue about whether Girard is more indebted to Durkheim or to Freud. Thiel, at the time of the essay's composition, was known (to the extent he was known publicly at all) as the co-founder of PayPal, which had been acquired by eBay in late 2002; Palantir had been founded only in 2003 and was not yet publicly visible. His positioning in the volume is that of a former Girard student who had gone into business and had something philosophical to say about the moment — a rare posture, and one that partly accounts for the essay's peculiar rhetorical mode. It is written by an insider to the Girardian circle who is also an outsider to academic philosophy, which gives the essay a certain freedom from professional-philosophical constraints even as it sometimes lets it indulge assertions that a more disciplined writer would have qualified.

## The essay's central problematic

The essay opens from a diagnosis that organizes everything that follows: modern liberalism possesses no intellectual resources to comprehend the violence it faces after 9/11. The West's dominant political self-understanding — the Enlightenment liberalism of Hobbes, Locke, Kant, and their 20th-century heirs — has worked for several centuries on the premise that the theological-political question could be privatized. Religion was to be a matter of personal conscience; politics was to be a matter of procedural legitimacy, constitutional limits, and the mutual recognition of rights. The great achievement of modern political philosophy, on this self-understanding, was to take the fire out of politics — to replace wars of religion with parliamentary debate, sacred violence with due process, theological absolutes with liberal accommodation.

Thiel's claim is that this achievement has now encountered an adversary it cannot describe. The jihadist movements that attacked the United States in September 2001 were not acting within the liberal frame. They took the theological-political question not merely seriously but primarily. Liberal modernity's response to such an adversary is structurally confused: the right wants to meet it with military force framed (somewhat incoherently) in the register of universal human rights; the left wants to meet it with dialogue, development, and pluralist tolerance framed in the register of procedural liberalism. Neither response, in Thiel's reading, comes to grips with what has actually happened, because neither can think outside the framework that presumes the theological-political question has been settled.

The problematic is thus Schmittian in structure but delivered with a slightly different emphasis than Schmitt himself would have given. Schmitt's charge was that liberal parliamentarism evades the political by dissolving it into discussion. Thiel's charge is that liberal modernity evades the *theological* substrate of the political — that it has produced a civilization fluent in the vocabulary of rights and procedures but illiterate in the vocabulary of sacred violence, sacrifice, and apocalyptic confrontation. The consequence is not merely that liberalism is in bad faith (Schmitt's charge) but that it is in danger: it has disarmed itself intellectually at exactly the moment when an intellectual disarmament is most costly.

## The Straussian frame

The essay's title points to its most explicit theoretical anchor: [Leo Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md). Thiel reads Strauss as the 20th-century philosopher who most clearly diagnosed the internal instability of liberal modernity and who preserved, against the grain of professional political philosophy, a serious engagement with the theological-political question. Three Straussian theses carry the weight of the framing.

The first is Strauss's account of modernity as a *lowering of the horizon*. Pre-modern political thought (Plato, Aristotle, the medievals) took the central question of politics to be the cultivation of virtue and the orientation of the polity toward the good. Modern political thought — beginning with Machiavelli and systematized by Hobbes — replaced this with a narrower question: how to secure life, property, and comfort against the threat of violent death. The horizon descends from the cultivation of the best to the preservation of the minimum. This is not an incidental shift of emphasis. For Strauss, and for Thiel following him, it is the foundational move of modernity: the willful forgetting of the question of the good in favor of a politics of security. The forgetting makes modern politics feel clean and efficient. It also makes it incapable of recognizing adversaries who have not forgotten.

The second is Strauss's periodization of modernity into *three waves*. The first wave (Machiavelli, Hobbes) lowers the horizon and grounds politics in passion and self-interest. The second wave (Rousseau, Kant) attempts to recover freedom and morality from within the lowered horizon, producing the modern idea of autonomous rational self-legislation. The third wave (Nietzsche, Heidegger) recognizes that the second wave's recovery fails — that the autonomous rational subject is itself a theological remainder that cannot be sustained once its theological backing has been denied — and pushes modernity toward nihilism, historicism, and the confrontation with the abyss. Thiel takes this periodization substantially as Strauss leaves it: modernity is not a single stable achievement but a sequence of increasingly desperate attempts to maintain the original lowering of the horizon against the internal pressure of its own incompleteness.

The third is Strauss's claim that the forgetting of the theological-political question is not a neutral achievement but a symptom of liberalism's own instability. The question does not go away because modern philosophers have stopped asking it. It returns — in Romanticism, in revolutionary politics, in fascism, in political Islam — and each return finds liberalism poorly prepared. For Strauss this is a ground for philosophical seriousness: the philosopher's job is to keep the question alive, even when the public culture has decided that it has been settled. Thiel accepts this diagnosis without hedging. The essay is, in this respect, a straightforwardly Straussian performance: a reader trained in the ancients and in the Weimar crisis finds, in the post-9/11 moment, a return of the question the liberal consensus had declared closed.

## The Schmittian frame

Alongside the Straussian frame runs a [Schmittian](../people/carl-schmitt.md) one, and the essay oscillates between the two in ways that reward careful reading. Three Schmittian elements organize Thiel's diagnosis.

The first is the [friend/enemy distinction](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md) as the definition of the political. Schmitt's claim in *The Concept of the Political* (1932) was that the specifically political distinction — the one that identifies an action or conflict as political rather than as economic, moral, or aesthetic — is the distinction between friend and enemy in the existential sense. The enemy is not a criminal or a competitor or an immoral person; the enemy is the other whose mode of existence is sufficiently at odds with our own that, in the limit case, combat is possible. The political, on Schmitt's view, cannot be abolished because this distinction cannot be abolished. Liberalism's attempt to dissolve the political into economics (the market) or ethics (universal human rights) is the attempt to pretend that the friend/enemy distinction has gone away. It has not; it has merely been displaced.

Thiel accepts this diagnosis. The post-9/11 moment, in his reading, is precisely a case in which the friend/enemy distinction has reasserted itself against a liberal self-understanding that had forgotten it. The jihadist adversary has decided that the West is its enemy in the existential sense. The liberal response, to the extent that it refuses to acknowledge this, is not more peaceful than Schmittian politics but more confused — politics conducted as if it were not politics.

The second is Schmitt's political-theological thesis: *all significant concepts of the modern theory of the state are secularized theological concepts*. (See [Schmitt's Political Theology](schmitt-political-theology.md) for the full context.) Modern sovereignty inherits from divine omnipotence; the exception inherits from the miracle; the general will inherits from providence. The modern state is not, as the liberal self-understanding claims, a neutral procedural apparatus built from rational premises. It is a historically specific transposition of a theological vocabulary whose theological substrate has been partly repressed and partly forgotten. The transposition works only so long as the substrate holds. When the substrate is stressed — in a genuine crisis, in an encounter with an adversary who has not forgotten — the transposition becomes visible as what it is.

The third is Schmitt's critique of liberal parliamentarism as a degenerated *discussion-class* that has lost the capacity for decision. This line, inherited from Donoso Cortés via Schmitt's 1923 *Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy*, enters Thiel's essay as a diagnosis of the West's incapacity to respond coherently to the post-9/11 challenge. Parliaments deliberate; committees consult; academics refine their positions. Meanwhile the adversary, who has already decided, acts.

Thiel's relationship to Schmitt in the essay is diagnostic but not prescriptive. He accepts Schmitt's account of what liberalism has been hiding from itself. He does not accept Schmitt's recommendation that the solution is a more robust sovereign decision within the friend/enemy framework. A straightforwardly Schmittian response to 9/11 would be sovereign decisionism — an executive that identifies the enemy, suspends the relevant legal scruples, and pursues existential confrontation. Thiel's essay flirts with this reading without quite endorsing it. It takes the Schmittian diagnosis to be correct and then turns, in its second movement, to argue that the Schmittian prescription is not the best available response — that there is another, less dangerous way of taking the diagnosis seriously. This is the Girardian move.

## The Girardian solution

The essay's most distinctive philosophical contribution is the attempt to relieve Schmitt's diagnosis with [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)'s anthropology. [Mimetic theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md) provides, on Thiel's reading, the anthropological foundation that Schmitt both needed and lacked.

Girard's thesis, developed across *Deceit, Desire, and the Novel* (1961), *Violence and the Sacred* (1972), and *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World* (1978), is that human desire is mimetic — we learn what to want by copying others — and that mimetic desire, once it spreads, generates rivalries that tend to escalate into generalized violence. Archaic societies discovered an unconscious mechanism for short-circuiting such crises: the [scapegoat mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md). A community in mimetic crisis converges, as if by accident, on a single victim whose death or expulsion restores peace. The peace so produced is genuine but built on a founding murder that is misrecognized as a sacred act. Ritual, myth, and prohibition are the cultural sedimentations of this mechanism. Religion, for Girard, is what the scapegoat mechanism leaves behind.

Two consequences follow. The first is that politics, when understood anthropologically, is always already concerned with the management of mimetic violence — which is to say, always already concerned with the sacred. Schmitt had intuited this in his secularization thesis, but he had treated the theological as a fact about the intellectual history of sovereignty rather than as a fact about the anthropological foundation of social cohesion. Girard's mimetic theory supplies the missing substrate: it explains *why* political concepts have theological structure, and it does so at a depth that the intellectual-historical account cannot reach.

The second is that the Hebrew Bible and especially the Gospels occupy a unique structural position in human culture. These texts, alone among the great religious traditions, narrate the scapegoat mechanism from the side of the victim rather than from the side of the persecuting community. The passion of Christ is, on Girard's reading, the archetypal scapegoat event told with the scapegoat declared innocent. This narration destabilizes the mechanism: once you have read the Gospels with open eyes, you can no longer participate in scapegoating without knowing what you are doing. Christianity, in Girard's reading, is not one religion among others but the unmasking of the religious — the revelation that exposes the sacred violence at the root of culture.

Thiel's move is to use this Girardian unmasking to answer Schmitt. Schmitt was correct that liberal modernity has repressed the theological-political substrate and that the substrate will reassert itself in moments of crisis. But the Schmittian response — sovereign decision, existential confrontation with the enemy, the restoration of the friend/enemy distinction — reinstates the sacrificial logic that the Gospel has revealed. If one takes the Gospels seriously, one cannot answer the return of sacred violence with more sacred violence. Christianity, read through Girard, is the only tradition that can hold the Schmittian diagnosis without collapsing into sacrificial politics. The Christian identification with the victim functions as an alternative to the Schmittian identification of the enemy.

This is the essay's distinctive philosophical move and the single most important thing a reader should carry away from it. The essay is not a neoconservative argument for a stronger response to jihadism; it is not a liberal argument for dialogue and development; it is a Girardian-Christian argument that the post-9/11 moment demands a politics able to see the sacrificial mechanism at work on all sides — in the adversary, in the West's response, in the civilizational framing of the conflict itself.

## The apocalyptic register

What makes the essay philosophically interesting rather than merely an exercise in framework integration is that Thiel does not offer the Girardian move as an easy solution. The essay is written in an apocalyptic register that is often underemphasized in secondary readings that focus only on the Strauss–Schmitt–Girard synthesis.

The apocalyptic worry is a late-Girardian one (see [late apocalyptic Girard](girard-apocalyptic-turn.md)). The Gospel's unmasking of the scapegoat mechanism has not produced a more peaceful world. It has made the mechanism less effective — modern societies have increasing difficulty believing in the sacred innocence of their persecutions — but this failure of the mechanism has not been replaced by a stable non-sacrificial order. What has happened instead is that mimetic conflicts now have less effective containment, and the violence therefore tends toward escalation rather than toward sacrificial resolution. Modernity is not post-sacrificial; it is, in Girard's later framing, inadequately-sacrificial, which is a more dangerous condition than the archaic societies that sacrificed confidently and were stabilized by it.

Thiel inherits this worry and extends it. The essay's references to the figure of the *Antichrist* are the most rhetorically charged and philosophically revealing of its moves. In the Pauline and Johannine texts, the Antichrist is the eschatological figure who imitates Christ while inverting his meaning — who appropriates the Christian vocabulary of victimhood, inclusion, and human rights in order to use that vocabulary against Christianity and against the very restraint-functions that the old sacred violence previously provided. Modern egalitarian ideologies, Thiel suggests, occupy this structural position. They weaponize Christian themes against Christianity; they produce a world in which everyone is a victim, no one is a perpetrator, and no coherent response to actual violence remains available.

This is the essay's most politically fraught claim and the one most likely to be misread. Thiel is not making the crude conservative argument that progressive victim-talk is bad. He is making the much stranger Girardian argument that the progressive vocabulary is Christian in origin and has become dangerous precisely because it has been detached from the Christian metaphysics that gave it its point. A Christian victim-identification makes sense inside a theology that maintains the reality of evil and the possibility of redemption. A secularized victim-identification, cut loose from that theology, produces an ever-proliferating set of victim claims that no longer has the capacity to distinguish the scapegoat from the perpetrator or to organize a response to genuine aggression.

The apocalyptic tone of the essay is the tone of someone who believes the containment mechanisms of modernity are failing and that the consequences of that failure will be large. The post-9/11 moment is offered as early evidence of the failure rather than as a discrete crisis that better management could resolve. This is a darker reading of the present than either neoconservatism or liberal internationalism typically offer, and it is delivered in a register closer to theology than to policy.

## The 9/11 frame and the rejection of available responses

The essay's specifically post-9/11 context enters in the form of an extended critique of the two most visible Western responses to the attacks. Neither, in Thiel's reading, is adequate.

The right-wing response — the neoconservative project of democracy promotion through military action, articulated most visibly in the early Bush-administration doctrine — fails because it misdiagnoses the adversary. It treats jihadism as a local pathology that can be corrected by transplanting liberal institutions and thereby giving people what they really want. This misses the possibility that what the adversary wants is precisely not the lowering of the horizon that liberal institutions embody. The neoconservative response is also sacrificial in the Girardian sense: it organizes the political community by identifying an enemy, it requires a rhetorical construction of the enemy as monstrous, and it produces a founding narrative in which the violence against the enemy restores the political community to its proper self-understanding. The essay does not endorse the neoconservative response even as it acknowledges that the neoconservative response has more reality to it than its liberal critics want to admit.

The left-wing response — the liberal-humanitarian frame in which jihadist violence is read as a symptom of inequality, colonial aftermath, and underdevelopment, and the recommended response is dialogue, aid, and multilateralism — fails for a different reason. It refuses to take the theological-political dimension of the conflict seriously at all. It treats the adversary as if the adversary's self-understanding were a false consciousness concealing the real economic grievances. But the adversary's theological commitments are not false consciousness; they are the operative commitments. A response that refuses to engage those commitments engages shadows while the substance continues to act.

Thiel's positioning is neither. Neither neoconservative triumphalism nor liberal pacifism can diagnose the situation, because both are variants of the liberal forgetting of the theological-political question. Only a position that has first recovered the theological-political question — via Strauss — and then re-metabolized it through the Girardian anthropology — via the Gospel's unmasking of the scapegoat — can see the situation clearly. The essay does not offer this position as a policy. It offers it as a mode of reading.

## The essay's rhetorical mode

One further thing to say about the essay is that its rhetorical register is itself a philosophical object worth analyzing. The essay is written, at least in part, in the [Straussian esoteric register](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md): it says some things plainly to readers who can hear them and evasively to everyone else. This is not conspiratorial; it is a specific literary technique that Strauss recovered from his readings of Plato, Maimonides, and Spinoza and that Thiel clearly learned from the Straussian-Bloomian inheritance.

Several paragraphs in the essay are cryptic to the point of opacity on a first reading. The references to the Antichrist, the sections on the failure of the containment mechanisms, the final Girardian-Christian appeal — none of these is spelled out with the kind of argumentative scaffolding a professional philosopher would supply. They are offered as pointers for readers already inside the relevant traditions (Girardian, Straussian, Christian theological) and left underexplained for readers who are not. The essay is also ambiguous about Thiel's own position in several places. He articulates the Schmittian diagnosis with a sympathy that sometimes looks like endorsement; he articulates the Girardian response with a commitment that sometimes looks more aesthetic than theological. It is possible to read the essay as a conservative-Christian essay, as a Straussian-esoteric essay, or as a Girardian essay, and different careful readers have given different answers.

The essay closes not with a political program but with a Girardian-Christian appeal. The final movement is not "here is what the United States should do" but "here is how the situation should be *read*." The essay is an exercise in recovering a certain kind of seeing before a certain kind of action. This is in keeping with the Straussian premise that the philosopher's job is not to rule but to see, and to keep alive the seeing that public discourse has foreclosed.

## Reception and influence

"The Straussian Moment" has had an unusual reception trajectory. In mainstream political theory journals it has been barely cited; it was published in a specialized volume in a series most political theorists do not read, and its author, at the time of publication, had no standing as a political theorist. Within the Girardian scholarly community it has been noted with interest and occasional discomfort — discomfort because Thiel's subsequent political trajectory has made the essay's claims sound (to hostile readers) like a manifesto for a politics the Girardian community has not chosen. Book-length and essay-length Girardian responses exist but remain scattered across the specialist literature.

The essay's larger influence has been as a touchstone text for readers trying to understand Thiel's philosophical commitments. In this role it has functioned more as cult reading than as canonical argument. Attentive readers of Thiel's later interviews, speeches, and books have often pointed to "The Straussian Moment" as the frame that makes the later material legible. Journalistic profiles of Thiel that treat him seriously as an intellectual figure almost always reference the essay; profiles that treat him primarily as a business figure rarely do. The essay has, in other words, become the discriminating text — the document one can use to sort the readings of Thiel that have taken the philosophical commitments seriously from the readings that have treated him as a conservative tech billionaire with idiosyncratic tastes.

A secondary line of reception runs through the Straussian political-theory community proper — the milieu around the [Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md)-influenced departments and journals. Here the essay has been read with some ambivalence: it is Straussian in tone and in its use of Strauss's periodization, but its Girardian resolution is not a standard Straussian move. Classical Straussians tend to read Strauss as an invitation to sustained philosophical inquiry rather than as a pathway to a particular theological commitment. Thiel's essay takes the Straussian diagnosis and lands in an explicit Christian-Girardian place that more orthodox Straussians would hesitate to take.

## Relevance to Karp

The essay is, from the opposite vantage, the single clearest articulation of the position that [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s Habermasian training was built to refute. The [Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md) of *Between Facts and Norms* (1992) had undertaken to show that popular sovereignty and human rights could be co-originally grounded in the structure of communicative action, without requiring the theological substrate Schmitt and now Thiel argue is ineliminable. The Habermasian project is precisely the most rigorous post-war attempt to answer the Schmittian diagnosis on philosophical grounds and to defend a post-metaphysical grounding of democratic legitimacy. Karp's doctoral work (see [Aggression in der Lebenswelt](karp-dissertation-aggression-in-lifeworld.md)) was completed inside this framework.

If one wants to see the specific shape of what Karp disagrees with Thiel about, "The Straussian Moment" is the document to read. The disagreement is not primarily about policy; it is about the adequacy of liberal-procedural legitimacy to the situations it must handle. Thiel argues, with Strauss and Schmitt, that the liberal-procedural frame is insufficient in extremity and that a more honest engagement with the theological-political substrate is required. Karp argues, with Habermas, that the liberal-procedural frame can be defended precisely because it does not depend on any theological substrate — that its legitimacy comes from the discursive structure of communicative action itself. The fact that Thiel and Karp co-direct Palantir while holding positions this far apart is not an accident that the firm has survived; it is a condition of the firm's intellectual texture, and "The Straussian Moment" is the document that makes one side of that texture visible.

## Continuing resonance

The essay's themes show up throughout Thiel's subsequent public speech. The *Zero to One* thesis that competition is mimetic and therefore inferior to monopoly is the business-register translation of the Girardian anthropology the essay assumed. The 2009 Cato "Education of a Libertarian" essay — in which Thiel argued that he no longer believed freedom and democracy were compatible — is a direct descendant of the Schmittian strand here, made sharper and more public. His 2016 support for Trump, his 2020s funding of the new conservative institutional infrastructure (the Edmund Burke Foundation, Vance's political career, the neoreactionary-adjacent thinkers), and his increasingly explicit Christian self-positioning in public lectures all flow from commitments first articulated in this essay.

The connection is not that Thiel has been applying the essay as a playbook. It is that the essay is the most fully worked-through articulation of the framework from which his subsequent actions have been legible. When he speaks of the "decadent" West, of civilizational stakes, of the need for founders who can see past mimetic consensus, of the reality of evil, of the Antichrist figure in modern political ideology — he is speaking from inside the frame this essay constructs. Readers who ignore the essay and try to read the later material in isolation get a series of idiosyncratic provocations. Readers who know the essay get a worked-through, if contestable, philosophical position.

## Critical engagement

Any serious engagement with "The Straussian Moment" has to register both its strengths and its weaknesses.

Its strengths are real. It is philosophically serious in a register that most business-world writing is not. It takes the theological-political questions seriously rather than treating them as historical curiosities. It bridges three intellectual traditions — Strauss, Schmitt, Girard — that most readers keep separate, and the bridging is not mechanical; it is argued. It recovers a set of questions that professional political theory had largely foreclosed and does so in a tone that takes the questions' difficulty seriously rather than attempting to resolve them prematurely.

Its weaknesses are also real. The Girard it uses is selective. The Girard Thiel deploys in the essay is the early-to-mid Girard of *Violence and the Sacred* and *Things Hidden*, read as a Christian anthropologist; the specifically late, almost-apocalyptic Girard of *Battling to the End* (2007) appears only in anticipatory form here, and the later business-strategy applications that *Zero to One* develops are not yet present. The Christian resolution is under-developed. The essay gestures at a Christian alternative to Schmittian politics without doing the theological work of explaining what that alternative would look like as a political form rather than as a mode of seeing. The relationship between *diagnosis* and *prescription* is ambiguous in ways that have allowed the essay to be read in substantially different directions by different readers: some read it as a Christian-pacifist document, others as a proto-reactionary manifesto, others as a Girardian essay in civilizational analysis with no political prescription at all. The ambiguity is partly deliberate (the esoteric Straussian register permits it) and partly, one suspects, a function of the essay not being fully resolved even in its author's mind.

A further critical point: the essay's treatment of its own political moment is historically specific in ways that subsequent events have complicated. The post-9/11 framing of the confrontation between the West and political Islam has, in the two decades since the essay was written, been displaced by other organizing conflicts — great-power competition with China, the internal polarization of Western democracies, the pandemic and its political aftermath. The essay's diagnostic framework has shown considerable adaptability — many of the themes map onto these later conflicts as well — but the specific post-9/11 frame of the original argument now reads as dated in a way the framework itself does not.

A final debate, and perhaps the most productive one: is "The Straussian Moment" a conservative-Christian essay, a Straussian-esoteric essay, or a Girardian essay? Different readers have given different answers. The most careful reading is probably that it is all three, held in an intentional tension that the essay itself does not resolve. It is conservative-Christian in its final appeal; Straussian-esoteric in its rhetorical mode; Girardian in its anthropological substrate. The integration is not stable — it generates productive tensions rather than a synthesized position — and the essay's continuing interest may have less to do with its resolved conclusions than with the tensions it chooses to make visible.

## A caveat on interpretation

The essay is short, philosophically dense, and written partly in an esoteric register that deliberately resists single-reading summary. Any deep-dive note — this one included — is an interpretive reconstruction. Readers who want to work with the argument seriously should read the essay directly (it is not long) and form their own judgments about which of the competing readings — Christian-conservative, Straussian-esoteric, Girardian-civilizational — the text will bear. The interpretation offered here tries to hold all three together, because the essay itself does, but readers who emphasize one reading over the others can defend that emphasis with textual support. This is, for a text of this kind, a feature rather than a defect.
