## The Strauss–Kojève Debate

### A deep dive on one of the great 20th-century philosophical exchanges

**Primary text:** *On Tyranny* (Leo Strauss, with commentary by [Alexandre Kojève](../people/alexandre-kojeve.md), first published 1948; expanded 1954 edition with Kojève's "Tyranny and Wisdom"; corrected/expanded edition ed. Victor Gourevitch and Michael Roth, 1991)

The exchange between [Leo Strauss](../people/leo-strauss.md) and Alexandre Kojève on Xenophon's dialogue *Hiero* (or *Tyrannicus*) is one of the most important philosophical conversations of the 20th century. The two men were friends; they disagreed at the deepest possible level; they argued in print, through letters, and (across an extensive correspondence) in private for decades. The 1948/1954 *On Tyranny* is their most public confrontation.

The debate is relevant to this vault because it stages the core philosophical choice that Thiel's tradition must make — and because Kojève's position in the debate is the one that shapes Francis Fukuyama's *End of History* and, through a further transmission, some of the intellectual framing of post-Cold-War liberal democracy that both Thiel and Karp inhabit.

---

## What Xenophon's *Hiero* is

A short Socratic dialogue by Xenophon (4th century BCE). The tyrant Hiero of Syracuse and the wise poet Simonides meet. Simonides asks Hiero whether it is better to be a tyrant or a private man. Hiero unexpectedly insists that the tyrant's life is miserable — isolated, paranoid, unable to enjoy friendship, love, or any genuine human good. Simonides then offers counsel on how the tyrant might govern well, benefit his people, and earn honor.

The dialogue is short, strange, and almost always neglected. Strauss's 1948 book *On Tyranny* reads it closely and argues it is one of the most important texts in classical political philosophy.

---

## Strauss's reading

For Strauss, *Hiero* is a Socratic political teaching disguised as a conversation with a tyrant. The teaching:

1. **Tyranny is bad for the tyrant.** The Socratic-Platonic claim that injustice damages the soul of the unjust is enacted in Hiero's misery.
2. **Wisdom stands apart from political power.** Simonides is the wise man who *could* tell the tyrant how to govern well, but the wise man does not *become* the tyrant. Wisdom preserves itself by a kind of withdrawal from rule.
3. **The philosophical life is the happy life.** The contemplative life is higher than the political life, even at its most magnificent. This is the classical hierarchy Strauss wants to recover.
4. **Esoterically read, the dialogue shows more.** On close reading, Simonides' counsel to Hiero is doubtful, ironic, even subversive — one cannot govern well as a tyrant; the advice is a dignified concealment of this impossibility.

The Straussian Xenophon is part of Strauss's broader recovery of classical political philosophy against modernity.

---

## Kojève's reply ("Tyranny and Wisdom," 1950)

Kojève's response, published as a long essay and included in the 1954 edition, accepts almost nothing of Strauss's reading. His counter-thesis:

1. **Tyranny and wisdom are not in opposition.** Historical action requires both; the sage who refuses to participate in rule merely delegates the task of actualizing wisdom to less wise rulers.
2. **The philosopher must engage power.** Kojève, himself a senior French economic bureaucrat, argued this as philosophy and lived it as practice. Wisdom that does not become historically actual is wisdom that fails on its own terms.
3. **History has a direction.** Kojève's Hegelian view: history is the working-out of the [struggle for recognition](../concepts/master-slave-dialectic.md) toward universal and homogeneous state. The philosopher-sage cooperates with this direction; the philosopher who withdraws is, on Hegelian grounds, a romantic or a reactionary.
4. **The wise tyrant is possible.** Not the actual tyrants of history, but the figure of the ruler who implements wisdom is philosophically coherent. Alexander the Great, Napoleon, and (by suggestion) modern bureaucratic-administrative executives are candidates.

### The contrast

- **Strauss:** philosophy and politics are in irreducible tension; the philosopher lives apart, preserves questioning, resists the temptation to rule
- **Kojève:** philosophy and politics must be united in history; the sage who refuses engagement abandons philosophy to merely private contemplation

The two men agreed that this was the decisive question. They disagreed on every aspect of the answer.

---

## Strauss's rejoinder ("Restatement," 1954)

Strauss replied to Kojève in a long essay, "Restatement on Xenophon's *Hiero*," that became the centerpiece of the expanded *On Tyranny*. The argument:

1. **History does not have a direction in the strong Hegelian sense.** The "end of history" thesis is a philosophical-religious prediction, not an observable historical fact. Accepting it requires accepting the Hegelian metaphysics Strauss finds unconvincing.
2. **The universal homogeneous state would be a nightmare.** If history achieves universal mutual recognition, what then? The result would be the "last man" of Nietzschean diagnosis — bored, content, unambitious, no longer capable of philosophical or political greatness. Kojève's utopia is Strauss's dystopia.
3. **Philosophy's detachment from rule is what preserves philosophy.** If philosophy becomes co-extensive with rule, it ceases to be philosophy and becomes ideology. The wise man's abstention is a substantive choice, not a failure of engagement.
4. **The theological-political problem returns.** Without the Greek-philosophical hierarchy between contemplation and action, the theological question of ultimate meaning either collapses into progressive historicism (Kojève's position, by Strauss's lights) or returns in barely-disguised Christian or pseudo-Christian forms.

## Why the debate matters beyond itself

The Strauss–Kojève exchange articulates the two major post-WWII positions on history, wisdom, and political engagement that remain live today:

### The Kojèvian trajectory

- Francis Fukuyama's *The End of History and the Last Man* (1992) is explicitly Kojèvian. Fukuyama took Kojève's thesis that history has ended in universal recognition and applied it to the post-Cold-War liberal-democratic consensus.
- The broader post-1989 triumphalist liberal-democratic frame — that liberal democracy is the final political form, with only diffusion and implementation remaining — is Kojèvian in structure.
- The EU's technocratic-federal project (in which Kojève was personally, institutionally involved) is a concrete Kojèvian artifact.

### The Straussian trajectory

- Strauss's refusal of the end-of-history thesis shapes the Straussian skepticism of post-1989 triumphalism. Many Straussians, especially the more culturally conservative wing, read the post-Cold-War consensus as exactly the last-man outcome Strauss predicted.
- The recovery of classical political philosophy (Plato, Aristotle) as the genuine alternative to historicism is the Straussian program.
- The political-theological dimension that Strauss and Kojève debated — whether history itself has theological structure — returns in [contemporary political theology](../schools/political-theology.md).

## Why this matters for Thiel

[Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s relationship to the Strauss–Kojève debate is unusually rich for a business figure:

### His relationship to Fukuyama's thesis

Thiel has engaged extensively and skeptically with Fukuyama's *End of History* over the last three decades. His diagnosis of technological stagnation is, in part, a claim that Fukuyama's end-of-history was premature — the liberal-democratic consensus has produced stasis, not completion. Thiel's read is, broadly, Straussian: Kojève was wrong, and we are living in the last-man outcome.

### The Christian dimension

Thiel's Girardian Christianity gives him an additional move not available in the Strauss–Kojève frame: the Christian revelation of the [scapegoat mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md) is, for Thiel, the true end of a certain kind of history — not the Kojèvian completion of the recognition-struggle, but the unveiling of sacred violence that makes continued mimetic rivalry self-consciously absurd.

### The corporate-philosophical posture

Thiel's own engagement with political power (PayPal-Mafia Silicon Valley, Palantir's defense work, his political donations, his role in the 2024 American right) puts him in an interesting position regarding Strauss's advice. Strauss would have recommended philosophical withdrawal; Thiel has chosen vigorous engagement. The question of whether this is Straussian *in form* (the philosopher speaking to the philosophers while appearing to speak to the city) or anti-Straussian *in substance* (a Kojèvian choice to unite wisdom with rule) is a useful diagnostic for reading his career.

## Why this matters for Karp

Karp's tradition is neither Straussian nor orthodox Kojèvian, but it sits closer to Kojève in one important sense: [Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md)'s democratic-discursive framework assumes that historical progress toward rational institutions is genuinely possible. It is not the utopian end-of-history thesis, but it is a progress-compatible view that Strauss would have rejected. Karp's career — a CEO actively engaged with the world, using philosophy to inform business and civic positions — is a Kojèvian choice, though mediated by Habermasian rather than Hegelian assumptions.
