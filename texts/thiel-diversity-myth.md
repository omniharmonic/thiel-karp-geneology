# The Diversity Myth (1995)

## A deep dive on Peter Thiel and David O. Sacks's Stanford-years book

**Full title:** *The Diversity Myth: 'Multiculturalism' and the Politics of Intolerance at Stanford*
**Authors:** David O. Sacks and [Peter Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)
**Publisher:** The Independent Institute, Oakland, California
**First edition:** 1995 (hardcover); paperback issued 1996
**Length:** approximately 280 pages
**Genre:** campus-politics critique; book-length polemic organized around Stanford case studies

---

*The Diversity Myth* is the only full-length book [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) wrote before *Zero to One* (2014). It is the clearest documentary record of his Stanford years and the single most direct window into the intellectual posture he carried out of the university into his legal, financial, and technological careers. The book was co-authored with David O. Sacks — Thiel's *Stanford Review* successor, his Stanford Law School classmate, later a co-founder of PayPal and Yammer, founder of Craft Ventures, and a senior figure in the mid-2020s Trump administration's AI and crypto policy apparatus. The Thiel-Sacks partnership is therefore not incidental to the book: the voice of the early Thiel is braided throughout with Sacks's, and the positions the two men took together in 1995 are the positions from which the rest of their careers depart.

Reading the book is essential for understanding how the Bloomian-Straussian vocabulary of Thiel's later philosophical work was first absorbed and operationalized. It is also essential for reading [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md)'s trajectory by contrast: the campus it describes is the same campus Karp inhabited at Stanford Law between 1989 and 1992, but the book records one Stanford sensibility while Karp, in those same years, was incubating the opposite one.

---

## The historical context

The book responds to a specific sequence of events at Stanford between 1986 and 1988 — the "Western Civ controversy" — which shaped a generation of American conservatives and is the hinge event in Thiel's intellectual formation.

Stanford's mandatory Western Culture requirement, an eight-book list heavy on the canonical tradition from the Greeks through the Enlightenment, was the object of sustained student protest from 1986 onward. A January 1987 rally, addressed by Jesse Jackson, produced the chant "Hey hey, ho ho, Western Civ has got to go" — a line that became the book's shorthand for everything it opposes. In 1988 the Faculty Senate voted to replace Western Culture with a new required sequence called Cultures, Ideas, and Values (CIV), which broadened the canon and incorporated non-European texts and perspectives.

The *Stanford Review*, which Thiel had founded in 1987 during his senior year as a conservative student paper, was on the losing side of this fight. The book is, among other things, the post-facto brief the losing side wanted to file. It arrived seven years after the faculty vote because its authors were in law school and then beginning their careers during the intervening period — *The Diversity Myth* was written by men who had watched their undergraduate institution reject their position and who had then spent the Stanford Law years (Thiel 1989–1992, Sacks arriving later) compiling the case studies, interviews, and documentary evidence that the book presents.

The intellectual backdrop is equally specific. [Allan Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md)'s *The Closing of the American Mind* had been published in 1987, Thiel's final undergraduate year, and had become an unlikely bestseller. Bloom's diagnosis — that American higher education had surrendered the Great Books tradition to relativism, and that this surrender left students without resources to ask serious questions — supplied the interpretive frame through which Thiel and Sacks read Stanford's curricular change. They were, in Bloom's sense, the students Bloom was writing for; *The Diversity Myth* is in part the Stanford case study that Bloom's more philosophical book had invited.

For the longer-form account of this milieu see [Stanford as meeting point](../synthesis/stanford-as-meeting-point.md).

## The central argument

The book's thesis is compact and repeated through the case studies: what Stanford called "multiculturalism" in the late 1980s was not, in practice, pluralist. It was a new orthodoxy. The rhetoric of diversity, Thiel and Sacks argue, masked an actual narrowing of intellectual possibility — a narrowing of which viewpoints could be aired, which cases could be argued on their merits, which students could expect due process, and which faculty could expect tenure.

The rhetorical inversion the book keeps performing: the self-described party of openness is the party of closure, and the self-described party of tolerance is the party of enforcement. A university committed to real intellectual pluralism, they argue, would protect dissenting voices, insist on procedural fairness, treat canonical texts as philosophically serious, and decline to substitute sensitivity codes for genuine argument. A Stanford that instead mobilizes diversity rhetoric to marginalize dissenters, foreclose debate, and discipline speech is, by the authors' lights, in breach of its own liberal-academic mission.

This inversion is the rhetorical engine of the book. It recurs across chapters in different registers — as a critique of speech codes, as a critique of adjudicative procedures, as a critique of the CIV reading list, as a critique of faculty hiring — and it is the posture that Thiel carried into his later public writing, where it reappears in different idioms across three decades.

## Structure and the case studies

*The Diversity Myth* is organized around Stanford-specific episodes rather than abstract argument. This is part of its polemical method: each chapter presents a documentary case — names, dates, sequences of events, quoted documents — and then draws the general lesson from it. The case-study chapters cover, among others:

- **The curricular change itself** — the Western Culture to CIV transition, treated as the founding episode. Thiel and Sacks reconstruct the faculty debate and the replacement reading list and argue that specific canonical texts were replaced on ideological rather than pedagogical grounds.
- **Speech codes and the "Grey Tropes" affair** — Stanford's attempts to regulate speech deemed racially harassing, and the procedural questions raised by cases tried under the codes.
- **The Ujamaa House incident** — a Black student residence episode the book reads as a case of anti-racism-policy escalation at the expense of proportionality and due process.
- **Sexual-harassment adjudication** — the most contested chapters of the book, treating campus cases (the "Tony Kaufman" / Crosby-adjacent case studies) through the authors' law-school-trained lens on procedural fairness.
- **The tenure case of Jim Rosse** — the book treats faculty hiring and promotion as sites where ideological screening had, on their account, become consequential.
- **The Stanford Law School environment** — a reflexive chapter, given that both authors had just come through it, engaging the law school's own cultural politics and the legal-academic register of the debates.

The chapters are unusually documentary for a book of this kind. The authors' law-school training is visible in their habit of compiling exhibits — quoted memos, policy documents, student-newspaper reporting — and building the chapter's argument out of the record rather than from first principles. This is a book by law students who had learned to build a case.

## The intellectual register

The book's voice is Bloom-inflected conservatism threaded with libertarian-legal reasoning. Several registers are braided together.

**Bloomian critique of the university.** The book shares *Closing of the American Mind*'s framing: the university has a mission, that mission is the transmission of a serious intellectual tradition and the cultivation of students capable of asking hard questions, and the mission has been betrayed by a relativism that presents itself as pluralism. The substance is [Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md); the method — case-by-case documentary critique rather than philosophical diagnosis — is the authors' own.

**Legalistic in places.** Thiel and Sacks were by 1995 both law-school graduates, and arguments about procedural fairness, due process, speech-code overbreadth, and institutional accountability are central. The book's most durable intellectual move is its insistence on treating the university as an institution whose own declared procedures should be honored — a move that converts the partisan dispute into an institutional-integrity question.

**Occasionally polemical.** The book is often blunt in a way that the mature Thiel is not. The rhetorical mode is closer to the *Stanford Review*'s house style — sharp, ironic, willing to name individual antagonists — than to the more oracular, compressed Thiel of *The Straussian Moment* (2004) or the interview Thiel of the 2010s and 2020s. Several of the book's passages, particularly around sexual-misconduct adjudication and around the treatment of specific student complainants, have aged poorly and have been the subject of sustained subsequent critique.

**Not yet Girardian.** This is the most important structural observation for the vault. The book contains essentially no mimetic-theory vocabulary. [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md)'s name appears glancingly if at all. The diagnostic categories the mature Thiel uses most — mimetic desire, the scapegoat mechanism, the sacrificial crisis, Girardian reading of modernity — are absent. This is the Straussian-libertarian Thiel *before* the Girardian turn became his primary diagnostic frame. Readers who come to the book expecting the voice of "The Straussian Moment" will not find it; they will find instead the voice that made that voice possible.

**[Strauss](../schools/straussianism.md) indirectly, not directly.** Strauss himself is not a governing authority in the book. [Bloom](../people/allan-bloom.md) is the Straussian transmission vector, and it is through Bloom that a Straussian posture — the defense of the philosophical canon, the critique of historicism and relativism, the suspicion of the progressive university — structures the book's frame. The esoteric-reading Strauss of Thiel's later self-presentation is not yet operative here.

**[Austrian-libertarian](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md) substrate.** The Hoover-Institution-adjacent conservatism that was in place in Thiel's Stanford years is visible throughout, particularly in the book's economic and procedural framings. Property rights in one's own reputation, the importance of competitive institutional alternatives, skepticism of centralized administrative authority — these are audible in the legal-institutional arguments the book makes.

## What the book reveals about early Thiel

Several features of the mature Thiel are already present in 1995; several are conspicuously absent; and the pattern of presence-and-absence is itself informative.

**Already present:**

- The Hoover-adjacent conservatism is firmly in place. The book takes for granted a set of free-market, procedural-liberal, skeptical-of-administrative-power commitments that place Thiel inside the broader Reagan-era conservative-libertarian intellectual space.
- The *Stanford Review* network is visible as a philosophical-political project rather than as a student hobby. The book treats the Review's alumni network as a serious intellectual community with ongoing purpose — a framing that would, over the subsequent decade, become the documentary basis for what later observers called the "PayPal Mafia."
- The anti-progressive-orthodoxy framing that characterizes Thiel's 2009 Cato essay "The Education of a Libertarian" and his 2010s public positions is already fully formed.
- Contrarianism as a posture is already operative. The defense of dissenting intellectual positions against campus orthodoxy in 1995 is the structural ancestor of the "secret" in *Zero to One* — the claim that valuable truth is often found where consensus declines to look.

**Conspicuously absent:**

- [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md) and mimetic theory. There is no mimetic vocabulary. The crises the book describes are described as ideological and procedural, not as sacrificial.
- Christianity as an explicit frame. Religious-theological substrate is not a governing register of the book. Thiel's later Christian commitments — read through Girard, oriented by a post-Christian theory of sacred violence — are not yet legible in the writing.
- The apocalyptic register. The stagnation thesis, the eschatological urgency, the sense that civilizational time is running short — these enter Thiel's vocabulary from the late 2000s onward and are not present here.

**The trajectory this implies:** the 1995 book is the Straussian-libertarian Thiel. The Girardian-Christian Thiel is still to come. The period between *The Diversity Myth* (1995) and "The Straussian Moment" (2004) is the period in which Girard's framework becomes Thiel's primary diagnostic layer.

## Relationship to later Thiel

Read retrospectively, several through-lines from *The Diversity Myth* to the mature Thiel corpus become visible:

- **The stagnation thesis.** Thiel's 2011 essay "The End of the Future," which argues that technological and institutional innovation in the West has slowed, is prefigured in the 1995 book's critique of the university as having abandoned serious intellectual ambition. The object of stagnation shifts from the curriculum to the whole civilizational apparatus, but the diagnostic posture — a declinism grounded in the betrayal of inherited seriousness — is already in place.
- **"The Straussian Moment."** The 2004 essay's critique of liberalism as unable to think its own theological preconditions is visible in embryonic form in the 1995 book's argument that the liberal university cannot think its own philosophical preconditions when it abandons the canon. The philosophical ambition widens dramatically in 2004; the underlying suspicion of the secular-liberal self-description is continuous.
- **The *Zero to One* "secret."** The defense of dissenting intellectual positions against campus orthodoxy in 1995 is the structural ancestor of the *Zero to One* (2014) frame in which the founder's job is to see what consensus refuses to see. The move from campus to startup is a change of domain; the contrarian epistemology is the same.

What is missing, as noted above, is the Girardian, Christian, and apocalyptic register. The 1995 Thiel is an anti-orthodoxy liberal-conservative with canonical-humanist commitments. The 2004-and-after Thiel has added three more layers on top.

## The book's reception

The reception in 1995 was mixed and largely predictable along existing ideological lines. Conservative audiences received the book positively and circulated it through the network of right-of-center journals and institutions. Mainstream academic readers largely ignored it. The Independent Institute publication — a libertarian-leaning Oakland publisher — kept it in circulation within a relatively small ecosystem of campus-reform-oriented writing rather than breaking through into the broader national argument that Bloom's *Closing* had briefly occupied.

The book's retrospective reception is more consequential than its original reception. After Thiel's rise as a political and technological figure, particularly from the mid-2010s onward, *The Diversity Myth* began to be read as a primary source for understanding his formation rather than as a freestanding contribution to the campus-politics debate. Journalists and scholars writing about Thiel's politics, the PayPal Mafia's ideology, or the *Stanford Review* network routinely cite it. It has become, in a sense, more read as biographical evidence than as argument.

Later critical readings have focused in particular on the book's treatment of campus sexual-misconduct cases and on its broader framing of campus feminism. The specific case studies and the procedural-skepticism framing have been criticized as inadequate to the substantive questions the cases raised. Thiel has not substantively disowned the book but has also not highlighted it in his later public work. Honest engagement with the book requires acknowledging these passages and the serious critical literature around them; the book is not simply a clean early document of a later Thiel, but a document whose uncomfortable passages are part of what it records.

## Relevance to Karp

[Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) was at Stanford Law from 1989 to 1992, overlapping with Thiel (Thiel JD 1992). His philosophical formation — Haverford undergraduate, then the German Habermasian doctoral trajectory that produced his 2002 Frankfurt dissertation — would have placed him on the opposite side of virtually every argument the book makes. A Habermasian reading of the Stanford speech codes would focus on their distortions of the communicative structure from a different direction than a Bloomian one; a Habermasian reading of the curricular change would take the expansion of the discursive community as a prima facie good rather than as a dilution of canonical seriousness.

The book is therefore a documentary record of the campus *environment* Karp inhabited — the specific incidents, the specific personalities, the specific controversies — but not a record of Karp's own views at the time. Reading it alongside Karp's subsequent intellectual trajectory illuminates how a single campus produced two friends with opposite intellectual loyalties. That the friendship survived the opposition is one of the most consequential contingent facts in the later Thiel-Karp story; Stanford gave them a common referent to argue about, and the book is the most durable written record of one side of that argument.

## The Stanford Review milieu

*The Diversity Myth* is the clearest written artifact of the *Stanford Review* network. That network — founded by Thiel in 1987 and continuing through Sacks's successor editorship — produced, over the subsequent two decades, a disproportionate share of what became a consequential American political-technological cohort:

- David Sacks (co-author; later PayPal, Yammer, Craft Ventures, and mid-2020s federal policy)
- Keith Rabois (Review contributor; later PayPal, LinkedIn, Square, Khosla, Founders Fund)
- Reid Hoffman (Thiel-adjacent though his politics diverged; LinkedIn, Greylock)
- Max Levchin (PayPal, Affirm; adjacent to the Review milieu)
- The broader PayPal Mafia whose network effects connect into Musk-adjacent early payments and into the Thiel-founded Palantir

The book therefore provides the documentary basis for understanding how a small undergraduate political-journalistic network became a significant American political-technological force. It records the intellectual frame in which the network formed, the specific battles through which its members developed a shared sensibility, and the tone of argument the network carried with it into Silicon Valley.

For the vault, this matters because the *Stanford Review* milieu is not simply a biographical detail about Thiel; it is a small network whose internal coherence — political, philosophical, stylistic — had outsized effects on later American life. The book is the closest thing to a founding document of that coherence.

## A caveat on engagement

The book contains passages, particularly around campus sexual-misconduct adjudication, that have been substantively criticized and that warrant serious engagement on their own terms rather than elision. This note has attempted to acknowledge that without pretending to adjudicate the critical literature. Readers using the book as biographical evidence for Thiel's formation should do so without the assumption that citing it as formative amounts to endorsing its specific case-by-case arguments.
