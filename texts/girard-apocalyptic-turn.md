## The Late Apocalyptic Girard

### A deep dive on the Clausewitz turn in Girard's late work

**Primary texts:**
- *I See Satan Fall Like Lightning* (*Je vois Satan tomber comme l'éclair*, 1999)
- *Battling to the End* (*Achever Clausewitz*, 2007, with Benoît Chantre)

Girard's late work — from his 70s into his 80s — took a sharply apocalyptic turn. The earlier [anthropology of sacred violence](girard-violence-and-the-sacred.md) remained intact, but Girard came to think the modern world had entered a distinctive and dangerous phase in which the old containment mechanisms (ritual, prohibition, sacred violence) no longer worked — while humans remained as mimetic, and therefore as susceptible to violence, as ever.

This late Girard is the Girard most directly relevant to [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s own apocalyptic register. Understanding it is part of understanding why Thiel reads modernity as a crisis and not merely as a continuation.

---

## The core late-Girard thesis

The Gospel's unmasking of the scapegoat mechanism — Girard's argument in *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World* (1978) — did not make the world more peaceful. It did something more dangerous: it stripped away the containment mechanism without neutralizing the underlying mimetic force.

The analogy: imagine a dam. Water builds pressure behind it. The dam contains the pressure. Now imagine someone discovers that the dam is built on a lie — the downstream valley is not sacred, the ritual of maintaining the dam is a ruse. The revelation is morally correct but catastrophically untimely: the pressure remains, and now the dam is weakened.

Pre-Gospel culture contained mimetic violence through sacred ritual. The Gospel revealed the ritual as a lie. What comes after the Gospel is either:

1. A genuinely non-violent Christian form of life — the *Kingdom of God* in which mimesis is redirected toward the Christic model rather than rivalrous models. This is Girard's normative ideal.
2. Escalating mimetic violence with no containment — modernity's actual trajectory.

The late Girard thought we were primarily experiencing (2), with only tiny pockets of (1).

## The Clausewitz reading

*Battling to the End* is Girard's book-length engagement with the Prussian military theorist Carl von Clausewitz (1780–1831), author of *On War* (1832). What Girard saw in Clausewitz:

### The "escalation to extremes"

Clausewitz's most prescient concept: once war begins, there is a logical tendency for each side to escalate its commitment and violence in order to overcome the other. Each escalation invites counter-escalation. In the abstract, there is no natural stopping point; the logic of war drives toward absolute violence.

Clausewitz himself qualified this with his famous maxim that "war is the continuation of politics by other means" — meaning that political objectives, not the logic of war, should define when war ends. But Girard reads Clausewitz as more pessimistic than his maxim suggests. The escalation tendency is real and intrinsic; political containment is fragile.

### Mimesis in military terms

Girard reads the Clausewitzian duel as mimetic rivalry in military form. Each side in a conflict becomes the other's double through escalation. The enemy is not fundamentally different from us; the enemy is the one through whom our own mimetic rivalry is expressed. This is [Schmitt](../concepts/friend-enemy-distinction.md)-adjacent but read through anthropology rather than through politics.

### The modern historical case

Girard uses the Napoleonic Wars, the World Wars, the Cold War, and (in more suggestive form) the 21st-century wars on terror as case studies of Clausewitzian escalation without adequate political containment. The global scope of modern conflict, the dissolution of limits (nuclear weapons, unlimited war), and the absence of an authoritative political frame to say *this is when we stop* — all these are, for late Girard, evidence that the escalation-to-extremes is no longer a theoretical possibility but a historical trajectory.

## The theological dimension

Late Girard is more explicitly Christian-apocalyptic than mid-period Girard. The reference points are:

- **The Johannine Apocalypse.** Girard reads the Book of Revelation not as cosmic vengeance but as the unveiling (*apokalypsis*) of what was hidden — the scapegoat mechanism laid bare. The apocalypse is what happens when the mechanism is revealed without being transcended.
- **The Antichrist.** The figure, for Girard, is not a specific cartoon villain but the structural possibility of mimetic rivalry weaponizing Christian vocabulary against Christianity. Modern egalitarian ideologies that scapegoat in the name of anti-scapegoating would, on this reading, be candidates for the Antichrist figure.
- **Satan as the accuser.** Girard etymologically reads *Satan* (the Adversary / the Accuser) as the principle of scapegoating itself. *I See Satan Fall Like Lightning* is the book-length elaboration.

This theological register puts late Girard at some distance from the more strictly anthropological Girardian school. Some readers accept the mimetic theory while declining to follow him into apocalyptic Christianity; Girard himself thought the anthropology was incomplete without the theology, and vice versa.

## Why this matters for Thiel

[Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md)'s apocalyptic sensibility — his recurring theme that modernity has lost the capacity to contain what it has unleashed — is directly late-Girardian. The specific transmissions:

### The rejection of progress-optimism

For late Girard, the story of progressive Western modernity is not what modernity tells itself. Modernity is not an ascent from violence to reason; it is the gradual unveiling of a violence that used to be sacralized and now is simply operational. Thiel's distrust of the progressive-consensus version of modernity is the Girardian version of the Straussian diagnosis of the three waves of modernity.

### The containment thesis

Thiel's framing of civilization as fragile and requiring active defense is not classically conservative (preservation of traditional forms) or reactionary (restoration of a lost order). It is late-Girardian — the containment mechanisms that prevented mimetic escalation from becoming civilizational catastrophe are being lost faster than we are replacing them.

### Christianity as distinctive

Late Girard's insistence that Christianity is unique among world religions — the one that has definitively unmasked sacrificial violence — is Thiel's theological position. This is a philosophically specific claim, not a generic religious affiliation, and it is worth noting how non-standard it is within American Christian public discourse.

### The apocalyptic-register diagnosis

Thiel's occasional invocations of the apocalyptic — that the window for avoiding civilizational catastrophe is closing, that progress has slowed at exactly the moment it needed to accelerate, that the state of emergency is not rhetorical — has late-Girardian shape. Thiel is not predicting the Second Coming; he is saying that modernity's own logic, per Girard via Clausewitz, is generating accelerating pressure that modernity's own frameworks cannot contain.

## Why this matters for Karp

Almost not at all, directly. Karp's tradition does not think in these terms. [Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md) would read late Girard as a form of conservative-theological pessimism whose political consequences are potentially authoritarian.

But there is one parallel worth noting: Karp's own recent writing on American technological and defense capacity has occasionally taken on an urgent, close-to-apocalyptic tone when discussing the stakes of falling behind China and Russia. The registers are different — Karp's urgency is rational-institutional, Girard's is eschatological — but both are responses to a sense that the structures we inherited may not be adequate to the situation we are in.
