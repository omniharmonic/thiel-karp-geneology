# Thiel's Essays — Education of a Libertarian, End of the Future, Mimesis Foreword

*A combined deep dive on three short-form [Thiel](../people/peter-thiel.md) writings from 2009–2011*

Between the 2004 essay "[The Straussian Moment](thiel-straussian-moment.md)" and the 2014 book [*Zero to One*](thiel-zero-to-one.md), Thiel published a cluster of short pieces that do more, per word, to reveal what he thinks he is doing than either of the longer works. Three in particular — "The Education of a Libertarian" (April 2009, *Cato Unbound*), "The End of the Future" (October 2011, *National Review*), and the foreword to *Mimesis and Science* (late 2011, Michigan State University Press) — together constitute the essayistic corpus in which Thiel's political, civilizational, and philosophical commitments are stated in register that is more explicit, and less esoterically shielded, than in his books. None of the three is long enough to merit a standalone deep dive. Read together they form the clearest map we have of the mature Thiel's operating frame. This note treats them as a single unit.

The three essays are not coordinated — they were written for distinct audiences and occasions. But they cohere. The libertarian essay states the political posture: democracy and freedom have come apart, and the work of the free man is exit. The stagnation essay states the civilizational diagnosis: the West stopped building in 1970, and our smartphones are an illusion masking secular decline. The Girardian foreword states the philosophical source code: Thiel's diagnostic framework is [mimetic theory](../concepts/mimetic-theory.md). The rest of Thiel's public thinking — the startup-strategy work of *Zero to One*, the political-theology register of "The Straussian Moment," the Palantirian national-security posture — can be derived from these three short texts more economically than from the books themselves.

## The Education of a Libertarian (2009)

The essay is short — roughly 1,500 words — and was commissioned by *Cato Unbound* for its April 2009 lead-essay slot on libertarianism's future. The title is a deliberate and legible echo of Whittaker Chambers's 1952 memoir *Witness*, whose first chapter is titled "The Story of a Middle-Class Family." Chambers's later writings on his "education" as a Communist and then anti-Communist are the direct reference. Thiel is cueing the reader: this is a testimony, a declaration of ideological position arrived at after a long internal process — not a policy brief.

The essay opens with autobiography, moves to thesis, and ends with program. The autobiographical frame is that Thiel was a libertarian as an undergraduate — founding *The Stanford Review* in 1987, drawing on the [Austrian and Chicago-school](../schools/austrian-libertarianism.md) tradition that was the operating dialect of American conservative student politics in the 1980s — and that in the two decades since, he has continued to think of himself as committed to liberty in the classical sense, but has come to see the received institutional vehicle for that commitment (the American democratic-liberal compromise) as beyond repair.

The thesis is stated in a sentence that has been quoted more than any other Thiel has ever written: "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible." The essay's argument for the claim is crisp and, in the context of American political writing, unusually direct. Thiel identifies two historical shifts, both centered on the first decades of the twentieth century, that together constituted the transition from a republic in which freedom and democracy could co-exist to a democracy in which the two terms have become structurally opposed: the extension of the franchise to women (1920), and the consolidation of the welfare state as a permanent feature of the American political economy. His claim is not that women's suffrage caused the collapse of the free-market order — the essay is careful to note that women's participation in the electorate is not the relevant variable in a normative sense — but that the empirical expansion of the voting franchise combined with the expansion of state redistributive function created a democratic constituency that has structural interests opposed to the preservation of market freedom. In a polity with sufficient redistributive machinery, the median voter's incentive is to use the vote to extract, not to preserve the conditions of production.

The essay's provocations are multiple, and the phrase about women was the one that most of the subsequent commentary seized on. Thiel later clarified that the demographic observation was not meant as a normative judgment about the franchise, but the clarification has not prevented the line from being cited as evidence of anti-democratic, and specifically reactionary, commitments. What is less often noted is that the essay's core logic is not conservative in any ordinary sense. It is a post-libertarian thesis: that the classical-liberal project cannot be rescued through democratic means, because democratic means are the mechanism of its dismantling. Once this is granted, the question becomes not "how do we win the next election" but "where do we go?"

The essay's answer is the three-frontiers program. Thiel identifies three domains in which the libertarian imagination might still find space for the construction of genuinely free institutions, each representing a form of *exit* from the existing political constraint:

- **Cyberspace** — the internet as a domain in which new institutions, new currencies (PayPal is the explicit reference), and new forms of coordination can be built outside the reach of existing states. This is the frontier that has most obviously been realized; the crypto ecosystem of 2010–present is its direct descendant.
- **Outer space** — the commercial exploration and eventual colonization of space. The essay was written in the early years of SpaceX; its thesis has since been operationalized in a way the 2009 reader might not have predicted.
- **Seasteading** — the construction of politically-autonomous floating communities in international waters. Thiel had already been funding the Seasteading Institute (Patri Friedman's project) for several years by the time of the essay. This frontier has been the least realized.

Each of the three frontiers is framed as an *exit* rather than a *voice* project (in the Hirschman vocabulary the essay does not explicitly cite but plainly evokes). The structural claim is that voice-mode politics — reform of democratic institutions from within — has become a losing strategy, and that the work of the free is to construct exit-capable alternatives.

The essay's afterlife has been outsized. Three threads:

- It is the most frequently cited single document in the critical literature on Thiel. Writers who argue that Thiel is anti-democratic in a substantive rather than rhetorical sense cite the essay as evidence. The "freedom and democracy are not compatible" line is the fulcrum of that argument, and Thiel's later walk-back — that he was making a *theoretical* rather than *policy* claim, and that he continues to vote — has not displaced it.
- It is a foundational document for what would become the network-state discourse. Balaji Srinivasan's 2022 *The Network State* reads as a direct extension of the three-frontiers argument, with cyberspace promoted to primary vehicle. Srinivasan cites Thiel explicitly.
- It is one of the most frequently cited upstream documents in the [neoreactionary](../schools/neoreaction.md) corpus. [Yarvin](../people/curtis-yarvin.md) did not need Thiel's permission to conclude that democracy and freedom are antagonistic; his own *Unqualified Reservations* was already in motion by 2009. But Thiel's essay provided respectability and aboveground articulation to a thesis that had, until then, circulated primarily in blog-comment culture. Thiel has never publicly endorsed neoreaction, but the essay is the most plausible upstream text for the neoreactionary public sphere in American tech.

The relevance to [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) is direct and sharp. Karp's philosophical formation is [Habermasian](../people/jurgen-habermas.md): the commitment that democratic legitimation is not opposed to freedom but its institutional realization, and that the work of political theory is to articulate the conditions of non-coercive collective self-determination. Thiel's 2009 thesis is the precise Habermasian antithesis: that the democratic process, once coupled with a redistributive state, becomes a mechanism for the liquidation of the liberal freedoms it was supposed to secure. The argument Karp and Thiel have been having since Stanford Law is compressed into the gap between these two positions. Reading the *Cato Unbound* essay with Karp's later [*Technological Republic*](technological-republic.md) in one hand is the shortest path to that disagreement.

## The End of the Future (2011)

Two and a half years after the libertarian essay, Thiel published "The End of the Future" in the October 3, 2011 issue of *National Review*. The piece runs roughly 3,500 words and is the first mature public statement of what has become the signature Thielian civilizational diagnosis: that the West, and specifically the United States, has experienced a sustained and under-acknowledged slowdown in fundamental technological progress since roughly 1970, in every domain except information technology.

The essay's rhetorical method is to catalog the stagnation. Air travel: commercial aircraft are slower than they were in 1970; the Concorde was decommissioned in 2003 and nothing replaced it; flight times on major routes have not declined in four decades. Ground transport: American rail, subways, and road travel have not meaningfully accelerated since the 1960s. Energy: the promised civilian nuclear buildout of the 1970s did not arrive; per-capita energy consumption has plateaued; fusion remains perpetually thirty years away. Space: we went to the Moon in 1969 and we have not gone back. Pharmaceuticals: cost per new approved drug has risen exponentially; we cure fewer diseases per dollar than we did three decades ago (the Eroom's-law observation that later became central to the progress-studies discussion). Macro-engineering: the American capacity to build dams, bridges, skyscrapers, and public-works infrastructure has markedly declined since its 1960s peak.

Against this catalog Thiel places the single domain of evident progress: information technology. The essay is careful not to deny that the internet and mobile computing have produced real improvements. But it insists that the improvements are narrow and that the civilizational imagination of 1960 — interplanetary travel, abundant fusion energy, supersonic transport, radical life-extension — has been abandoned and then forgotten, with the internet serving as consolation prize. Founders Fund's later motto — "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" — compresses the thesis into a slogan.

The essay appeared in the same year as Tyler Cowen's *The Great Stagnation* (January 2011), and a half-decade before Robert Gordon's *The Rise and Fall of American Growth* (2016). The three texts form an intellectual constellation: Cowen's version is economically cautious and productivity-statistics driven; Gordon's is quantitative-historical and definitive for the mainstream economics reception; Thiel's is the earliest popular statement, the most rhetorically pointed, and the one most legibly infused with a civilizational rather than merely economic diagnosis. The contemporary "progress studies" movement (Jason Crawford and the Roots of Progress, Patrick Collison, the various "build" manifestos of the post-2020 period) traces its lineage through all three texts, but Thiel's essay is the one that functions as the emotional source document — the one that names the loss as a loss.

The philosophical substrate is what distinguishes Thiel's version from Cowen's or Gordon's. For Cowen the stagnation is an economic puzzle; for Gordon it is a demographic and technological-historical one. For Thiel it is a civilizational symptom. The essay operates in a register continuous with "[The Straussian Moment](thiel-straussian-moment.md)": modern liberal-managerial reason has exhausted its generative capacities, and technological stagnation is the material trace of a philosophical exhaustion. The [dialectic of Enlightenment](../concepts/dialectic-of-enlightenment.md) has a Thielian version here — arrived at via Strauss rather than via the Frankfurt School, but structurally analogous. The claim is that the very rationality that produced the mid-century technological miracle has, in maturing into its administrative form, become the thing that prevents its recurrence.

The essay also supplies the conceptual frame that would become the most widely repeated of Thiel's signatures in *Zero to One*: the distinction between *definite* and *indefinite* optimism (and pessimism). A definite optimist believes the future will be better and knows specifically what it will look like; an indefinite optimist believes the future will be better without having any particular account of what better looks like and so invests in diversified index funds and generalist credentials. Thiel's diagnosis is that the United States transitioned from the first mode to the second somewhere in the 1970s, and that the stagnation is a direct consequence: indefinite optimism is structurally incapable of producing the 1960s-style breakthroughs because it cannot commit to the specific futures those breakthroughs would realize. The distinction is sketched in the *National Review* essay and elaborated in the Stanford CS183 lectures that became *Zero to One*; the 2011 essay is where it first appears in public.

The practical corollary is Thiel's investment posture at Founders Fund. The fund's manifesto (written by Bruce Gibney but Thielian in substance) explicitly positions the firm as a corrective to the indefinite-optimist equilibrium: fund breakthrough bets that demand specific futures, not incremental consumer-software plays. The 2011 essay is the public-intellectual version of the Founders Fund manifesto; the manifesto is the investment-committee version of the essay.

The relevance to [Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) is again sharp and again revealing. Karp's [*Technological Republic*](technological-republic.md) (2025, with Nicholas Zamiska) argues for a civilizational re-engagement with technology from a posture nearly opposite to Thiel's. Karp's frame is Habermasian-republican: the West has lost its nerve because it has lost the normative self-understanding that links technological capacity to civic purpose, and the recovery project is a re-articulation of democratic legitimation in a technological register. Thiel's frame is Straussian-diagnostic: the West has lost its nerve because the modernity that produced its technological power has exhausted itself, and the recovery project is the reconstruction of a more-than-modern relation to innovation, whether through Girardian anthropology or through exit-mode alternatives. The two men agree that the West is stagnating and that technology is the register in which that stagnation is most legible; they disagree, philosophically, about what the stagnation is a symptom of. Reading the 2011 Thiel essay and Karp's 2025 book together is the clearest way to see the disagreement.

## Foreword to *Mimesis and Science* (2011)

The third essay is the shortest of the three and the least known. Thiel's foreword to *Mimesis and Science: Empirical Research on Imitation and the Mimetic Theory of Culture and Religion* (edited by Scott Cowdell, Chris Fleming, and Joel Hodge, Michigan State University Press, 2011) runs a few pages. The volume collects essays by Girardians and empirically-minded researchers (neuroscientists, developmental psychologists, anthropologists) on the interface between mimetic theory and the contemporary sciences of imitation. Thiel's foreword is the book's prefatory frame — an introduction of [Girard](../people/rene-girard.md) and of mimetic theory to a readership that may be coming at it from outside the humanities.

The register of the piece is notable. Where the *Cato* essay is programmatic and the *National Review* essay is diagnostic, the *Mimesis and Science* foreword is humble, student-to-teacher. Thiel writes as a former Stanford undergraduate who took Girard's courses and has spent the subsequent two decades realizing their importance. He does not position himself as an innovator within Girardian theory — he positions himself as a disciple and a patron. The tone is consequential: Thiel's public writing in general is declarative and argumentative; the foreword is the rare piece in which he is explicitly deferring.

The foreword makes a set of substantive claims worth cataloging:

- **Girard as anthropologist.** Thiel's first move is to insist that Girard's importance is not reducible to his role as a literary critic, theologian, or philosopher. Girard is, in the strong sense, an anthropologist — someone who made a claim about the origin of human culture that has the structure of a scientific hypothesis. This framing is important: it positions mimetic theory as the kind of thing that is supposed to be empirically confirmed or disconfirmed, which is the volume's project.
- **Mimetic theory and contemporary cultural pathologies.** Thiel gestures, without elaborating, at the diagnostic power of mimetic theory for understanding the distinctive pathologies of late-modern cultures — mass-media saturation, the viral dynamics of reputation and shame, the escalating mimetic rivalries of contemporary political life. The foreword does not name social media explicitly (2011 was early in the Facebook era and before the Twitter-era escalation), but the logic is there in germ.
- **The Gospels' unique status.** Thiel affirms, briefly but without hedging, Girard's claim that the Gospels occupy a singular place in the history of the world as the text that reveals the [scapegoat mechanism](../concepts/scapegoat-mechanism.md) from the side of the victim. This is the first public document in which Thiel plainly aligns himself with Girard's *Christian* reading of mimetic theory, as opposed to the secular-anthropological reading some of Girard's readers prefer.
- **Applicability to business and civic life.** The foreword gestures at the applicability of mimetic theory to domains beyond the literary-theological, but it does not develop the business-strategy application that would become *Zero to One*'s central argument. Thiel is writing for a scholarly Girardian audience, not a startup-founder audience, and the tone is appropriate to that readership. The business-strategy argument exists as an undertone, not as the thesis.

The foreword's role in Thiel's philosophical biography is as the moment of public affiliation. By 2011 Thiel had already been an important private patron of the Girardian research program — he had co-founded Imitatio (the Girardian research foundation) in 2007, and had been funding Girardian institutions and conferences for years. But the foreword is the first public document in which he positions himself not merely as a reader of Girard but as a member of the [Girardian school](../schools/girardian-school.md). The distinction matters: readers are many, members are few, and members assume a responsibility to the tradition's continuation. The foreword and Imitatio together establish Thiel as the primary patron of the twenty-first-century Girardian research program — the role Hamerton-Kelly and the Colloquium on Violence and Religion had institutionally occupied in the previous generation.

The piece is also the first public document that is readable, in retrospect, as the philosophical key to everything else Thiel has been doing. If one did not know that Thiel was a Girardian, the [*Zero to One*](thiel-zero-to-one.md) argument about competition would read as a contrarian business axiom; the *Cato* essay would read as idiosyncratic libertarianism; "[The Straussian Moment](thiel-straussian-moment.md)" would read as a post-9/11 political-theology essay. With the foreword in hand, each of these reads differently: each is a specific application of the Girardian diagnostic frame to a specific domain. The foreword is the philosophical acknowledgment that the other writings presuppose but do not announce.

## How the three essays cohere

The three essays together map the three registers of Thiel's thought — political, civilizational, philosophical — in the voice closest to his own operating assumptions.

1. **The Libertarian essay is the political statement.** Thiel's mature political posture — skepticism of democratic legitimation, orientation toward exit rather than voice, openness to post-democratic institutional forms — is all in the 2009 essay. Everything Thiel has said and done politically in the subsequent fifteen years (the 2016 Republican National Convention speech, the funding of J.D. Vance's Senate campaign, the public alignment with the second Trump administration, the private correspondence with [Yarvin](../people/curtis-yarvin.md) and the neoreactionary right) can be read as variations on the essay's thesis. When Thiel funds an exit-oriented project — a crypto startup, a seasteading initiative, a charter-city proposal, a network-state think tank — he is acting the 2009 essay.

2. **The End of the Future is the civilizational statement.** The technological-stagnation diagnosis is where Thiel's business strategy (build breakthrough companies that create new futures), his cultural criticism (the West has lost its nerve), and his philosophical substrate (modernity has exhausted its generative capacities) converge into a single coherent claim. The essay is the crossover point where the Founders Fund investment thesis, the [*Zero to One*](thiel-zero-to-one.md) argument, and the Straussian diagnosis meet.

3. **The Mimesis foreword is the philosophical acknowledgment.** What the other two essays imply, this one states plainly: Thiel's diagnostic framework is Girardian. Mimetic theory is the underlying anthropology from which the political thesis (exit from democracy is a response to mimetic crisis) and the civilizational thesis (stagnation is a symptom of exhausted mimetic forms of ambition) are both derived.

Together, the three essays are the shortest path to understanding what Thiel thinks he is doing. They are shorter than the books; they are more direct than the interviews; they are less performatively contrarian than the public appearances. Read in order (2009, 2011a, 2011b) they trace a hardening of Thiel's public position from libertarian contrarian to civilizational diagnostician to avowed Girardian — a trajectory the books ([*The Diversity Myth*](thiel-diversity-myth.md), [*Zero to One*](thiel-zero-to-one.md)) bracket without making fully explicit.

The absences are informative. None of the three essays engages [Habermas](../people/jurgen-habermas.md) or the Frankfurt School; none concedes to progressive-democratic normative frames; none hedges the Straussian commitment to [esoteric](../concepts/esoteric-writing.md) reading. These are the *exoteric* statements of positions that in other venues Thiel encodes more carefully. The *Cato* essay states, without defense, that freedom and democracy are incompatible — a claim that in the "Straussian Moment" would be mediated through four layers of textual reference and deniability. The *National Review* essay diagnoses civilizational decline without apology. The foreword affiliates with a theologically Christian reading of Girard without qualification. The essays are Thiel speaking in the mode Strauss called the "gentlemanly" mode — speaking openly to an audience he trusts to understand, rather than carefully to one he distrusts.

The secondary essays one might add to this cluster — "Thinking Too Highly of Higher Education" (2010, *Cato Unbound*) and "Competition is for Losers" (2014, *Wall Street Journal*) — are in a different register. The higher-education piece is the essayistic version of the Thiel Fellowship's rationale; the *WSJ* piece is a popularization of the *Zero to One* monopoly argument and appeared alongside the book's release. Both are less load-bearing than the three treated here. The 2009–2011 triad is the essayistic corpus. Everything else in the Thiel short-form canon is derivative of one of these three arguments.

## How to use this note

Read this note before [*The Straussian Moment*](thiel-straussian-moment.md) deep dive if one wants an accessible entry point into Thiel's thinking; the three essays make the longer Strauss essay legible in a way that a first-time reader of "The Straussian Moment" may not experience. Read this note after [*Zero to One*](thiel-zero-to-one.md) deep dive if one wants to understand where the book's arguments came from in Thiel's prior public thinking. Read this note alongside [Karp's *Technological Republic*](technological-republic.md) deep dive if one wants to see the Thiel–[Karp](../people/alex-karp.md) disagreement in its clearest public form: "The End of the Future" and *Technological Republic* are the two texts that map most directly onto each other.

For genealogy navigation: the libertarian essay is the primary document for the [neoreaction](../schools/neoreaction.md) connection; the stagnation essay is the primary document for the progress-studies connection; the Girardian foreword is the primary document for the [Girardian school](../schools/girardian-school.md) affiliation. Each essay is an entry point to a different sub-region of the genealogy.
