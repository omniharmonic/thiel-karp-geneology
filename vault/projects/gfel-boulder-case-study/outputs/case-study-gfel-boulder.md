cosmo-local coordination; regenerative finance; bioregional organizing; public goods funding</p></td></tr></tbody></table><h2>Context</h2><p>The week before GFEL Boulder, ETH Denver drew roughly thirty thousand people to a mile-high crypto carnival of token launches, hackathons, and side-event hospitality suites. Ninety minutes north, a smaller question was being prepared: what does any of this have to do with the place we are standing on?</p><p>GFEL Boulder existed because a quiet but persistent cohort of people inside the Ethereum ecosystem had grown tired of speaking past their own values. They had wandered through years of crypto cycles, watched the rhetoric of decentralization get absorbed by the same incentive structures it claimed to disrupt, and continued — stubbornly — to believe the underlying primitives could be put in service of mutual aid, solidarity, and communal autonomy. That belief had a name. Ethereum Localism. Not a brand, not a DAO, not a token: a stance.</p><p>The lineage was already there. Michel Bauwens’ framing of cosmo-localism — <em>everything that is heavy should be local and everything that is light should be global and shared</em> — had been circulating in commons-economics conversations for over a decade. Nathan Schneider’s work on platform cooperativism a few blocks away at CU Boulder named the same instinct from a different vector. Gitcoin’s quadratic funding rounds had demonstrated that on-chain coordination could route real resources to public goods. Regen Finance, mutual credit experiments, bioregional organizing networks, the Localism Fund — all of these were already in motion. What had not yet happened, in this configuration, in this place, was a gathering that put the bioregional organizers and the protocol designers in the same room for three days and asked them to design together.</p><p>GFEL had begun in Portland — born, as the announcement put it, in <em>the misty urban landscape of the city of Roses and Bridges</em>. Boulder was the third iteration. The animating question of the week was simple and not at all simple:</p><blockquote><p>How can we weave the strength of global networks with local, community-driven stewardship?</p></blockquote><p>Everything that followed was an attempt to answer that question not with a paper but with a protocol — one designed from the start to be forked.</p><h2>Challenge</h2><p>The systemic tendency the gathering pushed against is by now familiar: technologies that promise coordination tend, in practice, to centralize attention, capture upside, and abstract away the places where the coordination would actually have to happen. GFEL Boulder set out to make something different possible — a venue where ecological organizers, mutual credit practitioners, DAO designers, indigenous land stewards, and protocol engineers could find each other as peers and prototype together.</p><p>That meant integrating dimensions of work that usually live in separate rooms:</p><ul><li><p>Currency design and local resource flows</p></li><li><p>Climate resilience and bioregional regeneration</p></li><li><p>DAO-to-DAO coordination and place-based mutual aid</p></li><li><p>Cultural belonging and the social infrastructure of trust</p></li><li><p>Public goods funding and on-chain participatory budgeting</p></li></ul><p>The deeper challenge was not topical but methodological. Could a gathering’s <em>form</em> — how it priced itself, how it governed itself, how it distributed its surplus — embody the politics it was discussing? Or would the conference, like so many before it, be a stage on which good ideas were performed but never practiced?</p><h2>Strategic Approach</h2><p>The working theory was that GFEL Boulder should be designed as a <em>living experiment</em> rather than a conference about living experiments. Every operational decision — pricing, programming, treasury, surplus distribution — was treated as a small piece of the same protocol the speakers would be discussing. The team called this stance cosmo-localism in practice; the more honest way of saying it is that we tried to make the container match the content.</p><blockquote><p>Can a 150-person gathering, held adjacent to a 30,000-person crypto conference, demonstrate that the way you fund, govern, and account for a convening can itself be the most legible argument for cosmo-local coordination?</p></blockquote><p>We held ourselves to several specific proof points:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Solidarity pricing without verification.</strong> Could a self-attested sliding scale (with pay-it-forward and full scholarships) hold financial integrity without becoming a means-testing apparatus?</p></li><li><p><strong>Two-round peer distribution.</strong> Could surplus be allocated first by the core organizing team and then by <em>every attendee</em> — extending value-recognition across the entire community rather than concentrating it in the producers?</p></li><li><p><strong>Live participatory budgeting at the closing party.</strong> Could $5,000 in matching funds be distributed by attendees to local Boulder non-profits in real time, on-chain, as a piece of the social fabric of the evening?</p></li><li><p><strong>Counter-positioning legitimacy.</strong> Could a small gathering held in the shadow of ETH Denver be perceived not as a side-event but as a different kind of signal entirely — and would crypto-skeptical bioregional organizers actually show up?</p></li><li><p><strong>Forkability.</strong> Could Boulder be executed in a way that made the next node — Berlin, Bangalore, Mexico City, Amsterdam — easier rather than harder to spin up?</p></li></ol><p>The deployment context was The Riverside on Broadway in downtown Boulder, with The Regen Hub anchoring an optional extension week. OpenCivics Labs served as fiscal host, which is the quiet keystone of the entire architecture and the part of the story most easily missed.</p><h2>Process</h2><h3>1. Formation &amp; Alignment</h3><p>The organizing team formed across two cities and several existing networks. The Portland Ethereal Forest crew (Christy, Exeunt, CStreet) carried the GFEL lineage and the website / ticketing toolchain from prior iterations. The Boulder side (Benjamin Life via OpenCivics, Aaron Gabriel and Josh as community liaisons, Will Thomas and Matthew Duffy on arts, Naiya on F&amp;B, Christina on sponsorship) brought local relationships with The Riverside, The Regen Hub, Woven Web, Boulder Commons, One Local, and The Wheel Collective.</p><p>Working groups self-organized around five functions: Programming, Ticketing &amp; Website, Sponsorship, Local Logistics, and Arts &amp; Party. Early agreements set the operating frame: solidarity pricing was non-negotiable, the closing party would include a live on-chain QF round, and surplus would be distributed by Coordinape rather than retained. Working group members were compensated $571 each from the budget — modest, and worth flagging — which made the underlying volunteer ethos explicit rather than disguised.</p><h3>2. Design</h3><p>The design followed three frames working in concert.</p><p>The first was Bauwens’ cosmo-localism: heavy things local, light things global. This shaped programming directly — morning panels were paired in <em>Local Flows</em> and <em>Global Currents</em> halves, alternating between place-based currency, coordination, and resilience on the local side and ecological and societal collapse on the global.</p><p>The second was the OpenCivics framing of <em>forkable protocols</em>. GFEL Boulder was understood from the outset as one node in a mutating network, not a flagship event with satellites. The Initiative Specification governing the project’s planning explicitly named outputs that other cities could lift and adapt.</p><p>The third was the <em>Localist</em> invitation pattern — Boulder community organizers (Boulder Food Rescue, Tinkermill, Climatique, Neighborhood Accelerator Program, Food Not Bombs, One Local) were invited to attend the entire weekend completely free, with lunch included. This was the design’s most quietly important move:</p><blockquote><p>The fastest way to make a gathering about place-based coordination feel like one is to make sure the people already doing place-based coordination can actually be in the room.</p></blockquote><h3>3. Technical Implementation</h3><p>Five pieces of infrastructure carried the operational logic.</p><ul><li><p><strong>BlockLive token-gated ticketing</strong> handled the solidarity-pricing matrix. A standard ticket was $100 USD or 100 DAI on Optimism, with self-attested sliding-scale codes at $75, $50, $25, and $0, plus a pay-it-forward tier in multiples of $100 with each increment funding one scholarship. No proof was ever requested. Scholarships were honored for everyone who applied.</p></li><li><p><strong>A Gnosis Safe on Optimism</strong> (<code>0x5D59881149DA8A0dBEaBEe4E34Df6a59944Dc1b6</code>) served as the gathering’s treasury. Pay-it-forward DAI flowed into it; the Coordinape distributions flowed out of it.</p></li><li><p><strong>A Coordinape circle</strong> (<code>app.coordinape.com/circles/31607/</code>) provided the surplus distribution mechanism. Rather than treating surplus as organizer compensation or as carry-forward into a future event, it was treated as collective recognition of contribution. Two rounds were run. Round 1 was the ~7-person core organizing team peer-evaluating each other. Round 2 — the methodological contribution — gave <em>every attendee</em> GIVE allocations to recognize value-add across the full community. Bartenders, panelists, lightning-talk speakers, the person who facilitated the most generative unconference session, the volunteer who quietly cleaned up after dinner: all eligible. This is, to our knowledge, a novel application of Coordinape at the gathering scale.</p></li><li><p><strong>Gitcoin Allo IRL on Celo</strong> (round 24 on chain 42220) hosted a live participatory budgeting round at the Cosmo.Local closing party. $5,000 in matching funds was distributed to local Boulder non-profits via in-person quadratic voting from attendees on the dance floor.</p></li><li><p><strong>Notion + a planned token-gated Markdown wiki</strong> were intended to host pre-event planning and post-event synthesis. The planning side worked. The retrospective side did not, fully — a documentation debt the case study itself is partly written to retire.</p></li></ul><p>Architectural trade-offs the team made consciously:</p><ul><li><p>Optimism for treasury (cheap, mature) vs. Celo for the live QF round (Allo IRL was the most production-ready in-person QF tooling at the time)</p></li><li><p>Self-attestation over verification (trust as a moral practice; verification as a failure mode of a different politics)</p></li><li><p>Coordinape for surplus rather than retention (legibility of <em>who contributed</em> over institutional accumulation)</p></li></ul><h3>4. Deployment</h3><p>Friday opened with <em>Local Flows: Currency &amp; Funding</em> and rolled into <em>Global Currents: Ecological Collapse</em>. Saturday mirrored the structure with <em>Coordination Networks</em> and <em>Societal Collapse</em>. Each panel ran in the morning; afternoons opened seven physical rooms at The Riverside for participant-proposed unconference sessions. Sunday was given over entirely to unconference, then to <em>Mycelial Field Trips</em> — Niwot Homestead, Yellow Barn Farm with Azuraye and DAR, Boulder Food Rescue, Dharma Gardens, Elk Run.</p><p>Saturday evening was the <strong>Cosmo.Local Party</strong>. Lightning talks from Boulder Food Rescue, Climatique, Neighborhood Accelerator Program, Tinkermill, One Local, and Food Not Bombs alternated with live music from Pinner Bot, Andy and Lara, Justin Holmes &amp; The Immutable String Band, and River Mann. In the middle of all of it, attendees voted on-chain to distribute $5,000 in matching funds to the local non-profits whose work they had just heard described.</p><p>What shifted when the work met reality was, as it always is, partly humbling. The panels ran longer than scheduled. The transitions between panel and unconference were lossier than designed. Some Localists came for one panel and stayed for three days; others came and quietly left, which is information too. The closing party did the most narrative work of the weekend — the experience of voting matching funds toward your neighbors’ organizations while a string band played in the next room turned out to be the clearest pedagogical moment we produced.</p><blockquote><p>Start where you are → Build what you need → Share what you learn.</p></blockquote><p>By Sunday afternoon, eighty-or-so people had moved through a working prototype of the politics they had come to discuss. The protocol had run.</p><h2>Outputs</h2><p><strong>A Coordinape circle and Gnosis Safe treasury that distributed surplus across the network rather than the team.</strong> The two-round Coordinape pattern — core team first, then all attendees — is the most directly forkable methodological artifact GFEL Boulder produced. The treasury Safe on Optimism remains addressable as an on-chain record of how the money moved. Future GFEL nodes inherit this primitive without needing to invent it.</p><p><strong>BlockLive token-gated tickets configured for solidarity pricing.</strong> The full pricing matrix — standard, four-tier sliding scale, pay-it-forward, free Localist invitation — is documentable as a template. The fact that no one was ever asked to prove their need is part of the design, not an oversight.</p><p><strong>A live Allo IRL quadratic funding round on Celo distributing $5,000 to local non-profits.</strong> The round is on-chain (chain 42220, round 24). It is the first time, to the team’s knowledge, that an in-person Allo IRL round was integrated into the social fabric of a closing party rather than staged as a separate event. The pattern is portable.</p><p><strong>A panel + unconference + participatory budgeting hybrid format.</strong> The structural choice to alternate curated morning panels with self-organized afternoon sessions, then to land the entire weekend in a participatory budgeting party, is itself a reusable design. It balances the legibility curation provides with the agency self-organization requires.</p><p><strong>An Initiative Specification.</strong> Filed under OpenCivics’ Consortium model, the spec defined working groups, budget envelopes, and intended outputs in advance. It is the document that allowed a self-organizing team to move ~$8,500 in sponsor and ticket revenue without spinning up a new legal entity.</p><p><strong>Mycelial Field Trips as a programming primitive.</strong> Sunday’s farm and field-trip arc is not a logistics afterthought — it’s a deliberate structural answer to the question of how a digital-first community grounds itself in a place. Other cosmo-local gatherings can fork it cheaply.</p><p><strong>The Localist invitation pattern.</strong> Inviting roughly thirty Boulder community organizers to attend completely free, with lunch included, is the social-design move that most distinguished the weekend’s atmosphere from a typical web3 conference. It cost very little. It changed the room.</p><p><strong>A sustained ~100-person Telegram community.</strong> Kevin Owocki, Christy PDX, Jeff Emmett, Kimberly Frye, Aaron Gabriel, Jon Bo, Monty Merlin (ReFi DAO), Will Szal, Susanna Choe, Spencer Cavanaugh, Anselm Hook, Magenta Ceiba, Kyle Weiss, Sara Johnstone, Justin Holmes, Rowan Yeoman, Eric Wolterstorff, Gregory Landua, and many more continued conversation, collaboration, and mutual referral well beyond the event. A community-led knowledge commons emerged from this self-organization, including the bioregional knowledge graph that Bonfires AI later activated at ETH Boulder 2026.</p><p><strong>Direct downstream artifact: the Schelling Point unconference app (OCL0020).</strong> The pattern of running participatory budgeting <em>during</em> an unconference was carried forward into a self-serve unconference platform with QV / linear / approval voting and on-chain budget distribution, production-deployed at ETH Boulder 2026. GFEL Boulder is the headwater.</p><blockquote><p>We aren’t here building products, we’re building economies. — Zachary Marlow</p></blockquote><h2>Outcomes</h2><h3>The headline numbers</h3><ul><li><p>~80 people attended (cap 150; target 100)</p></li><li><p>$5,000 distributed live, on-chain, via Allo IRL QF to local Boulder non-profits</p></li><li><p>Two Coordinape rounds executed; surplus distributed across both core team and full attendee base</p></li><li><p>100% of scholarship requests honored; zero verification required</p></li><li><p>~$8,500 total operating envelope, with The Riverside donating $1,000 of venue cost</p></li><li><p>~100 people sustained in the post-event Telegram community</p></li></ul><h3>Patterns in participation</h3><p>The most legible pattern was the integration of two cohorts that rarely cross-pollinate at this depth. On one side, web3 designers and protocol practitioners — Coordinape, Citizen Wallet, VoiceDeck, Grassroots Economics, Gitcoin, Public Nouns, ReFi DAO. On the other, Boulder bioregional and mutual-aid organizers — Boulder Food Rescue, Climatique, One Local, Food Not Bombs, Tinkermill. The Localist invitation pattern was the structural reason the latter group was present in any density, and their presence was the structural reason the conversations stayed grounded.</p><h3>How it was perceived</h3><p>Qualitatively, attendees consistently named the weekend as a relief from the broader crypto event circuit — a place where the conversations they cared about were the foreground rather than the side-room. Several spoke of the closing party’s QF round as the moment the weekend’s argument became visible. The pairing of <em>Cameron Murdock’s</em> observation that <em>“one of the best technologies we have is still just knowing each other”</em> with <em>Scott Morris’s</em> harder line that <em>“we don’t need better money, we need better ways of trusting each other”</em> tracked the felt center of the weekend.</p><h3>Where legitimacy held — and where it fractured</h3><p>The case study has only two captured feedback responses out of roughly eighty attendees, which is itself a finding. Both deserve full weight.</p><p><strong>Respondent A</strong> wrote: <em>“I was blown away by the amazing knowledge of the contributors in areas like distributed governance and localized currencies — these were ethereal (forgive the pun) concepts with little grounding in my work until I started seeing how they connect with and amplify grassroots actions.”</em> They defined Ethereum Localism as <em>“coordination and connection, sharing information and resources across seemingly impermeable boundaries and developing global abilities to create meaningful grassroots support.”</em> They asked future iterations to surface a clearer thematic spine to <em>“help gel creative energies toward common cause,”</em> and suggested Pueblo and Taos as future locations.</p><p><strong>Respondent B</strong> loved the meals and the conversations, made many new friends — and left concerned. They felt the ideological terrain leaned heavily in one direction, that organizers offered no visible pushback, and that as an outsider they did not feel it was their place to intervene. Their phrase was that the room felt like <em>“all happy snaps.”</em> They wanted <em>“a lot more space made for constructive criticism”</em> in future iterations and worried what the reception in the room would signal back to sponsors.</p><p>This second response is the most important piece of feedback the case study carries. The gathering’s strong shared-values coherence — the very thing that made Respondent A’s experience generative — produced, for at least one attendee, a chamber that felt unsafe for ideological dissent. This is not a defect to be defended around. It is an open design question. Coherence-of-values gatherings need explicit, named, structurally safe space for friction, or they reproduce in miniature exactly the consensus dynamics they critique elsewhere.</p><h3>Tensions surfaced</h3><p>Several tensions ran underneath the weekend without resolving and probably should not have resolved: the gap between solidarity-pricing values and the modest $571 working-group compensation; the question of who actually used scholarship tiers and who paid forward (no demographic data was collected, which is itself a finding); the unmeasured Round 2 Coordinape participation rate; and the gentle sponsor-reconciliation discrepancy between the planned $4,000 matching pool and the $5,000 distributed at the party.</p><h2>Challenges</h2><p><strong>Documentation debt.</strong></p><ul><li><p><em>Issue:</em> The Initiative Specification promised a live wiki on a token-gated Markdown repository, a retrospective synthesis, and an open-protocol publication. Most of what was written lived in Notion planning pages. Post-event artifacts were thinner than designed.</p></li><li><p><em>Impact:</em> Other cities considering forking the format had to rely on conversation, screenshots, and the Telegram community rather than a clean handoff document. The case study you are reading is part of the resolution — and it arrived more than a year late.</p></li><li><p><em>Implication:</em> A forkable protocol requires a documentation discipline equal to its operational discipline. Future GFEL nodes should treat the retrospective artifact as part of the event, not as a follow-up.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Thin feedback infrastructure.</strong></p><ul><li><p><em>Issue:</em> Only two attendee feedback responses were captured out of roughly eighty.</p></li><li><p><em>Impact:</em> The case study’s qualitative claims rest on a much smaller surface than the gathering itself. Respondent B’s critique, in particular, has to carry more weight than a single voice should be asked to carry.</p></li><li><p><em>Implication:</em> Lightweight, anonymous, structurally-promised feedback collection (during the event, not after) needs to be a first-class output, not an afterthought. Ideally with explicit invitation for dissenting perspectives.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Equity and access without measurement.</strong></p><ul><li><p><em>Issue:</em> Solidarity pricing held — every scholarship was honored, no proof was required — but no demographic data was collected on who actually attended, who used scholarship tiers, or whose participation the format inadvertently shaped.</p></li><li><p><em>Impact:</em> The team can claim financial accessibility but cannot evidence demographic accessibility. This is a real limit on the legitimacy of the equity claim.</p></li><li><p><em>Implication:</em> Future iterations should design opt-in demographic capture that respects privacy while making the equity question answerable.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Comprehension gap for new entrants.</strong></p><ul><li><p><em>Issue:</em> The vocabulary of the weekend — cosmo-local, regenerative finance, mutual credit, quadratic funding, Allo IRL, Gnosis Safe, Coordinape — is dense. Localists who joined for a single panel sometimes encountered a register that assumed prior fluency.</p></li><li><p><em>Impact:</em> Some of the cross-pollination the Localist invitation was designed to produce was muted by jargon load, particularly in technical sessions.</p></li><li><p><em>Implication:</em> Onboarding scaffolding (a glossary card, a five-minute orientation at the start of each panel, plain-language session descriptions) is cheap and would meaningfully widen accessibility.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Space for dissent.</strong></p><ul><li><p><em>Issue:</em> See Respondent B above. The weekend’s strong values coherence created an atmosphere in which ideological pushback felt out of place, particularly for attendees positioned as outsiders to the dominant frame.</p></li><li><p><em>Impact:</em> At least one thoughtful attendee left feeling unable to voice substantive disagreement, and worried about what the absence of friction signaled.</p></li><li><p><em>Implication:</em> Cosmo-local gatherings need explicitly named structural space for friction — a “loyal opposition” panel slot, a facilitated disagreement session, an explicit invitation from organizers to push back. Coherence as an aesthetic is not the same as truth-seeking as a practice.</p></li></ul><h2>Key Insights</h2><p><strong>Solidarity pricing works when self-attestation is treated as a moral practice, not a verification problem.</strong> GFEL Boulder honored every scholarship request without ever asking for proof. Financial integrity held, sponsors covered the gap, and the weekend’s politics were legible in its own pricing page. The administrative cost of trust was zero. The administrative cost of verification would have been substantially more than the discount it would have policed.</p><p><strong>Two-round Coordinape distributes value to the network, not just the team.</strong> The novel move was Round 2 — giving every attendee GIVE allocations to recognize value-add across the entire community. This is a primitive other gatherings can fork directly. It changes who is recognized as a contributor and, downstream, who feels ownership of the next iteration.</p><p><strong>Coherence of values is a chamber that can also exclude.</strong> The same shared-stance that made the weekend feel like home for many attendees made it feel airtight for at least one. Strong-values gatherings need explicit, structurally safe space for dissent, or they will reproduce in miniature the consensus dynamics they critique in larger venues.</p><p><strong>A 150-person gathering held adjacent to a 30,000-person one can carry more signal than the larger event.</strong> Counter-positioning is a legitimate strategy when what you are trying to do is impossible inside the dominant context. GFEL Boulder did not compete with ETH Denver. It did something ETH Denver structurally could not.</p><p><strong>Forkability beats brand.</strong> GFEL Portland → GFEL Boulder → (whispered: Berlin, Bangalore, Mexico City, Amsterdam) only works because no one owns the protocol. The instinct to consolidate around a flagship is the instinct that kills the network. The discipline is to stay forkable.</p><p><strong>The container is the content.</strong> The way you price, govern, treasury, and distribute the surplus of a cosmo-local gathering is not the logistics underneath the politics — it <em>is</em> the politics. Every operational decision is either a small piece of evidence for the argument the speakers are making, or a small piece of evidence against it.</p><h2>Ongoing Inquiries</h2><p>The case study is closed; the inquiries it opens remain active explorations across the OpenCivics network and the broader GFEL community.</p><ul><li><p><strong>Round 2 participation rates.</strong> What percentage of attendees actually used their GIVE allocations in the all-attendees Coordinape round, and what does engagement vs. abstention tell us about the social legibility of peer distribution at gathering scale?</p></li><li><p><strong>Demographic accessibility.</strong> How can solidarity pricing’s financial accessibility be matched by measurable demographic accessibility without converting trust into surveillance?</p></li><li><p><strong>Designed dissent.</strong> What does a structurally safe, organizer-invited dissent slot look like in a coherence-of-values gathering — and who facilitates it?</p></li><li><p><strong>Forking telemetry.</strong> What minimum viable handoff (template, treasury skeleton, budget breakdown, vendor list, lessons doc) would let a Berlin or Bangalore node spin up a GFEL with two months of lead time rather than four?</p></li><li><p><strong>Working-group sustainability.</strong> How does cosmo-local economics fairly compensate the people who hold the operational load of its experiments without converting them into a permanent staff?</p></li></ul><h2>Significance for Open Civic Innovation</h2><p>GFEL Boulder is not best understood as an event. It is a template for cosmo-local civic gatherings that other cities can fork — and the conditions that made it possible are the part of the story most worth carrying forward.</p><p>The keystone is OpenCivics Labs as fiscal host. Without OpenCivics’ legal and fiscal infrastructure underneath it, a self-organizing team distributed across two cities could not have moved roughly $8,500 in sponsor and ticket revenue, executed an on-chain participatory budgeting round on Celo, distributed surplus through a two-round Coordinape on Optimism, and dissolved cleanly into ongoing community without any of that requiring a dedicated entity to be spun up, maintained, and eventually wound down. The Consortium model is what allows initiatives like GFEL Boulder to <em>appear, do their work, and dissolve</em> without organizational drag — and the absence of organizational drag is what makes the protocol forkable in the first place.</p><p>This is the broader thesis the case study exists to demonstrate: OpenCivics infrastructure enables self-organizing initiatives within the Consortium to operate with legitimacy and accountability without requiring each one to become a permanent institution. The same fiscal-host scaffolding that carried GFEL Boulder is now carrying the Schelling Point unconference app downstream of it, the bioregional knowledge graph the community continued building, and the next nodes whispered for Berlin, Bangalore, Mexico City, and Amsterdam.</p><p>For the broader field of open civic innovation, GFEL Boulder offers two reusable contributions: the two-round Coordinape model for community-wide value distribution at gathering scale, and the panel + unconference + participatory budgeting party as a hybrid format that balances curation with self-organization. Both are documented here precisely so they can be lifted by anyone, anywhere, who is trying to make the container match the content.</p><blockquote><p><em>We deserve a world based on consent, solidarity, and mutuality, and we believe web3 technology can serve those ends — if we choose to organize ourselves cosmo-locally and make it so.</em></p></blockquote><p><strong>The most consequential
