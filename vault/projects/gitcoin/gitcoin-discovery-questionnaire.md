<h1>Gitcoin OpenCivics Discovery Questionnaire</h1><h2>rebuild.how Partnership — Scoping Phase</h2><p>Thank you for the conversation on May 20. This questionnaire is the discovery form we committed to sending. Its purpose is to let us scope a partnership responsibly — so the proposal and number we bring you reflect the real shape of the work, not a guess.</p><p>A few notes before you begin:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Partial answers are welcome.</strong> You told us you’re “progressively elaborating” what you’re doing. We’d rather have an honest “still forming” than a polished placeholder. Mark anything that’s in flux.</p></li><li><p><strong>Some questions are deliberately probing.</strong> A few touch live tensions and open questions from the call. That’s intentional — the goal is shared clarity, not a test. We’re trying to surface what we’d otherwise discover three weeks into a contract.</p></li><li><p><strong>This feeds the follow-up call.</strong> Your answers will anchor the longer (≈1 hour) session next week, where we’ll work toward a concrete scope and a number by the end of May.</p></li><li><p>Feel free to answer in whatever format is easiest — inline, a linked doc, voice notes. Different questions are naturally for different people; answer in whatever combination makes sense across Kevin, Mathilda, Ash, and Julia.</p></li></ul><hr><h2>Section 1 — Strategic Vision &amp; the rebuild.how Whole System</h2><p>We want to understand the whole system before scoping a piece of it — the pivot, the movement, and the DAO are interrelated, and a partial view risks scoping the wrong thing.</p><ol><li><p>In your own words: what is the core thesis of the rebuild.how pivot, and what makes <em>now</em> the moment to make it? What happens to Gitcoin if the pivot doesn’t happen?</p></li><li><p>How does rebuild.how connect to Gitcoin’s lineage and ancestry? When you tell the community “this is continuous with who we’ve always been,” what’s the throughline you want them to feel — and where is it genuinely a break from the past?</p></li><li><p>The Learn → Connect → Act journey: for each of the three stages, what already exists today (content, tools, infrastructure, community), and what is still aspirational? Where is the journey strongest and where is it thinnest?</p></li><li><p>There are at least three versions of a “Federated Network brief” in motion — Julia’s draft from this past weekend, the version Owocki posted to the forum, and whatever Mathilda is aligning internally. What is the current canonical version, who owns it, and can OpenCivics see the latest before the follow-up call?</p></li><li><p>Daniel Schmachtenberger’s framing — a <em>federated</em> network, explicitly <em>not</em> a “network of networks” — was named as load-bearing. Concretely, what does “federated” require that “network of networks” wouldn’t? Where do you want coordination/centralization, and where do you want network autonomy?</p></li><li><p>The draft website and sitemap were described as “an artifact of the actions we’re doing.” What set of real-world activities is that site meant to represent? Is the site downstream of the strategy, or is it currently doing some of the strategy’s thinking?</p></li><li><p>Picture rebuild.how 12–24 months from now, in a good outcome. What is observably true that isn’t true today? How many people, how many local chapters, what are they doing?</p></li><li><p>What are the hard constraints we should design within — non-negotiables, board commitments, legal/regulatory limits, brand boundaries, prior public commitments?</p></li></ol><hr><h2>Section 2 — DAO Current State &amp; Transition Mechanics (including the token / GDC)</h2><p>On the call, two framings sat side by side: Kevin said there’s “no cleanup of the past era” needed, and Mathilda said plainly that “it doesn’t really feel valuable to be a DAO right now.” Both can be true — we want to understand each clearly, and where they reconcile, because the DAO is the substrate for the June–July work.</p><ol start="9"><li><p><strong>For Kevin:</strong> When you say no cleanup is needed — what is genuinely settled and complete from the past era? Is there anything that <em>feels</em> unfinished to the community even if it doesn’t require formal action?</p></li><li><p><strong>For Mathilda:</strong> What specifically doesn’t feel valuable about being a DAO right now? Paint the picture of a DAO that <em>would</em> feel valuable for this new era — what would be different in how it works, feels, and decides?</p></li><li><p>What are Gitcoin’s obligations — formal and informal — to current GDC token holders? Are there commitments, expectations, or reputational stakes that any transition must honor?</p></li><li><p>GDC token basics: current supply, distribution across holders, treasury position, and any vesting or lockups we should know about. What is the token’s function today, in practice?</p></li><li><p>The Phase 1 steward mechanic — 5–10 new stewards, 500k GDC delegated to each. Where does that GDC come from (treasury, Kevin’s holdings, other)? How will stewards be identified, vetted, and onboarded — and are they compensated for the role?</p></li><li><p>Does the future shape of this still look like a DAO at all? We’d value your honest reaction to both: (a) the GDC token as <em>seed capital</em> for small cooperative groups, who then co-invest in one another to create an economic flywheel of community co-ops; and (b) dropping the token’s economic role and treating Gitcoin as a network plus a shared knowledge commons — a “cosmo-local light layer.” Which resonates, which doesn’t, and is there a third option you’d put forward?</p></li><li><p>Kevin mentioned the board <em>may</em> elect to wind down the DAO or transfer control to a C-Corp at the end of the year. How real is that possibility, and how should a June–November engagement be designed so it stays coherent under either outcome?</p></li><li><p>Walk us through how DAO governance works today — proposal flow, voting, quorum, who participates. Where does it break down, and what’s the felt experience of a typical proposal right now?</p></li></ol><hr><h2>Section 3 — Movement Building &amp; Behavior Change Theory</h2><p>Patricia’s professional background is large-scale national behavior change and movement building, and OpenCivics has been tracking a recurring pattern across groups asking for this kind of support. What distinguishes it, in your mind, from a community or a set of offerings?</p></li><li><p>Movements involve an explicit behavior shift — <em>from</em> something <em>to</em> something. Has Gitcoin articulated the specific shift(s) rebuild.how is asking people to make? If so, name them. If not, what’s your current intuition?</p></li><li><p>Who is the target participant, really? What do you already feel confident you know about your audience?</p></li><li><p>OpenCivics has observed movement-building as roughly three stages — Coherence (a clear core purpose), Scene / “scenius” (a felt sense of community), and Movement (critical mass) — with real capacity thresholds between them. Where do you think rebuild.how sits today, and what’s the honest gap to the next stage?</p></li><li><p>The theory of change leans heavily on IRL, relational modalities. What’s the intended balance of in-person versus online, and how do you imagine that varying across different kinds of groups (a Boulder chapter vs. a distributed knitting community, to use the call’s examples)?</p></li><li><p>How do you imagine local chapters / cosmo-local groups forming and sustaining themselves?
