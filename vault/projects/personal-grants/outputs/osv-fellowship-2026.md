## Section 1 — About You

### Q1. Tell us a bit about yourself

I'm Benjamin Life. I took that name when I came to understand myself as part of a larger story than the one I had inherited.

I grew up the son of an Episcopal priest, watching institutional religion calcify the most revolutionary teachings ever offered to humanity. I made documentary films about poverty in middle school. I protested the Iraq War before I could drive. I worked on Obama's campaign at eighteen and watched him fill his cabinet with the same banking executives who had just destroyed the economy. I occupied Wall Street. I got disillusioned. I kept going.

In my twenties I built virtual reality films to put policymakers inside other people's suffering, on the theory that empathy at scale would change behavior. At COP23 I placed a VR headset on the Brazilian Minister of Forestry. He took it off after eight minutes, shrugged, and walked away. My theory of change shattered in that moment, and I spent the next year reading Buckminster Fuller, Joanna Macy, Vandana Shiva, and Daniel Schmachtenberger until something else took its place.

What replaced it is what I've been building ever since: the conviction that civilizational change happens not by reforming the dying system or fleeing it, but by *forking* it — patiently building parallel infrastructure that compounds with every person who opts in. Today that work runs through OpenCivics, the Localism Fund, Regen Commons, and the bioregional commons I help steward on Colorado's Front Range. It runs through 30+ Substack essays and a documentary called *In Search of Protopia*. It mostly runs through showing up, week after week, to the slow work of building the connective tissue between organizations, geographies, and paradigms. I am here to name what is dying and to seed what wants to be born.

## Section 2 — Main

### Q6. List 3 of your most exceptional achievements

**1. Co-founded OpenCivics — the connective infrastructure for civic innovation.**

Together with Tim Archer and Patricia Parkinson, I co-founded OpenCivics: a network and open-source platform for the field of civic technology. Most civic tech either centralizes (rebuilding the platforms it set out to disrupt) or decentralizes into tools nobody outside crypto uses. We built the third path — composable open protocols and a shared registry (CivicStack) that lets communities assemble their own civic infrastructure rather than depending on any single platform. The network now spans dozens of organizations from quadratic-funding designers to bioregional governance researchers. It is fiscally sponsored by the Buckminster Fuller Institute, runs an in-house software cooperative (OpenCivics Labs), and is currently in active grant negotiations with Google.org for *Open Council* — an AI early-warning layer for municipal decisions, piloting in Boulder and London, Ontario.

**2. Authored *A Regenerative Accelerationist Manifesto* and gave the field its name.**

I wrote *re/acc* as a coherent third path between effective accelerationism (e/acc) and degrowth — the idea that civilization changes not by slowing capitalism, fixing it, or destroying it, but by *redirecting* its momentum, aikido-style, into parallel regenerative systems whose feedback loops make each opt-out easier for the next person. The framework has been delivered at Funding the Commons under the title *Forking Civilization's Source Code*, has shaped the Ethereum Localism movement, and is the orienting language used by a growing community of bioregional organizers, ReFi builders, and protocol designers who previously had no shared vocabulary. The Substack archive where this thinking lives runs to 30+ essays.

**3. Co-stewarded the Localism Fund and organized Spirit of the Front Range — turning theory into watersheds.**

The Localism Fund applies quadratic funding (Glen Weyl / Vitalik Buterin's mechanism design) to bioregional priorities — letting community members signal directly what they want resourced rather than waiting for a grant committee to decide. Spirit of the Front Range is the bioregional commons I help steward in Colorado: a 501(c)(3) paired with a member-governed DUNA, running a Neighborhood Resiliency Program, food-system apprenticeships, regenerative project allocation rounds, and a Knowledge Commons. Both organizations are explicitly designed to make their stewards redundant — institutional self-negation as design principle. Most theorists who write about commons governance never run one. I have done both — and the Neighborhood Resiliency Program graduates, the funding rounds, and the Stewards Council minutes are all in my calendar.

### Q7. What are you working on?

I build software, funding tools, and shared knowledge systems that let communities run themselves — so neighbors can coordinate on housing, food, and governance without depending on big platforms or distant institutions.

### Q8. Why are you the best person to work on this? What is novel in your approach?

Most people working on civic infrastructure come from one of two backgrounds: technical (and build elegant tools nobody uses because they don't understand communities) or organizing (and depend on platforms they can't control). I come from both, and I've been pulling the same thread my whole life — first through documentary film, then through VR, then through year-long vows of poverty inside food banks, then through systems-change politics with One Nation, then through the network of organizations I steward now. I know what doesn't work because I built it and watched it fail. The COP23 moment with the Brazilian Minister of Forestry is in my bones. I do not have illusions left about empathy alone, or technology alone, or capital alone, fixing this.

**What's novel in the approach:**

*Composability over centralization.* CivicStack doesn't try to be the platform — it's the connective layer between many tools. The Localism Fund doesn't impose priorities — it mathematically amplifies grassroots signal. OPAL, the knowledge-commons substrate, doesn't host data — it gives communities the tools to govern their own. The pattern is the same: build the protocol, then get out of the way.

*Bioregional grounding.* A watershed doesn't care about your political affiliation. The conservative rancher and the progressive permaculturist share an aquifer. Place creates forced solidarity that ideology cannot sever. I am not building tools for "users" — I am building tools for neighbors who already have to coordinate on water.

*Functional pluralism as organizational design.* With Patricia Parkinson, I've articulated the principle that coherence doesn't mean homogeneity. Forests are coherent because they share resources through mycelial networks while occupying different niches. We design organizations the same way — letting forks happen, letting strategies diverge, letting the relationships do the integration work that a hierarchy would otherwise have to enforce.

*Writing as infrastructure.* The 30+ Substack essays aren't a side project. Movements need vocabulary. *re/acc*, *forking civilization's source code*, *the egregore of capital*, *resilience that isn't rooted in place is redundancy in the cloud* — these are tools as real as any line of code. They are what let people working in isolation recognize each other as part of the same thing.

*And honestly: I am cheap to operate.* I have lived in a van. I have driven Lyft. Every dollar that flows through me reaches the work.

### Q11. What might failure look like? What could be some of the original mistakes of your project?

I can name the failure modes precisely because I've watched each of them happen to other people in this field, and I know my own susceptibility to all of them.

**The tool trap.** I build technically elegant infrastructure that nobody uses because I optimized for composability over immediate utility. Communities need things that work today, not architectures that will be beautiful someday. *Antidote: ruthless user testing with non-crypto, non-Bay-Area communities. Ship MVPs before MVPlatforms.*

**The coordination paradox.** The work requires connecting many organizations, geographies, and paradigms. Spend too much time building bridges and not enough time building things, and I become a professional convener with no concrete output. *Antidote: 60/40 split — 60% building, 40% connecting. Quarterly review with a trusted operator who tells me when I've drifted.*

**The funding-shaped life.** Treat this fellowship as a lifeline rather than a launchpad and I'll start optimizing for what funders want to see rather than what communities actually need. *Antidote: build toward sustainability from day one. Localism Fund should fund itself. OpenCivics Labs should sustain through cooperative client work. The fellowship buys the year I need to make those things true — not the year after that.*

**The ideological bubble.** The regen / web3 / civic-tech world is small and self-referential. Build only for people already speaking this language and I've made infrastructure for a subculture, not a civilization. *Antidote: ruthless jargon elimination. Bioregional grounding. Constant engagement with the rancher, the city clerk, the school principal — people who will never read a Substack essay.*

**The solo bottleneck.** If everything depends on me personally, the work is fragile. Real infrastructure outlives its builders; midwives don't deliver themselves. *Antidote: open-source everything. Document obsessively. Distribute ownership through cooperative structures from the start. Spirit of the Front Range is explicitly designed for its stewards to make themselves redundant — apply the same principle to my own role.*

**The writing-as-deliverable trap.** This is the one I worry about most: I have written better than I have built for a long time. The 30+ essays could become the work, instead of the connective tissue around the work. The fellowship year has to end with running tools, funded rounds, and trained communities — not just a richer archive. *Antidote: ship more than I publish for the next twelve months. Treat the writing as the connective layer, not the product.*

### Q13. Describe something that you find beautiful in the world.

There's a practice in Boulder called "ditch day" — the first day each spring when the irrigation ditches that run through the city start flowing again. The ditches were dug over a century ago. They're still governed by water rights that predate Colorado statehood. On the morning the water arrives, neighbors come out to watch. Kids drop sticks in. People who haven't talked all winter stand on bridges together and just look down.

Nobody organizes this. There is no app. No nonprofit. No marketing. It's just what happens when shared infrastructure has been embedded in a place long enough that it becomes part of how people live together.

This is what I think about when I build tools for communities. Not the tool itself — the ditch day that might happen thirty years from now. The moment the infrastructure becomes invisible because it's just what people do together now. I want our software, our funding mechanisms, our knowledge commons to age into that. To stop being notable, and start being water.

What I find beautiful is not the technology. It is the trust that accumulates when communities have tools that actually serve them, kept simple enough to outlive the people who built them. The forest doesn't argue about which species should be planted. It explores what's possible. Resources flow to what thrives. Coherence is in the relationships, not in the homogeneity. That, to me, is the most beautiful pattern in the world — and it's the one I am trying to build for.
